14th amendment ratified: Quick answer and key facts

Short verdict: 28 of the 37 states had ratified the Fourteenth Amendment and the Secretary of State certified its ratification on July 28, 1868.

14th amendment ratified: How the proposal reached the states

Congress proposed the Fourteenth Amendment on June 13, 1866 and sent it to the state legislatures for consideration as part of the postwar constitutional process.

The Library of Congress and other contemporary summaries explain the congressional vote and the decision to submit the amendment to the states as a formal constitutional proposal.

Federal officials certified that 28 of the 37 states had ratified the Fourteenth Amendment on July 28, 1868, and that certificate remains the standard basis for the historical count.

Many of the state-level ratifications happened in the Reconstruction period, when Southern legislatures and Congress negotiated readmission rules that influenced the timing of votes.

How the ratification process worked and the relevant legal rule

The Constitution sets the amendment procedure in Article V, which requires proposal by Congress and ratification by three quarters of the states in effect at the time.

At the time the Fourteenth Amendment circulated, that three quarters threshold meant 28 of the 37 states; when officials recorded 28 favorable ratifications, the federal government moved to certify the amendment.

Contested ratifications, rescissions, and later corrective votes

Contemporary records show that some state ratification acts were disputed or delayed, and historians note that a few states later recorded corrective or symbolic ratifications.

Those later actions are treated by most scholars as acknowledgements or clarifications, and they did not change the Secretary of State's 1868 certification.

Common mistakes and questions readers have

A common question is whether a later symbolic or corrective ratification changes the historical count; the short answer is that it generally does not alter the 1868 certification.

Readers occasionally assume that a rescission recorded by a state automatically undoes a prior ratification, but historians examine legislative journals and federal certification practices when assessing such claims.
