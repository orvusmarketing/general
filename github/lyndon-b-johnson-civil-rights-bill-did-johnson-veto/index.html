Did Johnson veto the civil rights bill?

A neutral, source-first explanation that distinguishes the 1866 and 1964 episodes involving Presidents named Johnson.

Many readers ask a short, practical question: did Johnson veto the civil rights bill? The answer depends on which Johnson is meant. This article gives a concise answer, explains why the question is ambiguous, and points to primary sources so readers can verify the facts for themselves. The discussion is neutral and source-first. It will distinguish the 1866 Reconstruction-era episode involving President Andrew Johnson from the 1964 Civil Rights Act, which President Lyndon B. Johnson signed, and it will show exactly where to find the bill texts and presidential statements in official archives.

Quick answer and what this article will resolve

Short answer, in one sentence: Lyndon B. Johnson did not veto the Civil Rights Act of 1964; he signed H.R. 7152 into law on July 2, 1964, while a different President Johnson, Andrew Johnson, issued a veto of a civil rights bill in 1866 that Congress later overrode National Archives milestone document.

Why this question is ambiguous: the surname Johnson applies to two presidents about a century apart, and casual references or headlines that omit a year or bill number can conflate the Reconstruction-era episode with the 1964 Civil Rights Act.

Short summary: Lyndon B. Johnson and the Civil Rights Act of 1964

What happened in 1964 is direct and well documented: the bill H.R. 7152, commonly called the Civil Rights Act of 1964, was signed by President Lyndon B. Johnson on July 2, 1964 Congress.gov H.R.7152 entry.

Andrew Johnson vetoed the 1866 civil rights bill but Congress overrode that veto; Lyndon B. Johnson did not veto the Civil Rights Act of 1964 and he signed H.R. 7152 on July 2, 1964.

The National Archives maintains a milestone document summarizing the law and its enactment date, which is a straightforward source to cite when noting that the law became effective after the President signed the bill on July 2, 1964 National Archives milestone document.

The LBJ Presidential Library preserves President Johnson's public remarks at the signing; those remarks contextualize his role as the signing President and show public intent and framing at the moment of enactment LBJ Presidential Library remarks upon signing.

Andrew Johnson and the Civil Rights Bill of 1866

In 1866 President Andrew Johnson issued a formal veto of a congressional civil rights bill, and his veto message from March 27, 1866 is preserved in primary documentary collections and online editions of presidential messages Avalon Project veto message of Andrew Johnson.

That veto occurred during the Reconstruction period and reflected the contested politics of the moment; the veto message text itself is the primary source for what Andrew Johnson asserted about the bill and his reasons for rejecting it.

How Congress responded to the 1866 veto

Congress overrode President Andrew Johnson's veto, and congressional records show the override and the enactment of what is now known as the Civil Rights Act of 1866 Library of Congress narrative on the override.

Why readers often confuse the two events

One simple cause of confusion is the shared surname: both episodes involve a President Johnson, but they are separated by roughly a century and involve different statutes and political contexts. Common reporting mistakes and how to avoid them

A frequent error is repeating a statement like 'Johnson vetoed the civil rights bill' without specifying which Johnson or which year, which can invert the historical meaning between signing and vetoing.

To avoid that error, always include a year or a bill identifier such as H.R. 7152 for the 1964 law or H.R. 47 for the 1866 act, and when possible link to the primary record you consulted to support the statement.

How journalists, students, and voters should cite these records

Preferred primary sources are Congress.gov for bill text and action history, the National Archives for milestone documents, the LBJ Presidential Library for signing remarks, and the Avalon Project or Library of Congress for nineteenth century presidential messages.

Short citation examples: cite 'H.R. 7152, Congress.gov' for the 1964 law and 'Andrew Johnson, veto message, March 27, 1866, Avalon Project' for the 1866 veto message, and include the direct link to the primary record when publishing.
