What the Bill of Rights says about religious freedom

The phrase bill of rights freedom of religion refers to protections found in the First Amendment, ratified as part of the Bill of Rights on December 15, 1791. The First Amendment text sets the baseline for modern law by prohibiting Congress from making laws respecting an establishment of religion and protecting the free exercise of religion, as shown in the National Archives transcription of the amendment National Archives First Amendment transcription

Plainly put, the First Amendment contains two related clauses, often called the Establishment Clause and the Free Exercise Clause. The Establishment Clause addresses government action that would establish or favor religion, while the Free Exercise Clause protects individuals and, in some circumstances, organizations from government action that unduly burdens sincere religious practice.

Those two clauses together form the constitutional baseline courts start from when disputes involve religion and government. Over time, courts have developed tests and rules that interpret those clauses in particular fact patterns, but the text adopted in 1791 remains the foundation of analysis National Archives First Amendment transcription

Text and placement in the Constitution

The First Amendment sits at the start of the Bill of Rights and addresses several rights together, including religion, speech, and assembly. Its placement reflects the framers' decision to protect both freedom from government religion and freedom to practice religion.

Two clauses to know

When people ask what freedom of religion includes, it helps to separate the Establishment Clause from the Free Exercise Clause. Each clause answers different legal questions and leads to different kinds of court analysis.

How courts separate establishment and free exercise questions

Courts treat the two clauses as distinct analytic paths. The Establishment Clause asks whether government action endorses, favors, or effectively establishes religion, and it looks at government speech, policy, and funding decisions. The Free Exercise Clause asks whether government action impermissibly burdens an individual's or group's sincere religious exercise.

That distinction matters in practice because a single situation can raise both questions but produce different results under each clause. For example, a law that allows a public school to lead students in prayer raises Establishment concerns about government endorsement of religion; a law that forbids a teacher from praying privately on school property while on duty raises Free Exercise considerations about whether the teacher's private expression is protected.

In many disputes, courts analyze facts to decide which clause is the most relevant starting point, and they may then apply the doctrinal test tied to that clause before moving to any overlapping issues Employment Division v. Smith

Different legal questions each clause answers

The Establishment Clause focuses on whether government action amounts to endorsement, coercion, or favoritism of religion. The Free Exercise Clause focuses on whether government action imposes an unlawful burden on religious belief or conduct.

Why the distinction matters in practice

Keeping the clauses distinct helps judges decide which legal standards and remedies apply. Establishment challenges often ask whether government neutrality has been compromised. Free exercise claims often ask whether the government can justify a burden by reference to a neutral law, a compelling interest, or a specific statutory test.

How courts analyze free exercise claims today

Court analysis of free exercise claims often begins with the question whether the challenged law is neutral and generally applicable. The Supreme Court's decision in Employment Division v. Smith held that neutral, generally applicable laws that incidentally burden religion do not automatically require strict scrutiny, which changed earlier doctrine and narrowed the circumstances in which strict scrutiny applies Employment Division v. Smith

Under strict scrutiny, which courts still use in certain situations, the government must show a compelling interest and that the law is narrowly tailored to achieve that interest. That standard is the highest judicial review and is applied when a law targets religion or when a statutory regime like RFRA requires that test.

The Bill of Rights guarantees both protection from government establishment of religion and protection for the free exercise of religion, but courts balance those protections against neutral laws and public interests and statutory rules like RFRA can alter the test in specific contexts.

In practice, whether a court applies Smith or strict scrutiny depends on statutory context, the forum, and the specific facts. Identifying whether a law is neutral and generally applicable is often the pivotal first step; if a law targets religion or is not generally applied, courts may move to stricter review or other remedies. Neutral, generally applicable laws and Smith

The Smith decision means many routine criminal and regulatory laws survive challenges even when they incidentally burden religious practice, so long as they are neutral and generally applicable. Courts thus examine whether the rule applies across the board or singles out religious practice for different treatment Employment Division v. Smith

When strict scrutiny is used

Strict scrutiny is still the governing test when a law intentionally targets religion, when a statute or federal law requires it, or when a case involves certain constitutional protections that precedent treats as demanding the higher standard. The compelling interest test requires clear justification from the government and narrow tailoring of the law.

Statutory protections: RFRA and its effects

The Religious Freedom Restoration Act is a federal statute enacted to restore the compelling interest test for federal government actions and for certain federal applications. RFRA requires that, when applicable, the government show that a burden on religious exercise furthers a compelling interest and is the least restrictive means of achieving that interest Religious Freedom Restoration Act statutory text

RFRA therefore supplements constitutional analysis in covered contexts by imposing a statutory standard that can be stricter than the constitutional baseline in some situations. However, RFRA's reach depends on the forum: federal RFRA covers federal action, and many states have enacted their own RFRAs with varying scopes, so applicability is not universal.

Because RFRA is statutory, courts interpret its text and limits; in some cases, RFRA has produced exemptions that differ from what courts would provide under the constitutional baseline alone. That is part of why statutory context matters when assessing how freedom of religion claims will be decided.

What RFRA does

At its core, RFRA directs courts to apply the compelling interest test when a covered government action substantially burdens religious exercise. It therefore restores heightened review in many federal contexts where Smith would otherwise govern.

How RFRA changes the test in practice

RFRA can change outcomes by shifting the burden to the government to justify the law under strict scrutiny. Because RFRA is a federal statute rather than a constitutional amendment, its effect varies with the forum and with whether a state has its own parallel statute.

What the Establishment Clause includes and excludes

The Establishment Clause prohibits government from establishing religion or endorsing religious practice in a way the Constitution forbids. That prohibition covers government speech, official policies, and actions that may coerce participation or show favoritism toward religion.

Not every public mention of religion by a government actor raises an Establishment Clause violation. The context, degree of government involvement, and whether the government coerces or appears to endorse religion are central factors courts consider when deciding if the clause has been violated.

Public prayer, schools, and government speech

Public schools and school events are frequent sites of Establishment Clause issues because they involve minors and government-sponsored activities. Courts look closely at whether school practices amount to government-sponsored religious exercise or whether they permit private, voluntary expression that does not coerce participation.

How courts balance religious liberty against public interests

Courts commonly recognize that religious liberty is not absolute and that the government can justify some limits when it advances crucial public interests such as safety, health, or nondiscrimination. Those categories are typical examples of permissible government interests that can justify limiting religious practices under the applicable legal standard DOJ Religious Liberty overview

When balancing, judges identify the governing legal test, then weigh the strength of the government's interest against the severity of the burden on religious exercise. That balancing varies with whether strict scrutiny applies or whether a neutral law under Smith governs the case.

Examples of permissible limits include neutral criminal laws that address public safety, health regulations such as vaccination rules in certain contexts, and nondiscrimination requirements in commercial and employment settings. Courts analyze each case on its facts and the controlling legal standard.

Public safety, health, and nondiscrimination

Public safety and health laws often survive free exercise challenges when they are neutral and generally applicable, because courts recognize the government's compelling interests in protecting the population and preventing harm. Nondiscrimination laws present another common tension point where courts must reconcile religious claims with the rights of others to equal treatment.

How judges weigh competing interests

Judges assess the factual record, examine whether exemptions would undermine the government's interest, and consider narrower alternatives. The outcome often depends on how courts view the compatibility of accommodation with the compelling government interest asserted.

Religious objections in employment and benefits

Cases involving employer-provided benefits, such as insurance coverage issues, may invoke RFRA or statutory accommodation rules. Courts weigh corporate structure, statutory text, and the asserted burden when assessing whether an exemption is warranted.

Religious conduct and criminal law

When religiously motivated conduct conflicts with criminal statutes, courts start by asking whether the law is neutral and generally applicable. Under Smith, many neutral criminal laws will be upheld unless a statute like RFRA alters the test or a court finds discriminatory treatment.

Common misconceptions and frequent legal mistakes

One common misconception is that Smith eliminated all protections for religious practice. That is not accurate; Smith changed the standard for many cases but did not remove all judicial protection. Courts continue to protect sincere religious exercise when other doctrines or statutes apply Employment Division v. Smith

Another frequent mistake is assuming RFRA applies everywhere. Federal RFRA applies to federal government action, and state RFRAs vary by jurisdiction; the presence or absence of a RFRA statute can significantly change the applicable test in a case Religious Freedom Restoration Act statutory text

Readers should avoid equating a favorable ruling in one factual context with a universal right to an exemption. Outcomes depend on the statute, forum, and specific facts of a case, so what worked in one dispute may not apply in another.

Smith does not eliminate all protections

Although Smith explains the treatment of neutral laws, courts still protect religious exercise in other contexts and under different doctrines or statutes, so the legal landscape remains nuanced.

RFRA is not universal

RFRA can restore strict scrutiny in covered federal contexts, but state laws vary and RFRA does not automatically apply to every government action.

How recent rulings affect businesses and employers

Closely held corporations can, in certain circumstances, seek religious exemptions under RFRA as the Hobby Lobby decision illustrates. Court outcomes depend on corporate structure, statutory interpretation, and the specific burden claimed by the business Burwell v. Hobby Lobby

At the same time, workplace nondiscrimination laws and accommodation duties can limit how exemptions operate in practice. Employers and employees should expect courts to balance protecting religious exercise with the rights of others and with statutory nondiscrimination obligations.

For business planning, that means legal outcomes are fact specific. Companies often consult counsel, review statutory text, and consider the jurisdiction's RFRA status when assessing potential claims and accommodations.

Closely held corporations and religious claims

Hobby Lobby is the leading decision on closely held for-profit corporations seeking religious exemptions under federal RFRA, and it shows the interplay between statutory protections and corporate practice.

Workplace nondiscrimination and religious accommodation

Employers must consider both the possibility of religious accommodations and the applicability of nondiscrimination laws; courts resolve conflicts by applying the governing legal standards to the records before them.

State versus federal RFRA and patchwork protection

The federal RFRA applies to federal action, while many states have adopted their own RFRAs with differing language and reach, which creates geographic variation in how religious liberty claims are assessed.

Because states differ, the same factual claim might succeed in one state and fail in another depending on the state statute's wording and how local courts interpret it. That patchwork effect is a common feature of religious liberty litigation and a reason to consult primary statutory texts in the relevant jurisdiction Religious Freedom Restoration Act statutory text

Interaction between state RFRAs and state public accommodation laws also varies. In some jurisdictions, courts must reconcile religious claims with nondiscrimination obligations, producing different outcomes across states.

When state RFRAs apply

State RFRAs apply only where a state has enacted them, and their language determines the scope of protection. Readers should check the operative state text for precise coverage.

Interaction with state public accommodation laws

The balance between religious exemptions and public accommodation statutes depends on how courts read both laws and on state-specific precedent, which means results are jurisdiction dependent.

When reading a case, distinguish the holding, which is the legal rule the court applied to the facts, from dicta, which are helpful statements that are not essential to the decision. That distinction matters because holdings have binding effect in similar cases while dicta may guide but are not controlling.

Practical takeaways and what remains unsettled heading into 2026

Certain points are clear: the First Amendment text provides the constitutional baseline for religious liberty, Smith affects how neutral laws are treated, and RFRA can restore stricter review in covered contexts. Those are stable guideposts for readers trying to understand bill of rights freedom of religion National Archives First Amendment transcription

Open questions remain. Courts continue to work out the scope of exemptions for complex commercial entities, how state and federal RFRAs interact with public accommodation laws, and how lower courts will apply recent Supreme Court signals to new fact patterns.
