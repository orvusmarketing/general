Quick overview: finding your assigned polling site in Florida

Short answer: use the state Voter Precinct Lookup and your county Supervisor of Elections site to confirm your assigned location and local details, and bring an acceptable form of ID when you vote. According to the Division of Elections, the statewide Voter Precinct Lookup lets you find your assigned precinct and the polling location by address, which is the starting point for most voters Voter Precinct Lookup Voter Precinct Lookup (dos.fl.gov).

County election pages add local details such as hours, printable directions, maps, and accessibility or curbside procedures that the state tool may not display. Check both places before you leave home so you know the exact address and any county-level instructions.

Top actions before you go: save or print directions from the lookup tool, confirm opening and closing times, bring an acceptable ID as listed by the state, and have a plan if your name is not on the rolls. These steps reduce the chance of delays at the polling site.

What a polling place lookup shows, Florida polling place lookup

The lookup typically returns the precinct name, a polling site address, and sometimes a brief note about the type of site. Use the exact address the tool gives you when planning travel and parking, because precinct assignments determine where you vote for local contests.

When the state or county record is unclear, call your county Supervisor of Elections for confirmation. The county office can confirm the polling site, hours, and whether the location offers curbside or special accessibility services.

Why confirm both state and county sources

The state tool is centralized and useful for quickly finding your precinct by address, but county Supervisor pages often post printable directions, maps, and local hours. For many counties, local pages also list early voting locations and additional guidance for voters with accessibility needs Miami-Dade County where to vote.

Confirm both sources because counties can publish last-minute changes and local arrival procedures that a statewide lookup may not show. If you rely only on one source, you may miss county-specific instructions that matter on Election Day.

How to use the state Voter Precinct Lookup tool

Start with your full residential address. Enter it into the Division of Elections Voter Precinct Lookup address field and run the search. The result will identify your assigned precinct and list a polling location associated with that precinct according to state records Voter Precinct Lookup.

Step by step: 1) Open the Voter Precinct Lookup page. 2) Type your address exactly as it appears on mail. 3) Review the returned precinct name and polling site address. 4) Save or print the result so you have directions and the site name on Election Day. Step by step: search by address

Use the exact street number, city, and ZIP code. If the initial search returns multiple matches, select the one that matches your voting address. Save a screenshot or print the page to carry with you to the polling site.

What identification and documents to bring when voting in person

Florida publishes an official list of acceptable identification for in-person voting, and voters should check that page before Election Day to confirm their ID qualifies. The state page explains which forms of photo ID are acceptable and notes alternatives in limited circumstances What Identification Must I Provide to Vote?.

Common acceptable IDs include a Florida driver license, a Florida identification card, a U.S. passport, and other photo IDs listed on the state page. If your name differs from the name on your ID because of recent changes, check state guidance for instructions on how to proceed.

Other helpful items to bring: your voter registration card if you have it, a printed copy of directions to the polling site, and any documentation the county has requested for curbside or assisted voting. These items can speed the process, but rely on the state ID list for what is required.

Official acceptable ID list and how to check it

Other helpful documents to have with you

Bring a voter registration card if available and any printed directions from the lookup tool. These supporting items are helpful but are not a substitute for an acceptable form of ID listed by the state.

Accessibility and curbside voting: arranging assistance at your polling place

If your name is not on the rolls: provisional ballots and next steps

If a voter's name does not appear on the rolls at the polling site, Florida procedures allow that person to cast a provisional ballot and then follow up with the county Supervisor of Elections to verify eligibility and whether the ballot will be counted Provisional Ballots.

When you cast a provisional ballot, you should get clear instructions from poll workers about how to follow up. The county office will describe the process for verifying registration and the deadline by which the voter must provide additional documentation if required.

After casting a provisional ballot, contact the county Supervisor of Elections office to confirm receipt of any follow-up documents and to learn when a final determination will be made. Keep any confirmation numbers or written notes provided at the polling place.

When provisional ballots are used

Provisional ballots are used when the voter's eligibility cannot be confirmed at the polling place, such as a registration mismatch or a name that does not appear on the rolls for that precinct. The provisional ballot preserves the voter's right to have eligibility reviewed.

How to follow up with the county after casting a provisional ballot

Contact the county Supervisor of Elections office, provide any requested documentation promptly, and keep a record of your communications. The county will explain the timeline for review and any next steps you must take to support the verification process.
