Quick answer: What the Bill of Rights and the Declaration of Independence say exactly

At-a-glance distinction: legal text versus political declaration

The Bill of Rights is the collective name for the first ten amendments to the U.S. Constitution and contains the written, enforceable protections that courts apply in American law, including freedoms of speech, religion, and protections in criminal procedure, as recorded in the official archival transcription.

The Declaration of Independence, written in 1776, is a political statement about natural rights and consent of the governed; it is historically foundational but it is not an operative constitutional text that creates legal rights enforced by courts.

In short, the Bill of Rights sets out specific constitutional guarantees, while the Declaration sets out the philosophical case for independence and self-government; for the authoritative primary texts, consult the official archival transcriptions linked below.

One-sentence summaries of each document

The Bill of Rights: the first ten amendments to the U.S. Constitution, ratified in 1791, that list specific limits on federal power and protections for individuals and states, with the official transcription available from the National Archives National Archives Bill of Rights transcription.

The Declaration of Independence: the 1776 statement explaining the colonies' reasons for seeking independence, preserved in an official transcription at the National Archives National Archives Declaration transcript.

Clause-by-clause plain-language guide to the ten amendments (the Bill of Rights)

First Amendment: religion, speech, press, assembly, petition

The First Amendment protects several core freedoms: religious exercise and the prohibition on an official national religion, freedom of speech, a free press, the right to assemble, and the right to petition government; read the exact clauses in the National Archives transcript for precise wording National Archives Bill of Rights transcription.

For how courts interpret the First Amendment in areas like public protest or press protections, the Constitution Annotated and other annotated resources explain doctrine and cite key cases for each clause Constitution Annotated First Amendment overview.

Second Amendment: bearing arms

The Second Amendment text protects the right to keep and bear arms and frames that protection in language connected to a well-regulated militia; the exact phrasing is available in the official transcript and is the place to start for any quotation or close reading National Archives Bill of Rights transcription.

Modern legal debates about scope and regulation rely heavily on case law and annotated summaries rather than on standalone readings of the text, so consult the Constitution Annotated for current doctrinal summaries.

Third Amendment: quartering of soldiers

The Third Amendment restricts the quartering of soldiers in private homes during peacetime and is short and precise in its wording; to quote it exactly use the National Archives transcript National Archives Bill of Rights transcription.

Because this clause is rarely litigated today, annotations tend to focus on historical context rather than on extensive modern case law.

Fourth Amendment: search and seizure

The Fourth Amendment protects against unreasonable searches and seizures and sets standards for warrants supported by probable cause; the text in the archival transcript is the source for direct quotes National Archives Bill of Rights transcription.

For how the Fourth Amendment applies in practice, annotated resources connect the text to major Supreme Court decisions and evolving doctrines about privacy and searches by police Constitution Annotated Fourth Amendment analysis.

Fifth Amendment: due process, self-incrimination, double jeopardy

The Fifth Amendment includes several protections: a guarantee of due process, a prohibition on double jeopardy, a protection against compelled self-incrimination, and rules for grand jury indictment in federal felony cases; the exact language can be read in the National Archives transcript National Archives Bill of Rights transcription.

Annotations explain how courts have read terms like due process and how those readings have shaped criminal procedure doctrine over time.

Sixth Amendment: criminal-trial rights

The Sixth Amendment guarantees rights such as a speedy and public trial, an impartial jury, notice of charges, confrontation of witnesses, compulsory process to obtain witnesses, and assistance of counsel; quote the amendment from the official transcript for exact phrasing National Archives Bill of Rights transcription.

How these rights apply in practice can depend on procedural rules and cases, which annotated sources summarize and cross-reference.

Seventh Amendment: jury trial in civil cases

The Seventh Amendment preserves the right to a jury trial in certain civil disputes and restricts courts from reexamining facts tried by a jury; the precise words are in the National Archives transcription National Archives Bill of Rights transcription.

Because the Seventh Amendment addresses civil procedure, its modern significance often depends on statutory law and jurisdictional practice as well as precedent discussed in annotated resources.

Eighth Amendment: cruel and unusual punishment

The Eighth Amendment bars cruel and unusual punishment and excessive fines or bail; its short language is best quoted directly from the archival text National Archives Bill of Rights transcription.

Court decisions about what constitutes cruel and unusual punishment evolve, so annotated sources are important for current doctrine and interpretation.

Ninth Amendment: unenumerated rights

The Ninth Amendment notes that the listing of certain rights does not mean other rights do not exist; the amendment's wording in the transcript clarifies that enumeration is not exhaustive National Archives Bill of Rights transcription.

Interpretation of the Ninth Amendment is often contested, and scholars use annotated materials to trace how courts have treated unenumerated rights.

Tenth Amendment: powers reserved to states and people

The Tenth Amendment reserves to the states or the people powers not delegated to the federal government, and the exact wording is recorded in the National Archives transcript for direct reference National Archives Bill of Rights transcription.

Readers should consult annotated sources to see how federalism doctrines and case law have interpreted the Tenth Amendment's allocation of power between federal and state governments.

How the Declaration of Independence differs from the Bill of Rights

Purpose and legal status

The Declaration of Independence is a 1776 political statement that sets out philosophical principles about natural rights and consent of the governed and is not an operative constitutional law; for the historical text, see the National Archives transcription National Archives Declaration transcript.

By contrast, the Bill of Rights, ratified in 1791, consists of amendments to the Constitution that create enforceable legal protections and limits on government power, as shown in the constitutional text and legislative annotation Constitution Annotated Bill of Rights analysis.

Authorship and historical context

The Declaration was drafted and adopted during the independence movement as a collective assertion of colonial grievances and philosophical justification for separation, while the Bill of Rights emerged later as amendments proposed to protect individual liberties within the new constitutional framework; both originals are preserved in federal archives.

Understanding the different historical contexts helps explain why the Declaration is a political manifesto and the Bill of Rights is the source for many enforceable constitutional claims.

Common misunderstandings and reading pitfalls

Mistaking the Declaration for constitutional law

A common error is to treat the Declaration of Independence as though it were a source of enforceable constitutional rights; it is a political and historical statement, not an operative part of the Constitution, and readers should use the National Archives transcript for the text but not as a legal code National Archives Declaration transcript.

Another pitfall is assuming the plain words of an amendment answer complex modern disputes; courts interpret text through precedent, and annotated sources like the Constitution Annotated make those interpretive paths visible Constitution Annotated.
