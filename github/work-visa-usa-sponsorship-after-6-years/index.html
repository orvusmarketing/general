Quick overview: what happens after six years on H-1B and work visa usa sponsorship

After six years in H-1B status, an employee must either qualify for a statutory extension or else change status or depart the United States; this question is central to work visa usa sponsorship planning and triggers employer steps and documentation needs. According to USCIS guidance, two statutory relief paths commonly apply: AC21 one-year extensions when a PERM or I-140 has been pending for at least 365 days, and multi-year extensions when an I-140 is approved but no immigrant visa number is available USCIS AC21 guidance.

Recapture of time spent outside the United States is a separate mechanism that can add back days or months to the six-year allowance, provided travel is well documented and submitted with extension filings Murthy Law Firm guidance.

Definitions and key terms to know

AC21 refers to provisions in U.S. immigration law that allow limited H-1B extensions tied to employment-based permanent residence filings; it is the statutory framework often invoked for extensions beyond six years USCIS policy manual.

PERM is the Department of Labor program for labor certification that precedes many employment‑based immigrant petitions. An approved or pending PERM is a prerequisite to filing an employer I-140 immigrant petition in many cases DOL PERM information.

I-140 is the employer-filed immigrant petition to USCIS that establishes an employee's priority date. The priority date determines when an immigrant visa number becomes available according to the Department of State Visa Bulletin Visa Bulletin.

Recapture is the practice of adding back time spent abroad while in H-1B status to the six-year maximum, using passport stamps, boarding passes, and I-94 records as proof when requesting extensions USCIS AC21 guidance.

AC21 one-year extensions: eligibility, timing, and how they work

Under AC21, an H-1B beneficiary who has a PERM or I-140 pending for at least 365 days may receive one-year H-1B extensions beyond the six-year cap; this is commonly called the 365 day rule and is set out in USCIS guidance USCIS AC21 guidance, and Temple's guidance is another useful institutional overview Special H-1B Status Beyond Six Years.

To use this relief the employer must file Form I-129 requesting the extension and include evidence of the pending PERM or I-140 that meets the 365 day threshold; accurate filing dates and receipt notices are central to establishing eligibility USCIS policy manual.

Statutory options include AC21 one-year extensions for qualifying pending PERM or I-140 filings and multi-year extensions when an I-140 is approved but no visa number is available; recapture can add time spent abroad back to the six-year limit. If none of these apply, common options include changing status or departing and remaining abroad to reset cap eligibility, and employers must file Form I-129 to request extensions.

The timing is practical as well as legal: employees and employers should confirm that the PERM or I-140 filing date is recorded and that any I-129 extension is filed before the current H-1B authorization expires, because late filings can create gaps in authorized stay or force departures USCIS AC21 guidance.

Because the 365 day rule depends on a running clock of pending DOL and USCIS activity, employers should track PERM progress carefully and retain all receipts and notices for extensions and any audit purposes DOL PERM information.

Approved I-140 but no visa number: multi-year extensions and priority date retrogression

When an employee's I-140 is approved but no immigrant visa number is available due to priority date retrogression, USCIS practice permits multi-year H-1B extensions so long as the I-140 remains valid and a visa number remains unavailable USCIS AC21 guidance.

Visa bulletin retrogression can block adjustment of status even after I-140 approval, creating the need for three-year extension cycles in some cases; monitoring the Visa Bulletin is essential for planning and filing strategy Visa Bulletin.

Employees in this situation should confirm that their employer maintains the approved I-140 and can support extension filings that cite the unavailable visa number; if the priority date later becomes current, adjustment of status may proceed subject to USCIS processing timelines USCIS policy manual.

H-1B recapture: documenting time spent outside the United States

Recapture allows an H-1B beneficiary to add days or months spent physically outside the United States during prior H-1B validity back onto the six-year maximum, reducing time pressure for extensions Murthy Law Firm guidance.

To claim recapture, collect passport pages with entry and exit stamps, boarding passes, and I-94 records that align with dates of absence; these documents are typically submitted with an employer's Form I-129 when requesting additional H-1B time USCIS AC21 guidance.

Consistent recordkeeping matters: undocumented or approximate travel dates are less persuasive than stamped passport pages and carrier records, and employers should work with employees to assemble a chronological travel history before filing Murthy Law Firm guidance.

If no extension or recapture applies: cap reset, status change, and departure options

If neither AC21 relief nor recapture is available, one common route is to depart the United States and remain abroad long enough to reset eligibility for a new cap subject H-1B petition; practitioners commonly advise a stay abroad of about one year to reestablish cap subject status in practice Fragomen guidance.

Another fallback is changing to a different nonimmigrant status for which the employee qualifies, such as O-1 or L-1 if eligible, or a dependent status; these moves require timely planning and, where relevant, employer sponsorship or qualifying evidence USCIS policy manual.

Before departing or changing status, coordinate with the employer and counsel to preserve PERM and I-140 records where possible, since those filings can affect future eligibility for extensions or new petitions DOL PERM information, and use the contact page.

Who files and which forms matter: employer responsibilities and Form I-129

Employers are the filing party for H-1B extensions and must submit Form I-129 to request additional H-1B time; the I-129 is the key vehicle to invoke AC21 or to present recapture evidence during an extension request USCIS policy manual.

Employees should confirm with HR which party holds original receipts and approvals and request copies of PERM and I-140 receipts early; missing employer documentation can complicate AC21 claims and extension filings USCIS AC21 guidance.

Monitoring visa bulletin movement and USCIS processing updates

The Department of State Visa Bulletin is the authoritative monthly source for priority date movement and for identifying retrogression that can block adjustment of status and cause the need for extensions Visa Bulletin.

Because priority date availability can change month to month, employees and employers should check the Visa Bulletin regularly and coordinate filings around predictable movement when possible, while recognizing unpredictability in some categories USCIS policy manual.

USCIS processing times and receipt notices also matter for AC21 claims because the 365 day rule depends on documented filing dates; keep copies of all USCIS receipts and monitor posted processing times for context on likely adjudication windows USCIS AC21 guidance.

Practical timeline and checklist for employees approaching year six

Six to twelve months before the six-year limit, verify PERM and I-140 filing dates to check whether the 365 day rule applies or whether I-140 approval is likely to trigger multi-year extension eligibility USCIS policy manual.

Collect travel records early: passport pages with entry and exit stamps, airline boarding passes, and any employer records of business travel should be organized chronologically for recapture claims Murthy Law Firm guidance.

Set internal deadlines for I-129 filings tied to current H-1B expiry dates, and confirm with HR and counsel who will file and when; late coordination is one of the most common operational causes of complications Fragomen guidance.

Maintain a small, dated file of receipts, approval notices, and travel evidence that can be copied into an I-129 package quickly if the employer needs to file an extension under AC21 or to present recapture evidence USCIS AC21 guidance.

Common mistakes and documentation pitfalls to avoid

Failing to organize travel evidence is a frequent problem; passport pages without clear entry or exit stamps, missing boarding passes, or inconsistent dates can weaken recapture claims and delay adjudication Murthy Law Firm guidance.

Another common error is misunderstanding which filings meet the 365 day rule; relying on planning assumptions rather than confirmed PERM or I-140 receipt dates can leave an employee without a timely extension option USCIS policy manual.

Missed filing windows for Form I-129 or filing with incomplete supporting evidence can create status gaps that force departure; remedial steps include assembling missing documents and, if needed, seeking counsel quickly to explore alternative status options Fragomen guidance.

Corrective actions often start with a simple clean file of passport scans, stamped I-94s, and copies of PERM and I-140 receipts so that employers can refile with complete evidence when warranted DOL PERM information.

Employer considerations and coordination best practices

HR teams should assign clear ownership for H-1B extension workflows, including who will maintain PERM and I-140 records, who will coordinate with counsel, and who will communicate filing timelines to the employee Fragomen guidance.

Employers should plan for timely I-129 filings and retain copies of all receipts and approval notices; when relying on AC21 relief, a documented record of the PERM and I-140 history is essential for successful extensions DOL PERM information.

Regular check-ins between HR, the employee, and immigration counsel help reduce surprises from visa bulletin retrogression or processing delays, and ensure that necessary evidence is gathered well ahead of filing deadlines USCIS policy manual.

When to consult immigration counsel: red flags and complex cases

Consult counsel if you find missing PERM or I-140 receipts, unclear travel records, or if priority date retrogression is likely to affect your adjustment timing; these are common red flags that benefit from legal review USCIS policy manual.

Bring passport pages, I-94s, PERM and I-140 receipts, I-129 filings, and any employer correspondence to counsel so they can assess eligibility for AC21 relief, recapture claims, or alternative strategies DOL PERM information.
