What an FEC filing is and why it matters

The role of the Federal Election Commission

An FEC filing is a public disclosure document created under rules the Federal Election Commission administers and enforces to make federal campaign finance activity transparent to the public and to regulators. The FEC describes its reporting and filing help pages as the primary guidance for how committees disclose receipts and disbursements Reporting and Filing - Help for Candidates and Committees.

These filings give voters, journalists, and researchers a window into fundraising, spending, and where money flows during a federal campaign. Filings also provide the starting point for any compliance review or enforcement action, because they are the official public record maintained by the agency.

What counts as an FEC filing

In practice, an FEC filing includes summary totals and detailed schedules that break down itemized contributions and disbursements, so a single public filing can contain both an overview and line-level data. OpenSecrets provides user-facing guidance on reading these reports while the FEC maintains the raw data and reporting templates How to Read Federal Campaign Finance Reports.

Common labeled schedules include itemized contributions and itemized disbursements, often referred to as Schedule A and Schedule B. These schedules let readers see individual donor entries and specific expenditures rather than only aggregated totals, which improves transparency and traceability.

When a candidate committee must register with the FEC

The statutory threshold that triggers registration

A committee generally must register with the FEC once it has received or spent the statutory threshold commonly identified as $5,000; that trigger starts formal registration and reporting duties according to the FEC campaign guide and registration guidance Campaign Guide for Congressional Candidates and Committees.

That threshold is a common operational rule used by many campaigns and watchdogs, but campaign staff should check primary FEC pages for any updates or special rules that may apply in particular circumstances. Registration creates the legal obligation to begin filing the recurring reports required of candidate committees.

Timing and initial registration steps

Initial steps typically include filing a Statement of Candidacy and a Statement of Organization for the principal campaign committee, and verifying committee details with the FEC. The campaign guide lays out the forms and basic sequence of registration steps needed once the threshold is met Campaign Guide for Congressional Candidates and Committees.

After registering, committees must set up systems for recordkeeping, decide on electronic filing methods where required, and calendar recurring report deadlines so that filings remain timely and accurate.

Common types of committees the FEC tracks

Authorized candidate committees and principal campaign committees

The FEC describes several committee types with distinct registration and reporting rules, including principal campaign committees that are directly authorized by a candidate. The FEC pages on committee types explain these distinctions and how they affect reporting obligations Types of Political Committees.

Principal campaign committees report candidate fundraising and expenses and generally follow a reporting cycle tied to the candidate's election calendar.

An FEC filing is a public disclosure that records a committee's receipts and disbursements under rules administered by the Federal Election Commission; it matters because it provides transparency, enables public oversight, and serves as the basis for enforcement and reporting.

PACs, party committees, and leadership PACs

Other committee categories include connected and nonconnected political action committees, party committees, and leadership PACs; each category has unique rules for registration, contribution limits, and reporting frequency as the FEC explains Types of Political Committees.

Knowing the committee type is essential when interpreting reports, because items such as transfer activity, attributed contributions, and reporting frequency can differ across committee types.

Major FEC report types and filing deadlines

Quarterly, monthly, and special pre-election reports

Common report categories include periodic quarterly reports, pre- and post-election reports, monthly filings for certain entities, and special 12-day pre-election reports required in the run-up to many federal elections; the FEC publishes a reporting-deadlines calendar with exact due dates Reporting Deadlines - Campaign Finance Data.

Some committees must file additional reports tied to specific events or thresholds, so relying on the agency calendar helps campaigns and observers plan and avoid missed deadlines.

Where to find the FEC reporting-deadlines calendar

The FEC reporting-deadlines calendar is the official source for due dates and is regularly updated to reflect election cycles and specific filing rules. Observers and campaign staff should check that calendar before finalizing submissions to ensure they meet the correct deadline for their committee type and election context Reporting Deadlines - Campaign Finance Data. For calendar updates and related notices see Reports due in 2026.

Timely filing is a basic compliance requirement; using the calendar and setting internal reminders reduces the risk of late submissions and related enforcement attention.

How to read a public FEC report

Summary totals versus detailed schedules

Public filings typically begin with summary totals that provide a snapshot of cash on hand, total receipts, and total disbursements, followed by detailed schedules that list itemized transactions for those who want line-level detail. OpenSecrets offers practical guidance for readers interpreting those sections alongside the FEC raw files How to Read Federal Campaign Finance Reports.

Scan the summary to get a high-level view, then open Schedule A and Schedule B for itemized contributions and disbursements to verify dates, amounts, and payees or donors.

Key schedules to scan: Schedule A and Schedule B

Schedule A lists itemized contributions, including contributor names and amounts when required, while Schedule B lists itemized disbursements with payee names and purposes. Reviewing both schedules helps spot large donors, vendor relationships, and timing patterns in campaign activity.

When reading these schedules, compare the line-level entries to the summary totals to confirm that aggregates and itemized lists reconcile, and consult OpenSecrets for examples on interpreting common entries.

Electronic filing and public access to data

Who must file electronically

Many larger committees are required to file electronically, which aids public access and searchability; the FEC's reporting help pages outline who must file electronically and the formats the agency accepts Reporting and Filing - Help for Candidates and Committees.

Electronic submission speeds public availability and lets databases provide searchable fields, filters, and exports that benefit journalists, researchers, and voters checking committee activity.

How electronic filing improves searchability and public use

When filings are submitted electronically, they can be indexed and displayed with sortable tables and downloadable formats that make it easier to track changes over time and cross-check entries against other public records. Open data formats also allow watchdog groups and platforms to create explanatory tools and visualizations How to Read Federal Campaign Finance Reports.

Readers should remember that filing formats and submission interfaces can change, so consulting current FEC guidance is the best way to understand the latest electronic filing processes.

Step-by-step: registering and filing as a candidate committee

Initial registration and C/ PAC creation steps

Start by tracking whether the committee has reached the registration trigger. Once the common threshold is reached, the campaign should file a Statement of Candidacy and a Statement of Organization and follow the form instructions in the campaign guide Campaign Guide for Congressional Candidates and Committees.

These initial filings establish the principal campaign committee and make the committee visible in FEC systems where future reports and schedules will be posted.

Ongoing reporting cycle and practical tips

After registration, set an internal filing calendar that mirrors the FEC reporting-deadlines calendar and keep meticulous records of receipts and disbursements for each reporting period. The FEC reporting calendar helps campaigns align their internal schedules with official due dates Reporting Deadlines - Campaign Finance Data.

Practical tips include designating a treasurer responsible for filing, keeping digital backups of source documents, and correcting disclosures quickly when errors are found to reduce compliance exposure.

Compliance risks, enforcement, and remediation

Consequences of late or incomplete filings

Failing to file on time or to correct required disclosure can lead to civil penalties, administrative enforcement, and public referral actions; the FEC enforcement materials describe potential outcomes and procedures for settlement and enforcement reviews Reporting and Filing - Help for Candidates and Committees.

Consequences vary by case and may include fines or negotiated settlements, so timely correction and voluntary disclosure of errors are practical risk-reduction steps for committees facing reporting problems.

How enforcement and settlements are documented

Enforcement actions and settlement documents are part of the public record; CRS analyses and the FEC's own enforcement pages provide context on how cases are processed and resolved Federal Campaign Finance: Overview and Recent Developments.

Readers reviewing enforcement materials should interpret outcomes in context, because settlements may reflect negotiated resolutions rather than admissions of deliberate misconduct.

Common mistakes and how to avoid them

Late filings and missed deadlines

Late filings are among the most common compliance issues and can trigger fines and increased scrutiny; using the FEC reporting-deadlines calendar and internal reminders reduces this risk Reporting Deadlines - Campaign Finance Data.

Set clear internal responsibilities for preparing and reviewing reports well before due dates to catch reconciliation errors and incomplete schedules.

Incomplete schedules and poor recordkeeping

Incomplete itemization, missing documentation, and failure to file electronically when required are frequent problems. Maintain clear contributor and vendor records and reconcile bank statements to reported entries to prevent these errors Reporting and Filing - Help for Candidates and Committees.

When mistakes occur, file an amended report promptly and keep a record of the correction steps taken to demonstrate good faith and mitigate penalties.

Practical scenarios and examples

A small candidate committee registering after raising the threshold

Imagine a committee that collects small donations and reaches the common $5,000 threshold. The campaign guide explains that reaching that trigger requires filing the Statement of Candidacy and registration forms and then beginning the regular reporting cycle Campaign Guide for Congressional Candidates and Committees.

From there, the committee should set up recordkeeping, designate a treasurer, and plan each report around the FEC calendar so that quarterly and special pre-election reports are filed on time.

Reading an example Schedule A and Schedule B

To read a Schedule A entry, look for the contributor name, date, amount, and any required identifying details; for Schedule B, check the payee, date, amount, and stated purpose. OpenSecrets' how-to materials show example entries that clarify common fields and abbreviations How to Read Federal Campaign Finance Reports.

Practically, compare a few line items to the summary totals to ensure amounts aggregate correctly and note any unusually large transactions for follow-up or reporting context.

How to check FEC records for a candidate or committee

Searching the FEC database

Search by committee name or committee ID in the FEC database to locate filings, then open the specific report page to view summary totals and schedule attachments. The FEC site provides search tools and filters that help narrow results to the relevant election cycle or report type Reporting and Filing - Help for Candidates and Committees.

When searching, use exact committee names, the candidate's name, or the committee ID to avoid unrelated results and verify that the filing dates match the period you are researching.

Cross-checking with watchdog sites and primary filings

Watchdog platforms like OpenSecrets provide summaries, visualizations, and context that can aid interpretation, but rely on the FEC filings as the primary source for exact dates and amounts How to Read Federal Campaign Finance Reports.

For reporting or research, cite the FEC filing page and attach the specific report or schedule to support any claims about fundraising totals or particular transactions.
