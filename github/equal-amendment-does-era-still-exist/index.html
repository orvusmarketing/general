What the equal amendment is and why its status is debated

The equal amendment is the proposed constitutional amendment commonly called the Equal Rights Amendment, first offered by Congress in 1972 with a ratification framework that included a congressional deadline. The proposing resolution and its deadline are central to later legal disputes about whether subsequent state approvals can complete ratification National Archives ERA timeline

Supporters count 38 state ratifications and the DOJ OLC issued an opinion supporting certification, but because of unresolved issues about rescissions and the congressional deadline, the amendment's nationwide constitutional effect remains legally disputed in 2026.

By the original deadline set in the proposing resolution, the amendment had 35 state ratifications and therefore did not reach the three fourths threshold then required for adoption. That shortfall is a core factual reason the amendment's status has been contested in courts and policy debates Congressional Research Service background

The dispute that remains is mainly procedural: whether the deadline included in the proposing resolution still controls, and whether some states that acted earlier may lawfully rescind their ratifications. Those procedural questions, more than a single tally of state approvals, drive the legal uncertainty that persists Brennan Center explainer

How the equal amendment ratification count changed after 1982

After 1982 three states later adopted measures that supporters count toward the required total. Nevada voted to ratify in 2017, Illinois in 2018, and Virginia in 2020; advocates add those approvals to earlier ratifications to reach what they say is the 38-state threshold New York Times reporting (analysis)

National Archives continues to maintain a public timeline of state actions and related documentation that researchers use to verify the sequence of approvals and actions. That timeline is a central public record for tracking which states acted and when National Archives ERA timeline

Counting ratifications is a factual exercise in compiling state actions, but counting alone does not answer the legal questions about effect. As described by legal analysts, the legal effect depends on resolving procedural issues such as the original deadline and whether rescissions are valid, so the numerical claim to 38 ratifications is only one piece of the legal picture Brennan Center explainer

The DOJ opinion and the National Archives role in certification

On January 6, 2020 the Justice Department's Office of Legal Counsel issued a formal opinion concluding that the ratification record supports certification and that the Archivist has authority to publish the amendment. The OLC framed that view as an executive-branch legal opinion about the existing record DOJ Office of Legal Counsel opinion

NARA is the official repository that maintains the public timeline of state actions and documents related certification steps. Researchers and reporters rely on NARA's materials as the primary public record for state ratification activity and administrative actions National Archives ERA timeline (NARA statement)

Legal analysts note that opponents continue to press procedural arguments in courts, leaving full nationwide effect unresolved without further action Brennan Center explainer

Key legal disputes: rescissions, deadlines, and court questions

Some states that ratified the amendment earlier later adopted measures attempting to rescind those ratifications. Whether a state can withdraw a prior ratification is a contested legal question that courts have not finally settled in a way that resolves the nationwide dispute Brennan Center explainer

Congress's proposing resolution included a ratification deadline, and the legal status of that deadline is a central issue in litigation. Courts and scholars debate whether Congress can impose or extend deadlines for state ratification and what effect, if any, a missed deadline has on later state actions National Archives ERA timeline

Because the core issues are procedural, courts remain a primary route to a binding, nationwide resolution. Lower courts, appeals courts and potentially the Supreme Court could address whether rescissions are valid or whether the deadline bars later ratifications, and their rulings would determine practical legal effect National Constitution Center analysis

Practical paths to resolution for the equal amendment

One clear path to a definitive national result would be congressional action such as a joint resolution or statute that removes or clarifies the original ratification deadline. Congress has the power to propose and clarify procedural terms, and legal analysts note that action of that kind would change the amendment's legal posture by removing the deadline question from courts Brennan Center explainer (see H.J.Res.80 and how a bill becomes law)

The other major route is a definitive judicial ruling. Courts could rule on the validity of rescissions and on whether the deadline bars later ratifications, with a final appeal potentially reaching the Supreme Court. A clear judicial decision would resolve nationwide enforceability questions for the amendment National Constitution Center analysis

Until Congress or the courts act, many legal analysts and some advocates treat the ratification claim as unresolved in practice despite the OLC opinion and the state ratifications counted by supporters. That conditional status is why news coverage and legal commentary often describe the ERA's effect as disputed rather than settled DOJ Office of Legal Counsel opinion

What the uncertainty means in practice for laws and courts

In litigation and administrative practice the disputed status can produce uneven treatment. Some agencies or advocates may cite the OLC opinion or the state ratification count in arguments, while others highlight the deadline and rescissions to challenge enforceability. This leads to differing approaches across legal filings and administrative decisions DOJ Office of Legal Counsel opinion

Lower courts may consider the OLC view, but an executive opinion does not bind courts in all contexts, and judges decide based on legal briefs, precedent and statutory text. As a result, depending on the case and the court, the amendment may be treated differently until a binding resolution is issued Brennan Center explainer

A definitive, nationwide change in how courts and agencies treat the amendment would most likely follow a congressional clarification or a final judicial ruling. Until that happens, individuals and organizations citing the amendment should note the procedural disputes that limit an uncontested, universal legal effect National Constitution Center analysis

Common reporting mistakes and how to read primary sources

A frequent reporting error is treating a list of state actions alone as a conclusive statement that the amendment is adopted, without noting the deadline and rescission debates that affect legal effect. That omission can mislead readers about why disputes continue National Archives ERA timeline

Another mistake is to conflate an executive-branch legal opinion with a final judicial decision. The OLC opinion states a legal view that supports certification on the record, but it does not itself resolve challenges about rescissions or the deadline in a court of law DOJ Office of Legal Counsel opinion

Steps to verify ratification claims

Check dates and legal pleadings

When reading NARA entries, check the date of each state action, any state legislative language, and whether NARA notes administrative steps such as certification.
