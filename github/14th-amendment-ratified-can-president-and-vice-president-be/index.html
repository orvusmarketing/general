Short answer: what the Constitution allows and what usually happens in practice

One-sentence legal summary

The Constitution does not say that a president and vice president cannot be inhabitants of the same State; rather, the Twelfth Amendment forbids an elector from casting both of the elector's votes for persons who are inhabitants of that elector's State, as explained in the founding documents held by the National Archives U.S. National Archives.

That textual rule means same-state tickets are constitutionally possible, but the rule creates a practical electoral effect that campaigns must manage to avoid losing a state's electors in practice, a point discussed in Electoral College guidance U.S. National Archives Electoral College FAQ.

Practical takeaway for campaigns and voters

In short, a ticket with both principal nominees inhabiting the same State can run legally, yet electors of that State cannot legally give both of their votes to those two residents, which may cost the ticket the full slate of electors from that State U.S. National Archives Electoral College FAQ.

Because of that risk, political parties and campaigns normally avoid matching a nominee and running mate who claim the same legal residence, or they arrange for one candidate to change domicile before ballots are finalized, practices examined in historical summaries and reporting Ballotpedia and a local explainer Michael Carbonara.

The Twelfth Amendment: text, purpose, and immediate implications

Key lines from the amendment

The operative inhabitant restriction in the Twelfth Amendment says electors may not cast both votes for persons who are inhabitants of the elector's own State, and legal reference material treats that sentence as the controlling textual rule U.S. National Archives. Additional statutory provisions and explanations are collected in the National Archives' legal provisions on the Electoral College National Archives Electoral College provisions.

The amendment rewrote how presidential and vice-presidential votes were recorded after the early republic experience, making it clear that ballots separate the two offices, which helps explain why the inhabitant clause matters for modern elector behavior and allocation Congressional Research Service report and related CRS materials CRS product.

Why the inhabitant language matters

Legal summaries note that the Twelfth Amendment's inhabitant language directly limits what electors from a given State may do and therefore can have practical effects on how a ticket receives the State's electoral support Congressional Research Service report.

Interpreters read the text as a restriction on elector votes rather than as an express prohibition on nominations or on who may appear together on a ballot, a distinction that shapes campaign choices in real elections U.S. National Archives Electoral College FAQ.

How electors cast votes now: mechanics under the Twelfth Amendment

Elector ballots in modern practice

Today electors cast separate ballots for president and vice president under procedures shaped by the Twelfth Amendment, and authoritative guidance explains that an elector cannot cast both votes for two inhabitants of that elector's own State U.S. National Archives Electoral College FAQ and in posts by the National Archives National Archives blog.

That technical rule means if both nominees claim legal residence in State X, the electors chosen in State X cannot legally register both votes for the ticket on a single ballot, which in practice could deprive the ticket of those electors' support U.S. National Archives Electoral College FAQ.

What the word inhabitant has meant in legal and historical practice

Scholar and CRS views on inhabitant and domicile

Scholars and the Congressional Research Service emphasize that inhabitant is a fact-based term linked to domicile, and its application depends on the individual circumstances of a candidate rather than a single bright-line rule Congressional Research Service report.

That means modern living patterns, multiple residences, and changes of address can complicate determinations, and legal commentary notes courts have not uniformly defined every edge of the term SCOTUSblog analysis.

Why inhabitant is fact-specific

Because the Twelfth Amendment triggers an inquiry into who is an inhabitant of a given State at the relevant time, in tight contests campaigns and state officials may litigate or closely examine domicile evidence, a risk highlighted by CRS and legal scholars Congressional Research Service report.

Practical disputes can hinge on where a candidate votes, pays taxes, maintains a home, or declares legal residency, so factual records and official filings matter more than labels like birth State or business ties SCOTUSblog analysis.

Practical electoral effect: how a same-state ticket could lose a state's electors

A stepwise example of how electors of State X would vote

Imagine both the presidential nominee and the running mate are inhabitants of State X; electors chosen in State X would be prohibited by the Twelfth Amendment from casting both votes for those two residents on a single ballot, which could reduce the ticket's tally of electors from that State U.S. National Archives Electoral College FAQ.

In step form: State X voters choose a slate of electors; those electors meet and cast separate votes for president and vice president; if both candidates are inhabitants of State X, the electors cannot lawfully record both votes for the two, and the ticket may be deprived of some or all of State X's electoral support depending on how electors act U.S. National Archives Electoral College FAQ.

Why a ticket can be deprived of electors

The practical loss occurs because the electorate that selected those electors cannot collectively register both votes for two inhabitants of their own State, and campaign strategists treat that as an avoidable electoral risk rather than a purely theoretical issue Ballotpedia.

Parties may therefore prefer to pick running mates who will not trigger the inhabitant restriction, or they may plan elector slates and candidate domicile changes to avoid an adverse outcome, steps discussed in reporting and legal summaries Ballotpedia.

Why parties usually avoid same-state tickets: strategy and trade-offs

Historical party practice since the early 20th century

Political parties have long treated a shared home state on a ticket as a political liability, and summaries of practice note that parties generally avoid that pairing so they do not risk losing a State's electors through the inhabitant rule Ballotpedia.

Reports on notable episodes show party leaders and campaign staffs typically weigh geographic balance and electoral vote allocation when they choose a running mate, which is a strategic rather than a strictly legal decision The Washington Post.

Political trade-offs in running mate selection

Choosing a running mate involves trade-offs among experience, ideology, regional balance, and the practical risk that a same-state ticket could be shorted of a state's electors; campaigns make those calculations in light of both law and politics Ballotpedia.

Because the inhabitant rule is tied to electors rather than ballots in a vacuum, parties treat domicile and elector planning as part of their strategic toolkit when evaluating potential vice-presidential choices U.S. National Archives Electoral College FAQ.

How campaigns and candidates manage the risk: residency changes and elector planning

Changing legal residence or asserting different domicile

Campaigns have sometimes addressed the same-state issue by having a running mate change legal residence or more clearly assert a domicile in a different State before ballots are finalized, a tactic examined in reporting and commentary The Washington Post.

Yes, the Constitution does not categorically bar a joint ticket from the same State, but the Twelfth Amendment restricts how electors may vote, creating practical electoral risks that campaigns often avoid.

Legal commentators caution that residence changes invite scrutiny and may not remove all uncertainty because courts or election officials might probe where a candidate's true domicile lies, a point raised in legal analysis and CRS summaries Congressional Research Service report.

Elector slate strategies parties can use

Parties can manage elector slates by selecting electors likely to follow party strategy or by arranging for electors in a shared home State to cast votes in a way that avoids an outright double-casting violation, though the details depend on state law and party rules Congressional Research Service report.

Campaigns therefore combine legal advice, state rule reviews, and sometimes domicile declarations when they plan to avoid the inhabitant problem, recognizing that these measures reduce practical risk but do not erase all legal questions SCOTUSblog analysis.

Legal uncertainties: what courts have not decided and where litigation could arise

Limits of Supreme Court precedent

There is no single Supreme Court decision that has settled every interpretive question about the inhabitant restriction, and CRS and legal commentators observe that unresolved edges of the rule remain candidates for adjudication in tight disputes Congressional Research Service report.

Because the text is the primary source of the rule, courts would likely turn to the language of the Twelfth Amendment and to historical practice when asked to resolve a high-stakes residency case, according to legal analysis SCOTUSblog analysis.

Potential state-level disputes and litigation triggers

If a close election turns on whether both nominees are inhabitants of a single State, state courts or federal courts could be asked to decide domicile questions or to interpret state ballot rules, and scholars note this is where litigation would most likely arise Congressional Research Service report.

Factors that could trigger litigation include ambiguous domicile evidence, conflicting state election rules, or elector behavior that departs from party expectations, and analysts advise campaigns to document residence claims carefully to reduce dispute risk SCOTUSblog analysis.

Common myths and mistakes when people talk about same-state tickets

Myth: the Constitution flatly forbids same-state tickets

A frequent myth is that the Constitution expressly bans a president and vice president from being from the same State; in fact the Twelfth Amendment restricts elector votes rather than forbidding joint nominations, as the amendment text and primary-source guidance make clear U.S. National Archives.

Correcting that misunderstanding helps readers see why parties treat the issue as an electoral and strategic problem rather than as a categorical constitutional bar, a point CRS notes in its background work Congressional Research Service report.

Mistake: equating state of birth with legal inhabitance

Another common mistake is to equate where a candidate was born or worked with the legal idea of inhabitant or domicile; legal standards look to current ties, filings, and declarations rather than birthplace alone SCOTUSblog analysis.

Readers should therefore check primary documents like campaign statements, voting records, and state filings before treating birthplace or past addresses as dispositive evidence of current inhabitant status Ballotpedia and our primary-source guide primary-source guide.

Possible modern scenarios: narrow margins, state rules, and contested ballots

Scenario A: both nominees claim different domiciles

In one realistic scenario both nominees can and do document distinct domiciles so the inhabitant restriction does not apply in a given State; in that case electors can cast votes without invoking the Twelfth Amendment limitation, and the election proceeds on ordinary procedures Congressional Research Service report.

Campaigns often plan for that outcome when selecting running mates or when a candidate shifts residence, and careful documentation reduces the chance of post-election litigation U.S. National Archives Electoral College FAQ.

Scenario B: state electors refuse or split votes

In a closely contested alternative, electors in the shared home State might refuse to record both votes for a same-state ticket or might split their votes to avoid a legal violation, producing an atypical electoral tally that could invite review and litigation Congressional Research Service report.

Such outcomes would depend on state rules, how electors are selected, and whether courts are prepared to intervene, and analysts point out that these are plausible but contingent scenarios rather than certainties SCOTUSblog analysis.
