What the Toyota Sequoia is and which buyers it suits

The Sequoia is a full-size, body-on-frame SUV built around large passenger capacity and towing ability. Its architecture favors durability for towing and heavy loads, and that shapes who benefits most from the car.

For buyers who need three-row seating and room for gear, the Sequoia offers a confident package with a strong powertrain range, including hybrid-assist variants in recent model years that improve torque and towing performance Edmunds Sequoia review.

Suitability depends on priorities. If you value low running costs and daily fuel economy over towing or maximum passenger room, a midsize SUV will often be cheaper to operate. If towing capacity and dealer support matter more, the Sequoia can be a better fit.

Consider how often you will tow, how many people you regularly carry and whether you accept higher fuel and maintenance expenses in exchange for capacity and capability.

Model-year safety ratings and recall checks to run before you buy

Safety ratings and test results differ across model years and trims. Always look up IIHS and NHTSA ratings for the exact year and trim you are considering, because ratings can change with structural, restraint or equipment updates IIHS vehicle ratings.

Recalls are a separate but essential check. Search NHTSA recall listings by VIN or model year to find open campaigns and required repairs before you finalize a purchase.

Document the model year and VIN from the listing, then verify both ratings and recalls as part of your screening checklist. If a safety-related recall remains open, ask the dealer for evidence that the recall work was completed before you buy.

Fuel economy and running costs to expect for a used Sequoia

The Sequoia's fuel economy is lower than smaller SUVs and varies with powertrain and towing use. Expect real-world consumption to rise with towing or heavy loads, so factor higher fuel costs into ownership planning Edmunds fuel and ownership insights.

Other cost drivers include insurance and maintenance. Full-size SUVs often attract higher insurance premiums and greater routine maintenance expense than midsize models, and those costs compound over multi-year ownership horizons.
