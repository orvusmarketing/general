What the Civil Rights Act of 1875 set out to do: purpose and historical context

Background in Reconstruction politics

The Civil Rights Act of 1875 was passed as Congress completed the work of Reconstruction and sought to extend federal protections into everyday social and commercial life. Primary summaries describe the law as an effort to bar racial discrimination in public settings and civic duties (see the constitutional-rights page).

Library of Congress summary

Congressional goals for social and commercial equality, the civil rights act of 1875

Lawmakers framed the statute to guarantee equal access to public accommodations, public transportation, and jury service, and to prohibit discrimination on the basis of race, color, or previous condition of servitude. The language reflected the final phase of Reconstruction and an intent to protect African Americans' participation in public life. Read our explainer.

Congress adopted criminal penalties for violations rather than broad private civil remedies. That choice shaped how the law could be applied in courts and made enforcement depend on federal prosecution decisions.

National Archives teaching note

What the law actually said: key provisions and who it covered

Public accommodations and transportation

The statute contained clauses that prohibited denying the enjoyment of public accommodations and public transportation facilities on account of race, color, or previous condition of servitude. In plain terms, the law aimed to make it unlawful to exclude African Americans from inns, public conveyances, and other places used by the public.

Library of Congress summary

Jury service and legal language to prohibit discrimination

Another explicit provision protected the right of persons to serve on juries without racial exclusion. Access to jury service mattered because it affected criminal justice and civic participation; excluding African Americans from juries limited the fair administration of criminal law in many communities.

The statute used criminal penalties and federal prosecution as its primary enforcement mechanism. That meant private individuals generally could not pursue the same civil remedies the modern reader might expect; enforcement required federal authorities to bring charges in many cases.

National Archives teaching note

How enforcement worked in practice and the law's limits before 1883

The Act's practical protections were constrained by its enforcement design. Because the law depended on federal criminal prosecution, its effect turned on political will, available resources, and priorities in federal law enforcement during the late 1870s.

National Archives teaching note

Local resistance and limited federal capacity meant many violations went unprosecuted. Historians note that, as Reconstruction drew to a close, enforcement waned and the law rarely produced widespread, durable change on the ground.

Eric Foner, Reconstruction: America's Unfinished Revolution

The Supreme Court in 1883: the Civil Rights Cases and the change in legal authority

Overview of the 1883 rulings

In 1883 the Supreme Court decided the Civil Rights Cases and concluded that the Fourteenth Amendment did not authorize Congress to regulate private acts of racial discrimination. The ruling sharply limited the Act's reach by placing private discrimination largely outside federal power as the Court read the amendment. See the Oyez case overview and the Constitution Center discussion for additional context: Oyez overview of the Civil Rights Cases and Constitution Center summary.

The Act aimed to guarantee equal access to public accommodations, public transportation, and jury service, but its reliance on federal prosecution, declining political will during the end of Reconstruction, and the Supreme Court's 1883 interpretation limiting Congress's power over private discrimination left its protections limited and enabled state-level segregation to expand.

The Court's reading of the Fourteenth Amendment

The Court distinguished state action from private conduct and held that Congress could not use the Fourteenth Amendment to compel private parties to treat all persons equally in public accommodations. That legal reasoning removed a central tool Congress had used in the Act. The full opinion is also available at a case law repository: Justia - Civil Rights Cases.

How the Act's failure affected African Americans' civil liberties and the rise of Jim Crow

Legal historians trace a connection between weak federal enforcement, the 1883 decision, and a broader rollback of federal protections for African Americans in the late nineteenth century. The constrained federal role made space for state laws that mandated segregation and exclusion.

Encyclopaedia Britannica entry

Concrete areas affected included public accommodations, where segregation became common, and jury service, where systematic exclusion of Black jurors persisted. Those practical effects reduced civic participation and access to equal treatment in public settings.

Eric Foner, Reconstruction

While the Act did not prevent the rise of separate state laws that enforced segregation, scholars note it became an important antecedent in legal memory. Later civil-rights advocates and lawmakers cited Reconstruction-era statutes and court disputes when building twentieth-century reform strategies.

Encyclopaedia Britannica entry

The Act's legal legacy: how it was used in later reform and litigation

After its practical decline, the Act and the litigation surrounding it remained part of legal discussion. Historians and legal scholars identify lines from Reconstruction statutes to later federal civil-rights laws, even where the connection was one of argument and precedent rather than direct causal effect.

Eric Foner, Reconstruction

Lawyers and legislators in the twentieth century sometimes cited the 1875 statute and the Civil Rights Cases as reference points while arguing both for and against broader federal authority to secure equal treatment. Those references helped shape statutory and constitutional strategies used later.

Encyclopaedia Britannica entry

Common misunderstandings and pitfalls when reading about the Act

A frequent mistake is to conflate the statutory text with the on-the-ground effect. The Act's language promised certain protections, but the choice of enforcement mechanism and the political context governed how those promises translated into practice.

Library of Congress summary

Readers also sometimes misread the Court's role. The 1883 decision limited congressional authority over private discrimination; that judicial interpretation differs from statutes aimed at state action or public officials, so parsing the legal categories matters for understanding outcomes.

Conclusion: what the Act's purpose and failure tell us about civil liberties after Reconstruction

The Civil Rights Act of 1875 aimed to extend federal protection into daily social and commercial life, promising equal access to public accommodations, transportation, and jury service. Its enforcement design and the political context limited what the law achieved in practice.

Library of Congress summary

The Supreme Court's 1883 rulings narrowed congressional authority over private discrimination, and historians connect those developments to the later emergence of Jim Crow laws and reduced federal protection of African American civil liberties. Still, the Act and its litigation remained part of the legal memory that informed twentieth-century reforms.

Encyclopaedia Britannica entry

It sought to guarantee equal access to public accommodations, transportation, and jury service and to prohibit racial discrimination in those areas under federal law.

The Act relied on federal criminal prosecution rather than private civil remedies and enforcement declined as Reconstruction ended, limiting practical protections.

The Civil Rights Cases held that the Fourteenth Amendment did not authorize Congress to regulate private discrimination, sharply reducing the Act's reach.

Understanding the Act, its limits, and the 1883 decisions helps explain why federal protection of civil liberties narrowed after Reconstruction. The statute remains important in legal history, both as a statement of congressional intent and as a point of reference for later reformers.
