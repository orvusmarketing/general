Quick answer: Which amendment forbids double jeopardy?

The 5th amendment constitution contains the Double Jeopardy Clause. The Clause prevents a person from being tried or punished more than once for the same offense, as set out in the constitutional text recorded in the National Archives transcript National Archives transcript.

In plain language, double jeopardy means a government cannot put someone on trial twice for the same crime or impose multiple criminal punishments for one act. This short answer frames the longer discussion below on the legal tests, exceptions, timing rules, and practical scenarios.

5th amendment constitution: text and historical context

The Fifth Amendment is part of the Bill of Rights and includes the Double Jeopardy Clause, which, in essence, protects against multiple prosecutions or punishments for the same offense; the Amendment was ratified as part of the Bill of Rights in 1791, and the historical text is preserved in the National Archives National Archives transcript.

The Clause was included in the Bill of Rights to limit government power and to reflect concerns of the Founders about repeated prosecutions and abusive prosecutions. For a clear legal overview of how courts read the Clause today, legal summaries provide accessible explanations of the Clause and its practical effects LII double jeopardy overview and see related material on constitutional rights.

Put simply, the Clause serves as a constitutional backstop: it is part of the written Constitution and courts treat it as a fundamental procedural protection whenever criminal charges are at issue.

Core legal tests: Blockburger, dual-sovereignty, and mistrial rules

Courts apply a set of established tests to decide if double-jeopardy protection applies in a given case. One central test asks whether two charges are the same offense under a unit-of-prosecution approach known as the Blockburger test Blockburger decision.

Under Blockburger, two offenses are considered distinct when each requires proof of an element that the other does not. If both statutes require the same elements, then the second prosecution can be treated as barred under the same-offense framework.

Another critical line of authority concerns the dual-sovereignty doctrine. The Supreme Court reaffirmed that separate sovereigns, such as a state and the federal government, can prosecute the same underlying conduct under their different laws without violating double-jeopardy protections Gamble opinion and see the Court text at GAMBLE v. UNITED STATES.

The third doctrinal area addresses mistrials. A mistrial does not automatically bar a retrial. When a judge finds that a mistrial was necessary for manifest necessity, or that the defendant’s own conduct caused the mistrial, retrial is typically allowed under the rule explained in Arizona v. Washington Arizona v. Washington summary and see the full opinion at Arizona v. Washington (Justia).

Each of these doctrines answers a different question. Blockburger asks whether two offenses are legally the same. Dual-sovereignty asks whether separate governments may prosecute the same conduct. Arizona v. Washington asks whether the procedural end of a trial bars starting over.

When double jeopardy does not block prosecution: civil penalties and separate sovereigns

Not all repeated government actions are barred by the Double Jeopardy Clause. Civil, regulatory, or administrative penalties are generally not treated as criminal punishments that invoke the Clause; courts ask whether a sanction is punitive in character before calling it a criminal punishment LII double jeopardy overview.

That means many licensing suspensions, fines from administrative agencies, or regulatory remedies can coexist with criminal charges arising from the same facts. Whether a particular remedy is criminal or civil is a fact-sensitive inquiry that can change the outcome in close cases.

The dual-sovereignty exception is another clear limit on the Clause. A state may prosecute someone for violating state law and the federal government may prosecute for violation of federal law based on the same conduct, because the prosecutions are brought by separate sovereigns that legislate under different powers Gamble opinion.

In practice, prosecutors often coordinate to avoid duplicative proceedings, but the constitutional rule allows separate prosecutions where sovereign interests and statutes differ. Close cases about whether a sanction is so punitive that it should be treated as criminal are decided on the specific record and legal tests that courts apply.

How defendants raise a double-jeopardy claim and why timing matters

Defendants who want to assert double-jeopardy protections should raise the issue promptly, usually by pretrial motion, a trial objection, or on appeal, because courts treat the procedural posture as central to whether relief is available LII double jeopardy overview.

For example, a judge considering whether retrial is barred after a mistrial will examine the record and the reasons the trial ended; when a mistrial occurred for manifest necessity, retrial is often permitted under the rule explained in Arizona v. Washington Arizona v. Washington summary.

Failure to object at the proper time may forfeit the claim in many procedural contexts. Preservation of the issue, clear factual record, and timely briefing help appellate courts review the double-jeopardy questions with the necessary detail.

Decision criteria judges use: a practical framework

Judges typically follow a stepwise framework when assessing a double-jeopardy question. First, they identify the statutory elements of the offenses at issue and apply the Blockburger unit-of-prosecution test to determine whether the offenses are the same under governing precedent Blockburger decision.

Second, courts check sovereign identity. If separate sovereigns brought the charges, the dual-sovereignty doctrine may allow both prosecutions to proceed, because each sovereign enforces its own laws and interests Gamble opinion.

Third, judges classify the earlier proceeding: was it a criminal punishment or a civil remedy? If a prior government action was punitive and criminal in nature, it is more likely to trigger double-jeopardy protections; determining that often requires factual analysis of the sanction and its purpose LII double jeopardy overview.

Finally, the court reviews procedural posture. If the earlier trial ended in a mistrial, the court considers whether the mistrial was necessary or was caused by the defendant, per the Arizona v. Washington framework, before barring retrial Arizona v. Washington summary.

Common mistakes, myths, and pitfalls to avoid

A frequent myth is that any second prosecution for the same conduct is automatically unconstitutional. That is not the law. Dual-sovereignty and unit-of-prosecution tests show there are important exceptions to a blanket rule against repetition Gamble opinion.

Another mistake is assuming that a civil penalty automatically triggers double-jeopardy protection. Courts examine the nature and purpose of the sanction to decide if it is punitive; many regulatory penalties are treated as civil and are not barred by the Clause LII double jeopardy overview.

Procedural waiver is also common. If a defendant does not raise a timely objection or preserve the argument, courts may decline to address the double-jeopardy claim on appeal. That is why prompt pretrial motions and clear record development matter.

Practical examples and short scenarios

Scenario 1. A state prosecutor charges a person with state-level offenses arising from an incident. Later, federal authorities bring federal charges for conduct that also violates federal law. Under the dual-sovereignty doctrine, the federal prosecution can proceed even after the state case, because separate sovereigns enforce their own statutes Gamble opinion.

Scenario 2. A jury is unable to reach a unanimous verdict and the judge declares a mistrial for a hung jury. The judge then allows a retrial because the mistrial was necessary to reach a verdict. Under Arizona v. Washington, retrial in that situation is typically permissible when the judge finds manifest necessity Arizona v. Washington summary.

Scenario 3. An individual faces a civil enforcement action by a regulatory agency that imposes a fine or license restriction. The agency’s action is civil in form. If prosecutors later file criminal charges for the same conduct, courts often treat the civil penalty and the criminal prosecution as separate unless the civil sanction is so punitive as to be considered criminal in nature LII double jeopardy overview.

These scenarios show how the same facts can lead to different outcomes depending on who prosecutes, how the statutes are written, and what the record shows about the purpose of a prior proceeding.
