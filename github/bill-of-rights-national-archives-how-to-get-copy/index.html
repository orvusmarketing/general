What the Bill of Rights at the National Archives is and where it is kept

The original Bill of Rights is preserved and exhibited by the National Archives as part of the Charters of Freedom and is stewarded for public access and preservation National Archives Bill of Rights (Charters of Freedom).

NARA maintains both the physical exhibit in Washington, D.C., and digital resources. The Archives makes high-resolution images available online so students, researchers, and the public can study the documents without handling the originals Charters of Freedom - Preservation, Access and Reproductions.

The original documents remain on display at the Charters of Freedom exhibit in Washington. They are not removable from the Archives; viewing and reproduction are managed under NARA's stewardship Request or Order Copies of Records and Exhibits.

Knowing that NARA is the custodian helps you choose the right route: immediate downloads for study, formal reproduction orders for certified or high-quality physical prints, and visit planning for in-person viewing.

How to submit a reproduction request to the National Archives

Start by locating the Bill of Rights item record in the National Archives Catalog. The Catalog entry provides the record ID and links to reproduction options and forms, which is the formal entry point for requests National Archives Catalog: Bill of Rights.

A typical reproduction request asks for the record ID or citation, your contact and billing information, the intended use of the image or copy, and the reproduction format you want. Include these items clearly to avoid delays Reproductions and Permissions - National Archives.

Use NARA’s reproduction and permissions request forms as directed on the Catalog entry or the Reproductions page. The forms guide you through specifying format, resolution, and rights for the intended use.

After submission, retain the request confirmation and any tracking number. Reproductions commonly incur fees and have variable processing times; follow up via the Contacts listed on the reproduction pages if you need status updates Request or Order Copies of Records and Exhibits.

Reproduction options explained: digital scans, photographic prints, and certified reproductions

NARA lists several common products: high-resolution digital scans suitable for study, photographic or print-quality reproductions for display, and certified or notarized reproductions for official or legal purposes Reproductions and Permissions - National Archives.

Digital scans are the most common and are usually delivered as high-resolution image files. Photographic prints are suitable when you need a physical copy for display, subject to size and framing restrictions from the Archives.

Reproduction requests commonly involve fees. Fees vary by product type, format, and whether certification or notarization is required. Certified reproductions generally cost more than standard digital scans.

Certified and notarized reproductions: when you may need them

Certified reproductions are appropriate when an organization or legal process requires a reproduction with formal verification. Examples include provenance documentation, official court filings, or institutional archives that accept certified copies as primary evidence Reproductions and Permissions - National Archives.

The application process for certified or notarized reproductions is more formal. Expect to provide detailed intended-use statements and possibly to submit identity or institutional documentation as requested by the Reproductions and Permissions office.

Because certified reproductions can require extra review and clerical steps, allow for longer lead times and higher fees versus standard scans or prints. Confirm exact requirements with NARA before applying.

If you are unsure whether your use requires certification, contact the Reproductions and Permissions office for advice rather than assuming a standard download will suffice.

Common mistakes and pitfalls when requesting copies

Common errors include submitting incomplete request forms, providing the wrong record ID, or failing to state the intended use clearly. These issues delay processing and can lead to request rejection National Archives Catalog: Bill of Rights.

Another frequent pitfall is assuming a certified reproduction is free or will be produced quickly. Certified copies often cost more and require additional review; confirm fees and lead times on the Reproductions page Reproductions and Permissions - National Archives.

Also verify payment and ordering policies before you submit. Policies changed in 2025, so confirm current instructions to avoid payment delays or returned orders.

Finally, if you expect to publish or sell reproductions, check the reproduction terms and permissions to confirm whether commercial use is allowed and whether additional permission is needed.

How to cite or reference the Bill of Rights in research or publication

Plain-text transcriptions from the Library of Congress are public domain and can be cited with a standard source line naming the repository and the transcription page; this is suitable for most student and scholarly uses The Bill of Rights: A Transcription.

When citing a NARA high-resolution image, include the item citation or record ID, the repository (National Archives), and the date you accessed the image. If you use a reproduced image in publication, check NARA's reproduction terms first National Archives Bill of Rights (Charters of Freedom).

Differentiate between citing an image and citing a transcription. A transcription cites the producing repository for the text, while an image citation should include the digital file details when available.

If in doubt about commercial publication or exhibition, request permission through NARA’s Reproductions and Permissions office before finalizing your work.

Practical scenarios: examples for students, researchers and display
