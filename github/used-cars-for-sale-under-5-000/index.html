A compact checklist to run before you contact a seller

How to read a vehicle history report: what matters and what is ambiguous

Key fields to check: title status, ownership, service history, accident indicators

Start with title status. Branded titles such as salvage or rebuilt indicate prior severe damage or insurance write-offs and usually require deeper inquiry and a stricter inspection approach FTC buying a used car.

Check ownership history and mileage records. Multiple prior owners, frequent out-of-state transfers, or mileage inconsistencies should prompt follow-up questions and a mechanic review NHTSA used vehicle checklist.

Common report limitations and how to resolve them

Reports can show no accident reported when a vehicle still has undisclosed damage, because not all events are captured in public databases. Treat a clean report as useful context, not definitive proof, and verify key items with inspection and service receipts where available Consumer Reports used car checklist.

When you see mileage anomalies or gaps in service history, try to obtain prior service records or contact previous owners if possible, and insist on a pre-purchase inspection that includes a compression and underbody check NHTSA used vehicle checklist.

Step-by-step inspection plan: from test drive to independent mechanic

What to do on the test drive

Begin with a visual walkaround, then test systems during a short drive. Note noises, vibration, braking performance, and transmission shifts. Record any irregular sounds or warning lights to show the mechanic later NHTSA used vehicle checklist.

Bring a checklist and ask to drive on mixed roads if possible; a neighborhood-only test can hide transmission or cooling problems that appear under sustained speed or load Consumer Reports used car checklist.
