Quick answer: what the civil rights bill 1866 did

The civil rights bill 1866 declared that all persons born in the United States and not subject to any foreign power are citizens and provided them the right to make and enforce contracts and to own, purchase and convey property, as the statute's text states GovInfo reproduction of 14 Stat. 27

Congress enacted the law in 1866 and recorded a congressional override of President Andrew Johnson's veto in the legislative record, a fact noted in archival summaries of the period National Archives milestone page (House history)

How and why Congress passed the Act in 1866

In the months after the Civil War, Congress debated how to secure rights for formerly enslaved people and how far federal law could protect those rights in the states, a central question of Reconstruction politics National Archives milestone page (DocsTeach primary source)

Legislators wrote the statute to create a statutory guarantee of certain civil rights and then passed it when the vote to override the President succeeded in Congress, an outcome recorded in the Act's legislative history GovInfo reproduction of 14 Stat. 27

The law's core provisions: citizenship, contracts, property, remedies

The statute declares that citizens are those born in the United States and not subject to foreign power, and it provides that citizens shall have the same rights as enjoyed by white citizens to make and enforce contracts, to sue and be parties, and to inherit, purchase, lease, sell, hold and convey property GovInfo reproduction of 14 Stat. 27

The statute declared U.S. birthright citizenship for persons not subject to foreign powers and provided statutory rights to make and enforce contracts and to own and convey property, while enforcement during Reconstruction was uneven and later courts and the Fourteenth Amendment shaped its legal role.

Those clauses were written as statutory rights, and the Act also included remedial language intended to allow injured parties to seek legal redress in federal courts when local remedies were not available Legal Information Institute summary

In practical terms, the text aimed to put formerly enslaved people on the same statutory footing as other U.S. citizens for contracts and property, while providing courts a statutory basis to vindicate those rights Legal Information Institute summary

How the Act related to the Fourteenth Amendment and constitutional change

The Civil Rights Act of 1866 preceded the Fourteenth Amendment and helped spur debates that led to the amendment's adoption, a relationship documented in primary collections and historical summaries Library of Congress primary documents

Scholarship treats the statute and the Fourteenth Amendment as related but distinct legal developments: the Act is a statutory guarantee, while the Amendment created constitutional protections and new grounds for judicial review Eric Foner's discussion of Reconstruction

Enforcement on the ground during Reconstruction: limits and resistance

Historians note that enforcement of the 1866 law was uneven because local resistance and limited federal enforcement capacity often blocked consistent remedies for rights violations National Archives milestone page

Archival records and later histories describe episodes where local officials resisted federal efforts or where federal authorities lacked the resources or political will to enforce the statute fully Eric Foner's discussion of Reconstruction

Because enforcement depended on local courts and officials as well as federal action, the Act's protections did not always translate into consistent, nationwide relief in the 1860s and 1870s National Archives milestone page

How later courts used the 1866 law to address private discrimination

In the 20th century the Supreme Court relied on the 1866 statute to address private racial discrimination in housing and other areas, illustrating that the law retained a role beyond its Reconstruction origins Jones v. Alfred H. Mayer Co. opinion

The Court's 1968 decision in Jones used the statute's text to prohibit certain private acts of racial discrimination in property transactions, and later cases applied the law in similar contexts Legal Information Institute summary

Runyon v. McCrary further shows how courts read the Act against private discrimination in school admissions and related disputes, using the statute as one of several possible legal bases for relief Legal Information Institute summary

How the 1866 Act fits into modern civil-rights statutes and §1981

Legal commentators treat the 1866 statute as part of a broader statutory landscape that includes section 1981 and later federal civil-rights laws, and courts sometimes use the older statute alongside those modern provisions Legal Information Institute summary

Scholars and judges continue to discuss how the 1866 law interacts with statutory frameworks that developed later, and some questions about scope and overlap remain active topics of legal debate Eric Foner's discussion of Reconstruction
