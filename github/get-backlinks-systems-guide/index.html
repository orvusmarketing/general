What "get backlinks" means in 2026

Definition and why links still matter

A backlink is a hyperlink from another website that points to your site. In practical terms, to get backlinks means creating and promoting content or assets that others cite because they find value in them. Editorially earned links are different from paid or manipulative links; earned links arrive because a third party chose to reference your content within a relevant context.

Search engines still describe paid, manipulative, or artificially created links as schemes rather than legitimate editorial citations, which is important to know before you invest time in tactics that resemble buying or mass-creating links. Google Search Central

How search engines treat different link types

Search systems distinguish between contextual, editorial links and links that appear to have been placed for the sole purpose of influencing rankings. That distinction affects risk and long-term durability of any link you try to get. Earned editorial links tend to persist because they sit inside content that provides value to a reader, while low-value links often lack that editorial context.

Industry analysis continues to show that links gained through relevant content plus targeted outreach are the most sustainable source of referral and search architecture value, especially when they are relevant to your topic. Ahrefs industry analysis

Why get backlinks still matters for search architecture

Backlinks as part of search architecture

Backlinks remain an input to larger search architecture decisions. They help signal topical relevance and can route referral traffic into content funnels when links appear on pages with a matching audience. Treating links as an isolated metric misses their role inside content architecture and measurement systems.

When you plan how to get backlinks, think about the pages you want to support and which parts of the funnel need more visitors or trust signals. Organize content so that link-driven referrals land on pages designed to convert or to feed readers deeper into your site.

How links feed content funnels and measurement

A useful approach is to map likely referrers to funnel stages and then measure which links actually move engagement or conversions. That shifts the focus from collecting link counts to improving referral value and attribution accuracy.

Measurement best practices in recent guides recommend tying backlinks to conversions and referral traffic using analytics and experiments rather than treating raw link counts as the primary KPI. SEMrush guidance on prioritization and measurement

Safe versus risky ways to get backlinks

Google's line: what counts as a link scheme

Google's guidance still classifies paid or manipulative links as link schemes, so any tactic that looks like buying placement or mass-creating links increases risk and should be avoided unless you understand the tradeoffs. Google Search Central

Common risky tactics and red flags

Common risky tactics include paid link networks, extensive link exchanges, and mass submissions to generic directories. These approaches often lack editorial context and can be both low-value and high-risk. In some narrow cases a niche directory can be useful for local citation consistency, but that is the exception rather than the rule.

Prioritize opportunities that match your audience and funnel, start with asset-first outreach such as broken-link reclamation or resource pages, measure referral and conversion impact, then scale the tactics that show repeatable gains.

Signals that a link is low value include lack of topical relevance, pages that exist mainly to list links, and obvious automation in anchor text or placement. If a link does not bring a relevant audience, it is unlikely to add durable referral value.

A practical framework to earn backlinks without paying

Prioritize by relevance and funnel impact

Start with an audit: identify which pages already attract traffic, which parts of your funnel need help, and what assets you have that can plausibly earn editorial links. Prioritize opportunities where the audience overlap is strongest and where a link would route meaningful referrals into a conversion path.

Effective prioritization uses relevance and expected referral impact rather than raw domain metrics alone. Mapping assets to funnel stages helps you choose work that aligns with business objectives and measurement capabilities.

Asset-first outreach and matching

Build or adapt an asset to match the target. For broken-link reclamation or resource-page outreach, the most successful pitches match a specific existing asset to a specific missing or related resource.

Focus your outreach on assets that offer clear value to the linking page: original data, useful visuals, concise how-to steps, or a uniquely helpful aggregation. Matching asset to need increases editorial acceptance and reduces wasted effort.

Measurement and iteration loop

Make measurement part of the process. Tag outreach campaigns, track referral traffic, and run small experiments to see what kind of outreach or asset format generates repeatable gains. Then deprioritize approaches that do not show measurable referral or conversion impact.

When you apply this feedback loop consistently, you can allocate time to tactics that actually move the needle for your funnel instead of chasing link counts.

Tactics: How to get backlinks through content and outreach

Broken-link reclamation

Broken-link reclamation works by finding pages that link to resources that no longer exist and offering your matching content as a replacement. The best matches are specific: identify a broken target, confirm the context, and offer a single, directly relevant replacement asset.

To find opportunities, use crawler tools or manual site checks on pages that are thematically aligned with your content. Personalizing the pitch and explaining the direct relevance tends to increase success rates. Search Engine Journal guide to broken link building

Resource pages and targeted guest posts

Resource pages and editorial guest posts are still viable when the contribution adds clear value. For resource pages, locate lists that fit your subject and show why your asset improves the resource for their audience. For guest posts, pitch ideas that are tailored to the host site's readers and that demonstrate topical expertise.

Guest contributions that include original examples, case notes, or visuals tend to have better editorial fit than recycled overviews. Focused relevance helps keep the outreach process efficient.

PR channels and HARO

PR channels like HARO are useful as one part of a diversified outreach program. They can generate editorial links when a pitch matches a reporter's need and provides timely, newsworthy value.

Because HARO responses are competitive and variable, use them selectively and tailor pitches so they clearly match the reporter's query. Treat PR as a complementary tactic rather than your primary link source. HARO guidance for sources

Outreach workflow for small teams

Finding and qualifying targets

Use simple filters to qualify targets: topical relevance, audience overlap, editorial quality, and whether the target accepts contributions or links to resources. Prioritize targets that can route visitors to the part of your funnel you want to test.

Keep qualification fast: a short checklist of relevance, domain context, and contactability helps small teams avoid spending time on low-probability leads.

Personalization at scale

Use a template for structure but personalize three elements: the specific page you reference, the clear value your asset provides, and a single small ask. This minimum personalization is often enough to stand out while keeping outreach scalable.

Track which personalization elements correlate with positive replies, then bake those into your templates so the team improves as it runs more outreach.

Time allocation and cadence

Small teams benefit from batching: allocate a fixed weekly block for outreach research, another for messaging, and a short window for follow ups. A realistic cadence is more effective than trying to contact many targets with low-quality messages.

Set a limit for attempts per target and record why you stop pursuing certain prospects. That history helps the team learn faster and avoid repeating low-yield work. Search Engine Journal on practical outreach

Measure link impact: from referral traffic to conversions

Attribution: beyond link counts

Measure links by what they send, not how many you have. Track referral sessions, downstream engagement, and conversion metrics tied to pages that earn links. This perspective makes it easier to compare tactics by business impact rather than by raw link volume.

Prioritization frameworks that focus on attribution help teams stop work that produces links without traffic or conversions. Use UTM tagging and clear landing pages to avoid losing attribution in the analytics stack. SEMrush on measuring link impact

UTM tagging and referral tracking

Use UTM tags when you control the link placement or when you can ask a partner to include them. For editorial links you cannot tag, configure analytics to surface referral paths and landing pages so you can still measure the downstream effect of those links.

Good referral tracking shows which link sources lead to repeat visits or conversions and helps you attribute value when the link is part of a longer buyer journey.

Running simple experiments

Run time-boxed experiments: promote the same asset with different outreach messages or to different target sets and compare referral behavior. Small A/B style tests on landing pages let you see whether link-driven traffic converts at expected rates before scaling the tactic.

Document experiments and outcomes. Over time, this builds a test roadmap that improves predictability around which outreach formats and assets tend to work.

Common mistakes when trying to get backlinks

Mass submissions and low-value directories

Mass submission services and generic directories are often low-value because they lack editorial context and audience relevance. In most cases these approaches will not move meaningful referral traffic and can create maintenance overhead.

Use niche directories only when they serve real discovery or local citation needs, and verify editorial standards before investing time. Google Search Central

Over-optimized anchor text and transactional approaches

Over-optimized anchor text and obvious link exchanges reduce editorial credibility and can be a red flag to search systems. Aim for natural phrasing and context that explains why the linked resource is useful to readers.

Transactional approaches focused solely on placements tend to return links with little referral value, so weigh the time cost versus likely audience benefit.

Ignoring editorial relevance

Links that ignore topical relevance rarely produce durable referral value. Prioritize relevance in target selection and asset design so that links connect genuine audiences with useful content.

When in doubt, ask whether the link would help a real reader solve a problem or learn something new; if not, it is unlikely to be worth pursuing.

How to choose which backlink tactics to prioritize

Estimating time versus impact

Apply a simple ROI lens to each tactic: estimate time cost, relevance to audience, likelihood of editorial placement, and how measurable the result will be. This lets you compare a time-consuming outreach with high expected relevance against a low-effort tactic with little audience overlap.

Document these estimates and revisit them after running a small test so your prioritization becomes evidence driven.

Team constraints and channel fit

Match tactics to what your team can do. If you have technical capacity and data, create unique assets that showcase insights. If you have strong relationships with journalists or partners, prioritize PR and guest contributions. Choose tactics that fit current skills and contacts.

Over time, close gaps with small investments in tooling or workflows rather than hiring big teams for short-lived campaigns.

Building a test roadmap

Build a short test roadmap that sequences experiments from quick wins to larger bets. Start with broken-link reclamation or resource page outreach for relatively low friction, then scale to guest posts or targeted PR if early tests show referral lifts.

Stop or reassign tactics that fail to deliver measurable referral or conversion improvements within the time windows you set. SEMrush guidance

30 to 90 day example plan to start earning backlinks

Weeks 1 to 4: audit and quick wins

Week 1: run a content and backlink audit to identify pages with existing social or organic traction and any broken external links pointing to your domain. Week 2: prioritize three assets to promote based on funnel fit. Week 3 to 4: run broken-link reclamation and reach out to a short list of resource pages with one highly relevant asset each.

Quick wins often come from reclaiming broken links or updating resources that already receive traffic, because these tasks require relatively little creation time and can produce measurable referral changes.

Weeks 5 to 8: outreach and scaling

Expand outreach to a larger but still curated target list. Test guest post pitches on two sites and run selective HARO responses where your expertise matches a query. Use templated personalization and track responses carefully.

Scale what shows positive referral or engagement signals, and reduce work on approaches that return few links or little traffic.

Weeks 9 to 12: measurement and iteration

Analyze referral traffic, conversion rates, and engagement for pages that gained links. Run small landing page A/B tests if conversion behavior is unclear. Decide which tactics to repeat and which to stop, and plan the next 90-day cycle with evidence from these experiments.

Document the playbook and hand it to whoever will own the next cycle so tribal knowledge does not get lost.

Practical examples: email templates and asset checklist

Broken-link outreach template

Keep the outreach short: identify the broken page, state the replacement asset's specific usefulness, and offer the replacement link. Personalization should reference the exact URL and one brief reason why your asset fits the context.

Example structure: compliment, note of a broken or outdated link with the URL, single-sentence explanation of how your asset helps, and a polite close. This minimal format keeps responses more likely.

Guest post pitch checklist

Include these elements: a concise pitch headline, 2-3 bullet points of original value you will provide, an example paragraph, and links to past relevant work. Editors value clarity and a clear angle for their audience.

Keep pitches targeted to the host's readers and avoid sending generic outlines that could apply to anyone.

Resource page submission checklist

For resource pages, ensure the asset has clear utility, a descriptive title, a brief meta description, and at least one visual or data point that increases usefulness. Provide the exact suggested anchor text but allow the editor to choose final phrasing.

A linkable asset checklist includes original data or research, clear visuals, concise explanations, and direct relevance to the target audience.

Combining PR and ongoing content promotion for durable links

When to prioritize HARO and PR

Use HARO and PR when you have newsworthy angles or timely expertise that match queries. HARO is efficient for short responses but is variable, so treat it as one of several channels in your outreach mix. HARO guidance

Because press coverage can bring high editorial value, it is worth experimenting with a focused set of pitches, but it should not replace systematic outreach to niche-targeted resources.

Turning one-off coverage into ongoing relationships

After a placement, follow up with the author with useful updates, data, or exclusive angles. Offer sources or additional examples for future stories to convert one-off coverage into a longer-term relationship that can produce recurring links.

Relationships are easier to maintain if you act as a reliable, helpful source rather than a constant promoter.

Using partnerships and collaborations

Partnerships like collaborative content, data sharing, or co-authored guides can produce durable editorial links when both parties bring complementary audiences. These collaborations often yield links that are contextually relevant and useful to readers.

Approach partnerships with clear expectations and small initial pilots before committing more resources.

Next steps and how a systems approach helps you get backlinks

Recommended next steps: run a simple audit, pick one tactic to test for 30 days, and instrument measurement so you can see whether referral traffic or conversions change. This evidence-driven loop is more reliable than chasing link counts.

A systems approach that combines search architecture, small automations, and clear workflows reduces recurring effort and makes backlink work repeatable. In many cases a focused diagnostic from a growth partner can clarify which tactics to prioritize for your funnel.

The safest route is to earn editorial links by creating useful, relevant assets and doing targeted outreach or broken-link reclamation; avoid paid placements and mass submissions.

Track referral sessions, downstream engagement, and conversions tied to the linked landing page using analytics and UTM conventions to see if the link drives meaningful activity.

HARO can be useful when you have timely, relevant expertise, but it is variable and should be one channel among several in a diversified outreach program.

Begin with a short audit and a single 30-day test. Instrument outcomes, decide based on measured referral and conversion lifts, and document the workflow so the next cycle is faster.
