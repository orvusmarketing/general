Introduction and why this question matters

Brief preview of the six exceptions

U.S. law recognizes a limited set of speech categories that are not protected by the First Amendment, and these limits matter for online and political expression because criminal rules and platform policies now intersect more than before. This article uses primary legal tests and international frameworks to explain six common exceptions and to show where questions remain about enforcement of cybercrime rules and freedom online, including how cybercrime law and freedom of speech complicate crossborder cases and platform moderation

The Supreme Court provides the operative legal tests for many exceptions, but enforcement also involves statutes and international agreements. At a glance: the six exceptions to freedom of speech

List of the six categories with one-line legal tests

  1. Incitement - Unprotected when speech is intended and likely to produce imminent lawless action under the Brandenburg test Brandenburg v. Ohio
  2. True threats - Statements that a reasonable person would interpret as a serious expression of intent to harm, evaluated with attention to the speaker's state of mind and context Elonis v. United States
  3. Obscenity - Material meeting the three Miller prongs: community standards, sexual conduct depiction, and lack of serious value Miller v. California
  4. Defamation - False factual assertions about public officials actionable only with proof of actual malice under New York Times Co. v. Sullivan New York Times Co. v. Sullivan
  5. National security and prior restraint - Government attempts to bar publication face strict limits under the Pentagon Papers precedent New York Times Co. v. United States
  6. Certain cybercrime offenses - Domestic statutes and international cooperation can criminalize online conduct that intersects with expressive activity, producing jurisdictional complexity Budapest Convention on Cybercrime

Courts and statutes differ in application: constitutional doctrine sets high bars for penalizing speech, but statutory offenses and international instruments can create enforcement paths that raise free speech concerns

Incitement: when speech aims to produce imminent lawless action

The Brandenburg test explained

The controlling test for incitement requires both that the speaker intends to produce lawless action and that the speech is likely to produce imminent lawless action, a high standard that protects advocacy of ideas in many settings Brandenburg v. Ohio

Six main categories are commonly treated as unprotected in U.S. law: incitement, true threats, obscenity, defamation, certain national security prior restraints, and some cybercrime-linked conduct; cybercrime cooperation and statutes can complicate enforcement, especially for online and crossborder cases

Courts distinguish between abstract advocacy and direct calls to immediate unlawful conduct by asking whether the words were meant to and likely to cause immediate criminal behavior; timing and context matter, and a generalized call for violence at an unspecified future time will usually fail the imminence element Brandenburg v. Ohio

Online posts raise special issues for imminence because a single message can diffuse quickly while also lacking a clear proximate opportunity for audience action; judges therefore look closely at whether a post included tactical instructions, targeted audiences ready to act, or real-world conditions that made immediate action likely

True threats and online mens rea: reading threats in social media posts

What courts mean by a true threat

True threats are statements a reasonable person would interpret as a serious expression of intent to commit harm, and they may be prosecuted even if delivered as speech rather than action; courts balance objective interpretation with evidence of the speaker's intent or mental state Elonis v. United States

Elonis and the role of the speaker's state of mind online

The Supreme Court in Elonis emphasized that mens rea matters when assessing whether a social media post is a punishable threat, requiring courts to consider the speaker's subjective intent and the surrounding online context rather than relying solely on an objective reading of the words Elonis v. United States (Harvard Law Review; Justia)

Because online posts can be sarcastic, hyperbolic, or part of a broader performance, investigators and judges examine the account history, the relationship between speaker and target, and any corroborating behavior before treating a post as an unprotected true threat

Obscenity and the Miller test

Obscenity and the Miller test

The three Miller prongs

Material is obscene under the Miller test if, taken as a whole, it (1) appeals to the prurient interest under local community standards, (2) depicts sexual conduct in a patently offensive way as defined by state law, and (3) lacks serious literary, artistic, political, or scientific value Miller v. California

Community standards vary by jurisdiction, so what counts as obscene in one place may not be obscene in another; courts also emphasize that much sexual content remains protected because the Miller test is narrowly drawn

Obscenity rules focus on narrow categories of material and do not apply to all sexual expression, so enforcement typically targets content that clearly meets all three Miller prongs rather than broadly restricting adult expression

Defamation and the actual malice standard for public figures

New York Times v. Sullivan explained

When public officials or public figures bring defamation claims, the Supreme Court requires proof of actual malice, meaning the plaintiff must show the defendant published a false statement knowing it was false or with reckless disregard for the truth New York Times Co. v. Sullivan

This higher burden distinguishes public figures from private individuals and protects robust public debate, while leaving room for private plaintiffs to seek remedies under more lenient standards in appropriate cases

National security, classified information, and prior restraint

Prior restraint doctrine and the Pentagon Papers case

Government attempts to impose prior restraint on publication face strict scrutiny because the Court has limited prior restraints in the Pentagon Papers context, making prepublication censorship rare and legally difficult to justify New York Times Co. v. United States

The distinction between prepublication restraint and after-the-fact criminal liability matters: the Court has been reluctant to authorize blanket prior restraints, but government secrecy statutes can still lead to prosecutions after disclosure in narrow circumstances

Where cybercrime law intersects free expression

The Budapest Convention and crossborder enforcement

The Budapest Convention provides the main framework for international cooperation against cybercrime, and while it targets activities like hacking and unlawful access, its coordination role affects how domestic authorities pursue online conduct that can touch expressive activity Budapest Convention on Cybercrime

Examples of conduct criminalized online and free expression concerns

Domestic cybercrime statutes often criminalize actions such as computer intrusion, distribution of malware, and coordinated campaigns that facilitate harassment or threats, and those statutes can overlap with speech protections when the conduct is closely tied to disclosure or political activity Budapest Convention on Cybercrime

Courts and policymakers are actively grappling with how to treat disclosures by journalists or activists that rely on hacked material, and jurisdictions vary in how broadly they apply cybercrime offenses to politically sensitive online activity

Typical pitfalls, decision criteria, and what judges look for

Common mistakes in applying the tests

Common analytical errors include treating advocacy of ideas as incitement, overlooking mens rea when assessing online threats, and allowing cybercrime statutes to be applied so broadly that they sweep in legitimate journalistic or political speech Elonis v. United States (see Cato Institute brief)

Practical questions judges evaluate: intent, context, and harm

Judges typically assess speaker intent or mens rea, the imminence of any harm, the objective reasonableness of an interpretation, and any balancing of content value, keeping in mind jurisdictional rules and the specific statutory language that may apply in cybercrime cases Brandenburg v. Ohio

Outcomes depend heavily on factual context, including account histories, the presence of coordinating action, and local community standards when obscenity is raised

Practical examples, further reading, and conclusion

Short, sourced examples tying tests to real situations

As an illustration, a post that gives detailed instructions and identifies a location for immediate violence is likely to meet the Brandenburg incitement test, because the test requires intent and likelihood of imminent lawless action Brandenburg v. Ohio

By contrast, an angry or hyperbolic social media message without clear intent to carry out harm will often be assessed under true threats doctrine with attention to mens rea, as the Elonis decision highlights Elonis v. United States
