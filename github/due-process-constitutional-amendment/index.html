What the due process constitutional right is

Plain-language definition

The phrase due process constitutional amendment describes the constitutional basis for protections that limit government power at both the federal and state level. The text of the Constitution shows where the clauses appear and how they anchor later judicial interpretation, and readers can consult the original text for verification Constitution of the United States transcription.

In everyday terms, due process means the government must follow legal rules before depriving a person of life, liberty, or property. That general idea breaks into two legal categories, procedural protections about notice and opportunity to be heard, and substantive protections that bar certain types of government interference, as legal summaries explain Legal Information Institute explanation of due process.

The Fifth Amendment constrains federal action while the Fourteenth Amendment applies a related limitation to state government conduct; together these clauses form the constitutional basis for both procedural and substantive due process claims. Due process protections affect everyday government programs and serious civil liberties alike. Procedural rules determine the process a person gets before a government agency withdraws benefits or property, while substantive rules can limit laws that intrude on key personal decisions. At its core, procedural due process requires notice and a meaningful opportunity to be heard before the government deprives a person of life, liberty, or property. This baseline principle underlies many contexts where administrative action threatens interests such as public benefits or licensed privileges, and the Supreme Court has articulated these protections in its opinions Legal Information Institute explanation of due process.

When courts decide exactly what process is due in administrative or noncriminal settings, they commonly apply the Mathews v. Eldridge three-factor balancing test. The test directs judges to weigh the private interest affected, the risk of an erroneous deprivation and the probable value of additional safeguards, and the government interest including the burdens additional procedures would impose, as the decision explains Mathews v. Eldridge decision text.

Applied step by step, a court first identifies the individual's private interest such as continued receipt of a benefit, then evaluates how the current procedures might produce error, and finally considers whether the cost of more process is justified by the need to reduce error. These stages guide judges in tailoring remedies or procedures to the factual context rather than prescribing a single approach for all cases Mathews v. Eldridge decision text.

For example, where a program provides ongoing benefits that a person depends on, courts often place greater weight on the private interest and the risk of erroneous deprivation; where the government faces large administrative burdens, that factor can limit the scope of added procedures. The balancing test is flexible and fact dependent, which is why litigants frame their pleadings to show how each Mathews factor favors their requested procedures Mathews v. Eldridge decision text.

Goldberg v. Kelly and procedural protections in administrative contexts

The Supreme Court in Goldberg v. Kelly emphasized that certain administrative terminations require procedural safeguards such as timely notice and an opportunity to be heard, particularly when government action threatens essential benefits. That opinion remains a touchstone for claims involving welfare or other ongoing entitlements Goldberg v. Kelly decision text.

Court practice shows that Goldberg’s protections are most squarely invoked when a benefit is critical to daily life and the consequences of error are significant. Practitioners therefore highlight Goldberg in pleadings that seek an evidentiary hearing or preliminary relief where loss of benefits would cause urgent hardship Goldberg v. Kelly decision text.

Substantive due process: what kinds of rights are protected

The concept of fundamental rights and tests from the Court

Substantive due process protects certain fundamental rights from government interference by asking whether a claimed right is sufficiently fundamental or deeply rooted in the nation's legal tradition. Courts apply tests that focus on the nature of the asserted right and the historical and legal traditions that support it, with Supreme Court precedent guiding the analysis Legal Information Institute explanation of due process.

Washington v. Glucksberg and the deeply rooted standard

The Supreme Court in Washington v. Glucksberg articulated a narrow method for identifying fundamental rights, asking whether the right is objectively deeply rooted in American history and tradition and whether it is implicit in the concept of ordered liberty. That decision provides the controlling approach in many substantive-due-process challenges to novel rights claims Washington v. Glucksberg decision text.

Under Glucksberg, claimants seeking recognition of a new substantive right face a rigorous historical and legal inquiry. Courts require persuasive evidence that a right is fundamental before applying heightened scrutiny to government action, and Glucksberg thus limits expansive readings of substantive due process in many cases Washington v. Glucksberg decision text.

Obergefell v. Hodges and application to later decisions

The Court’s decision in Obergefell v. Hodges applied substantive-due-process reasoning in the context of marriage, recognizing that certain personal decisions are protected against state intrusion. The opinion illustrates how courts may analyze liberty interests and how later decisions situate those interests within the Glucksberg framework and related precedents Obergefell v. Hodges decision text.

At the same time, the line between permissible legislation and unconstitutional interference remains a central question in many cases. Courts emphasize that not every law affecting autonomy or property is a substantive-due-process violation; claimants must show that the asserted right is fundamental or that the government action is arbitrary or lacks a legitimate state interest, consistent with controlling precedent Legal Information Institute explanation of due process.

How to raise a due process claim: practical steps for practitioners and litigants

Practitioners typically proceed through a series of concrete steps when preparing a due process claim: identify the specific liberty or property interest, describe the government action or deprivation at issue, and tie the facts to the relevant procedural or substantive tests in controlling precedent. Legal summaries and Supreme Court decisions provide the framework for drafting pleadings and motions Legal Information Institute explanation of due process.

Step 1, identifying the protected interest, requires clear factual description of what the plaintiff stands to lose. A liberty interest may include certain personal choices and bodily integrity claims, while a property interest can arise from statutory entitlements or established benefit schemes. Pleadings should cite the governing statute or regulation that creates the entitlement and explain the factual basis for the interest claimed Legal Information Institute explanation of due process.

Common errors and pitfalls when asserting due process claims

A frequent error is misidentifying the protected interest. Claimants sometimes label routine economic regulation as a fundamental liberty interest without showing a historical or legal basis, which courts treat skeptically under the Glucksberg approach Washington v. Glucksberg decision text.

Another pitfall is overreliance on analogy to high-profile cases without citing controlling precedent in the jurisdiction. Decisions from other circuits may be persuasive but are not binding, so practitioners should prioritize recent district and circuit rulings that directly address similar factual patterns Legal Information Institute explanation of due process.

Procedural mistakes include failing to plead a property or liberty interest clearly or omitting allegations that show state action. Courts can dismiss claims at the pleading stage when the complaint does not connect the facts to a recognized interest or when it lacks a clear statement of the government conduct challenged Legal Information Institute explanation of due process.
