E-Verify explained: what the system is and the main case outcomes

E-Verify explained begins with its basic purpose: it is a DHS and USCIS electronic system that compares identity and work-authorization information an employee provides against federal records to return a case result, according to USCIS USCIS Tentative Nonconfirmation page.

When an employer runs a case, E-Verify can return several standard outcomes. Employment Authorized means the system matched records and shows the person is permitted to work. A Tentative Nonconfirmation, or TNC, means the federal records did not match the information entered. A case that moves from a TNC to a Final Nonconfirmation indicates the agency confirmed a mismatch after the contest process, per USCIS materials USCIS E-Verify data.

The practical effect of these outcomes differs. Employment Authorized typically requires no further action by the employee. A TNC triggers a required worker notification and a specific contest process. Final Nonconfirmation may end the E-Verify process and can affect the employment relationship if the result stands, as defined in agency guidance USCIS Tentative Nonconfirmation page.

How E-Verify works step by step, including the TNC workflow

Start with the data employers enter. Typical data points include the employee's name, Social Security Number, date of birth, and other identity elements that match what the employee provided on Form I-9. USCIS states the system compares those entries to federal records to create a case USCIS Tentative Nonconfirmation page.

E-Verify runs automated checks when the employer submits the information. Results are available quickly and can be Employment Authorized, a TNC, or other outcomes depending on the match status with federal data USCIS E-Verify data.

You must receive a written referral notice, you have a limited number of federal working days to contact the agency that produced the mismatch, and your employer may not take adverse action while you contest the result.

If the system cannot match key elements automatically, it will generate a Tentative Nonconfirmation. A TNC means the federal database did not confirm the employee's provided data. USCIS explains that a TNC is not a final finding against the worker and triggers a defined contesting process and employer notification duties USCIS Tentative Nonconfirmation page.

After a TNC, the employer must provide the employee with a written referral notice that explains the next steps, including the agency to contact and the timeline to begin the contest. The employee then has the prescribed number of federal government working days to contact the resolving agency and initiate the contest process USCIS Tentative Nonconfirmation page.

Employee rights and timelines after a Tentative Nonconfirmation

When a TNC occurs, USCIS requires employers to give a written referral notice explaining the mismatch and the contest steps, according to agency guidance USCIS Tentative Nonconfirmation page.

The notice must include the identity of the agency that produced the mismatch and instructions for contacting that agency within the specific federal government working days allowed to begin the contest. Employees should follow those instructions carefully and note deadlines in the referral materials USCIS Tentative Nonconfirmation page.

Federal rules and USCIS guidance prohibit employers from taking adverse action while the employee is contesting a TNC. Civil-rights groups also emphasize this protection and provide practical advice for workers who receive a TNC ACLU guidance on TNC rights.

Practically, that means employers should not fire, suspend, or otherwise discipline an employee solely because they received a TNC while the contest is pending. If an employer does take adverse action in that window, the employee may have legal recourse and should seek advice from a civil-rights organization or counsel ACLU guidance on TNC rights.

Why tentative nonconfirmations and errors happen: common causes and what oversight finds

Tentative nonconfirmations and apparent errors commonly stem from mismatches between what an employee provides and what is in federal records. Typical causes include name and SSN mismatches, data-entry mistakes, and older government records that have not been updated Migration Policy Institute overview.

Employer responsibilities and recommended practices when a TNC occurs

Under USCIS procedures, employers must provide the required referral notice and cannot take adverse action while a TNC is being contested, according to agency guidance USCIS Tentative Nonconfirmation page.

Recommended practical steps include documenting the referral, giving the employee contest instructions, tracking case updates in E-Verify, and preserving related records for possible appeal or audit. These measures are suggested in USCIS materials and in oversight guidance as sound compliance practices USCIS E-Verify data.

Employers should maintain clear records of when they provided notices and what instructions were given. If an employer sees repeated mismatches for the same individual, or if discrimination is alleged, consulting legal counsel is appropriate to manage compliance risk and potential enforcement exposure GAO review of E-Verify oversight.

Practical checklist: what employees should do if they receive a TNC

Read the referral notice right away and note the agency named for resolving the mismatch. The notice includes specific steps and the federal government working days you have to contact the agency and start the contest process USCIS Tentative Nonconfirmation page.

Gather copies of identity documents that match what you provided on Form I-9, such as Social Security records or identity documents that the resolving agency lists as acceptable. Keep copies of every page of correspondence and record dates and times of calls or visits ACLU guidance on TNC rights.

You are protected from adverse employer action while you are contesting a TNC. If you encounter employer pressure or discipline during the contest window, contact a civil-rights organization or legal counsel promptly for guidance ACLU guidance on TNC rights.

Common mistakes, legal risks, and enforcement issues to avoid

Employers sometimes make procedural mistakes that increase legal risk, such as failing to provide the written referral notice or taking adverse action while a contest is pending. These errors can lead to discrimination claims, as oversight bodies have highlighted GAO review of E-Verify oversight.

Keep records for potential audits

Employees can slow resolution by missing the agency contact deadline or by not keeping copies of documents. Simple steps like recording the referral notice date and maintaining a correspondence log reduce that risk and support a faster contest outcome USCIS Tentative Nonconfirmation page.

Oversight reports flag program-level risks when tracking and accuracy systems are weak. Those program risks translate to higher compliance exposure for employers and uncertainty for workers, which is why following process and preserving records matters in practice GAO review of E-Verify oversight.

Scenario examples and sample scripts for responding to a TNC

Employee scenario: Maria receives a referral notice showing a TNC. She reads the notice, notes the agency to contact, gathers her Social Security card and passport copy, and calls the listed agency within the working days allowed. She keeps a log of the call and any confirmation number the agency provides.

Employer scenario: A manager receives notice that an employee has a TNC. The manager provides the written referral notice immediately, records the delivery method and date, refrains from any discipline while the contest is pending, and updates the internal case log with the E-Verify case number.

Sample employee language when contacting the agency: "Hello, I received a referral notice from my employer through E-Verify. My name is [first and last name], and I would like to begin the process to resolve the Tentative Nonconfirmation. My E-Verify case number is [case number]." Sample employer documentation line: "Referral provided to employee on [date]; employee instructed to contact [agency name] within the working days noted. No adverse action taken during contest."
