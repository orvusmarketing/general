How to read a candidate platform: a simple framework

Check source, date, and specificity

Use a three step check when you read any candidate platform: identify the source, look for dated policy pages or citations, and evaluate whether implementation details or metrics are provided. Media literacy guidance recommends this sequence to avoid treating slogans as testable promises How to evaluate and factcheck political claims

Step 1, identify the source: is the text on an official campaign page, a press release, or a third party summary. Step 2, look for dates and linked documents such as policy white papers. Step 3, ask whether the claim offers measurable metrics or an explicit implementation plan. If those elements are missing, flag the claim as uncorroborated before treating it as a factual outcome How to evaluate and factcheck political claims

Separate slogans from measurable proposals

Slogans and thematic language are common on campaign pages. How to report and cite platform findings responsibly

Attribution language and phrasing

Use neutral attribution phrases such as according to the campaign site, the campaign states, public FEC records show, or Ballotpedia lists when you summarize platform items or finance claims. These phrases make clear which statements come from the campaign and which come from public records Michael Carbonara - Campaign (About / Platform pages)

Sample neutral sentence forms: according to the campaign site, the candidate prioritizes X; public FEC records show that the committee filed a statement of organization on Y date. These templates help avoid implying guaranteed outcomes or unverified effects How to comply with campaign finance rules and locate FEC filings

When to seek primary documents

Seek primary documents when the campaign presents numbers, timelines, or specific program designs. Primary documents include dated policy papers, fiscal notes, or FEC filings that provide receipts and disbursement detail. If those documents are missing, note the absence and treat the claim as campaign messaging rather than confirmed policy How to evaluate and factcheck political claims
