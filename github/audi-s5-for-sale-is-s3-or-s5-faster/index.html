Quick answer: which is faster in straight line performance?

Short answer, based on factory powertrains and published road tests: the S5 is usually the faster car in straight-line acceleration, though the gap is often smaller than factory horsepower numbers imply when you compare actual tests and different transmissions. What the Audi S3 and S5 are: definitions and key spec differences

The mechanical baseline explains much of the difference in straight-line speed. Audi lists the S3 with a turbocharged 2.0-L inline-4 and the S5 with a turbocharged 3.0-L V6 on its model pages, so the S5 starts with a clear engine advantage on paper Audi USA S3 page

Published peak horsepower and torque figures are directionally higher for the S5, and that higher torque, especially in the midrange, tends to help passing and real-world acceleration even if peak numbers do not tell the whole story Audi USA S5 page

Curb weight and chassis differences matter too. Manufacturer specs and reviews consistently list the S5 as the heavier car, which reduces some of the theoretical advantage from extra power and changes handling balance Audi USA S5 page

Top speed on many factory S3 and S5 models is electronically limited, and road tests note where optional performance packages or tuning change factory limits; this is a separate consideration from 0-60 performance and depends on model and packaging MotorTrend S5 review

Factory numbers versus real tests: acceleration and 0-60 comparisons

Factory 0-60 estimates and independent test runs do not always match. Factory specs provide a performance baseline, but independent measurements from road testers often use different instruments and launch techniques, so times can spread between sources Car and Driver S3 review

Independent road tests for recent model years generally show the S5 equals or slightly outpaces the S3 in straight-line acceleration under comparable conditions, though exact rankings can flip depending on transmission and testing method MotorTrend S5 review

The Audi S5 generally has a straight-line advantage over the S3 due to a larger V6 and higher torque, but transmission, curb weight and testing method can narrow or eliminate that advantage for specific cars, so verify the listing details and history report.

Testing method matters: launch control, tire condition, fuel load and even ambient elevation change the numbers reported by magazines and testing outlets, so a quoted 0-60 time should be read as context rather than a fixed property of a specific used car.

When comparing quoted 0-60 times on listings or in reviews, check whether the source used a manual start, two-test averages, or a GPS-based device; those details explain most of the spread between published figures and help you judge how meaningful a time is for a particular listing Car and Driver S3 review

Why the S5's extra power does not always mean it wins on every run

Power-to-weight is the key math. The S5’s larger V6 delivers more torque and horsepower on paper, but its curb weight is consistently higher than the S3, which moderates a straight-line advantage in some conditions Audi USA S5 page

Drivetrain and transmission tuning influence how that available power reaches the road. Different quattro calibrations and gearbox mapping change launch behavior and midrange response, so two similarly specified cars can feel different when accelerating MotorTrend S5 review

Higher power often comes with trade-offs in fuel economy and agility. The S3’s smaller engine tends to be more efficient and can feel more nimble in urban driving, while the S5 leans toward comfort and stronger midrange passing power, which matters for highway merging and overtakes Audi USA S3 page

These trade-offs mean the S5’s straight-line edge is most apparent in sustained midrange acceleration and higher-load passing, less so in short launches where weight and traction control behavior dominate the result MotorTrend S5 review
