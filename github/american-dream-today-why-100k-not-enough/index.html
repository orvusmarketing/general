What we mean by the 'American Dream' and why $100,000 is the question

Defining the American Dream in practical terms (american dream today)

For the purposes of this article, the american dream today is treated as a practical household standard rather than a slogan. That working definition includes secure housing, the ability to pay routine family expenses, access to health coverage, and a modest cushion in savings to withstand shocks.

Using a nominal $100,000 pre-tax salary as a benchmark is common because it sits near the middle to upper middle of U.S. household earnings and is easy to compare across regions. That benchmark does not automatically equal take-home pay, because taxes, payroll contributions, and employer benefits change the amount of cash a household actually controls.

Real affordability depends heavily on household size, local prices, insurance status, and outstanding debt. Readers should treat the $100,000 figure as a starting point for comparison rather than a universal threshold; regional price levels and household circumstances will change the conclusion. For regional context, consult BEA regional price parity data for your state or metro to see how far a given nominal salary goes locally BEA regional price parity data, and see our American Prosperity page for related analysis.

Why analysts use a nominal salary test

Analysts use a nominal salary test like $100,000 to create a simple, common yardstick for comparing living standards. The test is useful only if readers then convert nominal pay into disposable income and adjust for local prices and household needs.

That conversion is not automatic. Two households with the same gross pay can have very different take-home resources because of taxes, benefits, and local cost differences that we examine below.

From nominal salary to disposable income: taxes, payroll, and benefits

How federal and payroll taxes reduce take-home pay

Federal and payroll taxes lower gross pay into much smaller take-home pay numbers, and effective tax burdens vary by filing status, deductions, and credits. Analysts recommend treating taxes as a first-step deduction when modeling a $100,000 salary so readers do not overestimate available cash.

Tax Policy Center analyses show that effective tax rates and distributional effects matter when converting a nominal salary into disposable income and that those rates vary by household situation Tax Policy Center analysis of effective tax rates.

The role of employer benefits and out-of-pocket costs

Employer-provided benefits change household budgets in two ways. On one hand, employer coverage can reduce a household's direct spending on insurance. On the other hand, premiums, deductibles, and copays still impose recurring costs and can leave families exposed to large bills.

Kaiser Family Foundation reports that employer premiums and cost-sharing have continued to grow, so even households with workplace coverage may face significant out-of-pocket health expenses that reduce disposable income KFF employer health benefits findings.

Regional price differences: why $100,000 buys differently across the U.S.

BEA regional price parities explained

Regional Price Parities measure differences in price levels across states and metro areas for a comparable basket of goods and services, and they show that a given nominal income can purchase more in some places than others. That measurement is central to moving from a national salary benchmark to a local affordability judgment.

Because local price levels change the purchasing power of identical paychecks, many analysts first scale a nominal salary by the BEA index for the relevant area before building a household budget.

For local price scaling, check BEA metro tables and local housing listings to set realistic assumptions and review Bankrate's salary-by-city study for comparative metro results Bankrate salary-by-city data.

How to read metro versus state differences

A state average can mask sharp differences between metros and nonmetro counties. For example, some high-cost metros have price levels well above their state averages, which means a $100,000 salary will stretch less in those metros than in lower-cost areas within the same state.

Readers should use the BEA interactive tables and metro-level data to see local results rather than relying solely on state or national summaries BEA regional price parity data.

Housing: the largest single driver of affordability pressure

Why housing costs outpace typical incomes in many markets

Across many U.S. markets, housing costs have risen faster than typical incomes, which makes housing the principal driver of affordability shortfalls for middle-income households. Rising prices squeeze the share of income available for other needs.

The Joint Center for Housing Studies reports that housing costs have generally outpaced income growth in numerous markets and that affordability pressures remain persistent, especially where supply has not kept up with demand Harvard JCHS State of the Nation's Housing 2024.

How housing type and commuting trade-offs change budgets

Renting versus owning presents different cost profiles. Renters face rising monthly rents and potential rent increases. Owners carry mortgage principal and interest, property taxes, insurance, and maintenance, all of which vary by location and housing size.

Many households choose smaller housing or longer commutes to contain costs, but those trade-offs often increase transportation spending or reduce time for work and family, so they are not free solutions.

Health care costs and unexpected medical bills

Employer premiums versus out-of-pocket spending

Health coverage through work reduces some financial risk, but it does not eliminate the budget impact. Premiums taken from paychecks, deductibles, and copays are recurring items that reduce take-home resources and household resilience to shocks.

KFF documents continued growth in employer premiums and cost-sharing, which means that insured households still face significant regular health costs that should be counted when judging whether $100,000 is sufficient KFF employer health benefits findings.

Because taxes, payroll deductions, employer benefits, regional price levels, housing, healthcare, childcare, debt, and savings together determine disposable income and resilience; these factors vary widely by metro and household, so identical nominal salaries can produce very different outcomes.

How health costs change affordability even with employer coverage

Large deductibles or unexpected bills from an illness can quickly consume emergency savings and force difficult budget cuts. Because insurance designs vary, household exposure to out-of-pocket risk differs substantially even among those with employer benefits.

Readers should factor their specific plan's deductible and typical out-of-pocket spending into any affordability check rather than assuming employer coverage makes health costs negligible.

Child care and family-related costs that change the math

Center-based care costs and variation by state

Child care is a large and variable expense that often consumes a sizable share of household budgets, especially for families with infants and toddlers. Costs depend strongly on state and local markets and the type of care chosen.

Child Care Aware's state-level research shows that center-based infant and toddler care costs vary widely and can represent a substantial budget item for many families, so households with young children should model these costs explicitly Child Care Aware state profiles.

How household composition amplifies affordability gaps

Single-earner households face very different calculations from two-earner families. A second adult who can work for pay reduces childcare spending needs but increases other costs and scheduling complexity.

Because childcare costs often arrive monthly and over many years, they can tilt the affordability judgment for a $100,000 salary from manageable to strained in many metros.

Debt, savings shortfalls, and economic shocks: the fragility factor

Non-mortgage debt and monthly service costs

Monthly debt service for student loans, auto loans, credit cards, and other unsecured debt reduces monthly flexibility and can make an otherwise adequate salary feel tight. Many households carry non-mortgage debt that must be included in a realistic budget.

The Federal Reserve's report on household economic well-being finds that many families have ongoing debt obligations that lower disposable income and increase vulnerability to income changes Federal Reserve economic well-being report.

Emergency savings and resilience to shocks

Limited emergency savings mean that a single unexpected expense can require borrowing or selling assets, which can be costly. An adequate buffer helps preserve housing and avoid high-interest debt during temporary shocks.

Because savings levels differ widely, two households with the same income can have very different resilience. Readers should factor liquid reserves into any assessment of whether $100,000 is sufficient for their household.

Concrete household scenarios: how $100,000 plays out in different metros

High-cost metro example

Consider an illustrative single-family household in a high-cost metro where local rents and home prices are well above the national average. After payroll and federal taxes, higher housing costs, and employer health premiums, the share of take-home pay left for savings, childcare, and discretionary spending can be limited.

Because high-cost metros raise housing and many local services, a $100,000 nominal salary often buys less in these areas than the same income would in lower-cost metros. For local price scaling, check BEA metro tables and local housing listings to set realistic assumptions BEA regional price parity data and consult consumer-facing comparisons such as the ConsumerAffairs overview of where $100K stretches further Where a $100K Salary Stretches the Furthest.

Low-cost metro example

In contrast, a household in a lower-cost metro can often secure comparable housing and services for a smaller share of gross pay. Lower rents reduce the housing share and free up take-home pay for savings or other needs.

These examples are illustrative not predictive. Exact outcomes depend on household size, housing choice, and the specifics of local markets and benefits.

Suburban family scenario

A suburban two-parent household with two young children may face large childcare bills in addition to mortgage and commuting costs. Even with two earners, combined tax and payroll burdens, health premiums, and childcare can substantially reduce what feels like discretionary income.

Child care assumptions should be drawn from state profiles such as those compiled by Child Care Aware and then scaled to local price levels using BEA data to reflect realistic family budgets Child Care Aware state profiles.

How to decide for your household: a simple affordability checklist

Key questions to answer about your budget

Start by listing your gross salary, tax filing status, estimated payroll and federal taxes, and employer benefits cost. Then add realistic housing estimates, health premiums and likely out-of-pocket health spending, childcare, debt payments, and a target for emergency savings.

Use the BEA RPP for your metro to scale national cost assumptions and consult primary sources for housing, health, and childcare costs to populate the checklist BEA regional price parity data. For local policy context, see our Affordable Healthcare page.

Red flags that $100,000 is unlikely to be sufficient

Red flags include housing costs above 30 or 35 percent of take-home pay, high childcare bills, large deductibles or frequent medical expenses, and monthly debt service that leaves minimal discretionary cash. If multiple red flags apply, the $100,000 benchmark is less likely to meet your needs.

These rules of thumb are starting points, not strict rules; precise judgment requires modeling your own numbers and assumptions.

Common mistakes people make when judging salary adequacy

Ignoring regional differences

A common error is to use national averages or a single-city example to assess local affordability. National numbers can mislead because they hide wide price variation across metros and counties.

Consulting local BEA, housing, and childcare data helps correct this mistake and produces a clearer picture of what a given salary actually buys in your area.

Overlooking regular non-housing costs

People often focus on mortgage or rent and overlook ongoing costs such as healthcare premiums, deductibles, childcare, taxes, and regular debt service. Those items can materially change a household's financial picture over time.

A final frequent misstep is assuming a single year's salary will protect against future shocks when savings or debt position make the household fragile.

What researchers and public reports say - brief policy context

Synthesis of the major reports

Public reports converge on the same broad point: regional price differences, housing costs, healthcare, childcare, taxes, and savings levels jointly determine whether a given nominal salary meets household needs. Each evidence source emphasizes a different primary driver.

BEA shows regional price variation, Harvard JCHS emphasizes housing cost trends, KFF documents health cost trends, Child Care Aware highlights childcare burdens, the Federal Reserve documents savings shortfalls, and the Tax Policy Center frames tax burdens in the conversion to disposable income Harvard JCHS State of the Nation's Housing 2024.

What remains uncertain and why

Uncertainty arises from household heterogeneity and changing local markets. Policy changes, labour market shifts, and local supply responses can alter affordability patterns, so up-to-date local data are essential to any assessment.

Readers should consult the original reports and local data sources for detailed tables and state or metro profiles before drawing firm conclusions.

Practical steps readers can take right now

Immediate budgeting and checklist actions

Model your take-home pay by accounting for federal and payroll taxes and subtracting employer health premiums. Then compare realistic local housing estimates and potential childcare costs to that adjusted figure.

Increase emergency savings where possible and review outstanding debt to lower monthly service costs. These resilience measures improve how well a salary handles shocks and recurring needs.

How to model your own household budget using primary sources

Data inputs to collect

Gather gross salary, tax filing status, expected payroll deductions, housing cost estimates, employer health premium amounts, likely out-of-pocket health spending, childcare estimates, current debt payments, and a savings target. Label each assumption clearly.

Where possible, use BEA RPPs to scale national estimates to local price levels and consult JCHS, KFF, and Child Care Aware for sector-specific assumptions and context Harvard JCHS State of the Nation's Housing 2024.

A simple step-by-step modeling approach

Step 1: Convert gross pay to estimated take-home pay after payroll and federal taxes. Step 2: Subtract employer premiums and estimated out-of-pocket health expenses. Step 3: Subtract realistic housing costs adjusted by local RPP. Step 4: Subtract childcare and debt service. Step 5: Compare remaining cash to a savings and discretionary spending target.

Run a few alternative assumptions for housing, childcare, and health exposure to see which variables most affect the outcome.

Conclusion: what 'american dream today' means for different households

Core takeaways

The american dream today varies by household and place. Regional price parity, housing costs, healthcare, childcare, taxes, and debt jointly determine whether a $100,000 salary meets a household's needs.

Readers should model their own situation using primary sources, label assumptions explicitly, and treat results as illustrative inputs for local decisions and conversations with candidates and planners.
