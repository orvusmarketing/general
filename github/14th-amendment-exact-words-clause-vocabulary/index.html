Citizenship Clause: birthright citizenship and the wording to watch

Text excerpts and plain-language meaning

The Citizenship Clause states that all persons born or naturalized in the United States are citizens of the United States and of the state wherein they reside, language that established birthright citizenship and discredited the Dred Scott-era doctrine in constitutional terms, as the ratified text records and scholars note National Archives and National Archives milestone.

Plainly, the clause's key vocabulary-phrases like "born or naturalized" and "citizens of the United States and of the state wherein they reside"-is what courts examine when deciding questions about national and state citizenship status, making an accurate quotation important for legal discussion Constitution Annotated.

How courts have treated the clause

Courts treat the Citizenship Clause as the constitutional basis for birthright citizenship, and litigants who argue about citizenship outcomes will point to the exact wording in primary texts and to annotated histories for context Cornell LII.

Privileges or Immunities Clause: history, the Slaughter-House ruling, and its narrowed role

Original wording and intended scope

The Privileges or Immunities Clause appears near the start of the amendment and names protections for certain rights of citizens; the clause's original placement among the amendment's operative provisions matters to how scholars and courts read its intended scope Constitution Annotated.

The Slaughter-House Cases and doctrinal narrowing

The Supreme Court's decision in the Slaughter-House Cases of 1873 significantly narrowed the clause's practical role in constitutional doctrine, a historical turn that has left the clause less central in most modern opinions and that scholars continue to evaluate Slaughter-House Cases opinion.

That narrowing means that commentators and litigants often rely on other amendment provisions for doctrinal work, while academic debate persists about whether the Privileges or Immunities Clause could be revived as a broader interpretive tool in the future Constitution Annotated.

Due Process Clause: procedural protections and substantive rights

Difference between procedural and substantive due process

The Due Process Clause contains language that courts have read to guarantee procedural protections, such as notice and a fair hearing, as well as substantive protections that the Court has sometimes recognized as fundamental rights; incorporation of many Bill of Rights protections against the states has proceeded through Due Process Clause doctrine in modern jurisprudence Constitution Annotated and Due Process annotation.

Equal Protection Clause: wording, segregation rulings, and modern applications

Key textual phrases to note

The Equal Protection Clause forbids states from denying any person within their jurisdiction the equal protection of the laws, a short operative phrase that underpins a wide range of constitutional litigation about classifications and discrimination and that is quoted directly in judicial opinions Constitution Annotated.

Landmark cases and their textual reliance

Brown v. Board of Education used the Constitution's equal-protection framework to address state-sponsored racial segregation; the opinion relied on the amendment's wording to frame why state practices of segregation were constitutionally problematic in public education contexts Brown v. Typical errors and citation pitfalls when quoting the amendment

Misquotations and out-of-context paraphrases

A common error is paraphrasing key phrases in ways that change their legal meaning, for example omitting "born or naturalized" or compressing clause boundaries; always quote the primary transcription when the precise wording matters and cite the source National Archives.

Another pitfall is taking an excerpt out of its clause context. Clause connections matter because courts read the amendment as an integrated text, and omission of surrounding language can mislead readers about the amendment's scope Constitution Annotated.
