Safety: crash tests and equipment differences

The Lexus RX generally scores strongly in IIHS evaluations while Audi Q5 results can vary by model year and equipment; check the model year safety pages for the specific car you are considering IIHS vehicle ratings for Lexus RX.

Safety ratings depend on more than brand. Advanced driver assistance packages, optional airbags, and structural revisions differ by trim and year, and those options can change an IIHS score or influence NHTSA results for a particular model.

When a listing claims advanced safety systems, confirm the exact package and whether sensors or cameras were repaired after any reported damage. A repair that was imperfect can affect the operation of lane keeping or automatic braking systems.

How safety ratings vary by model year and equipment

Optional safety packages, headlights and active crash mitigation options commonly change safety grades. For example, a higher trim or an option package that adds automatic braking or upgraded headlights can move a vehicle from a lower to a higher category in IIHS listings.

Always confirm the presence of the specific safety features listed by the test agency on the vehicle page. If the listing does not clearly list equipment, ask the dealer for the VIN and verify the factory build or the history report entries that document equipment and recalls.

What to check on the history report before you buy

On the history report, prioritize title status, any salvage or rebuilt labels, accident history that mentions airbag deployment or structural damage, and service records for safety system repairs. These entries are strong risk signals that change the value proposition for a used luxury SUV.

If the report shows a salvage title, or multiple owners with repeated accident entries, that should either disqualify the listing for some buyers or trigger a professional inspection focused on the affected systems.

Fuel economy and powertrain choices

EPA labels show many Q5 powertrains, particularly smaller engines or plug in variants, can offer higher combined mpg or MPGe compared with the non hybrid RX 350; check the EPA model pages for the exact trim and year when you compare running costs 2025 Audi Q5 fuel economy and see Edmunds' MPG data.

That said, the RX 350 non hybrid trims typically report lower combined mpg than comparable Q5 configurations. Hybrid or plug in options change that comparison and can produce notably different running costs for commuting or mixed use.

When you look at an individual listing, locate the EPA label or the EPA model page for that exact trim and powertrain. You can also consult US News performance summaries. Factor local fuel prices and driving patterns into any direct comparison. A vehicle with higher rated combined mpg can still cost more in your hands if local driving is heavy on city routes or if you regularly tow or carry heavy loads that change real world economy.

Hybrid and plug in variants: what changes for running costs

Hybrid and plug in variants alter running cost expectations by offering electric assist or limited all electric range. For example, a plug in model with a short electric range may reduce fuel use in daily commuting, but charging access and actual usage patterns determine the realized saving.

How to use fuel economy labels when comparing used trims

Verify the exact powertrain and the EPA combined rating. On a listing, confirm odometer mileage and service history that might indicate heavier than normal use. Use the stated combined mpg or MPGe as an input to estimate fuel costs, not as a guarantee of future consumption.

Check for modifications that can affect economy, such as aftermarket wheels or performance tuning, which may reduce rated efficiency and change maintenance needs.

How reliability affects maintenance and long term costs

Vehicles with stronger reliability scores often need fewer unscheduled repairs and can present lower ownership cost risk. That can also translate into lower expected downtime and fewer surprise expenses for families or professionals relying on the car for daily use.

However, maintenance history, previous ownership and how the vehicle was driven remain primary determinants of near term repair risk. A high rated brand vehicle with poor service records can still be risky.

What to look for in service history on a used listing

Look for regular oil changes, scheduled service items, documented timing system work if applicable, and repairs that address recurring faults. If the history shows deferred maintenance or conflicting entries, order a deeper inspection or request invoices from the dealer.

Confirm the mileage listed on the bill of sale against the odometer and the vehicle history record to spot rollback or tampering signals. A consistent ownership history and continuous service records are strong positive signals.

How trim, mileage and condition change value

Check for items that commonly affect perceived value such as interior wear, wheel curb damage, or mechanical issues reported in the history. These items are easy to price in and can be used to negotiate or to set an inspection threshold.

Common buying mistakes and how to avoid them

One common error is assuming all RX 350 or Q5 examples are the same across years and trims. That can lead to overlooking safety equipment differences, powertrain variations, or recalls specific to a year and trim.

Another frequent mistake is relying solely on price without checking the vehicle history report checklist or service records. A lower price may reflect hidden repair needs or title problems that raise total ownership cost.

Remedies include always checking the VIN report, asking for maintenance receipts, and comparing price analysis labels across nearby listings. Use dealer transparency signals to prioritize listings with clearer histories and documented service records.
