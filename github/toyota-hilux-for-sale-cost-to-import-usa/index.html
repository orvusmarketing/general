Why Toyota does not offer the Hilux as a regular new model in the United States

Regulatory and market context

Toyota's decision not to sell the Hilux new in the U.S. reflects market choices and regulatory costs, since any model sold here usually must meet U.S. federal safety and emissions standards that apply to the market. Meeting those requirements during design and production is a material consideration for manufacturers deciding which models to offer in a country. NHTSA importing guidance

How manufacturers decide model lineups

Manufacturers choose model lineups by balancing expected sales volume, compliance cost, and where a model fits in the local product range. When a vehicle like the Hilux is profitable in other global markets but would require additional certification work for the U.S., the maker may keep it off the regular U.S. new car roster. How importing a Toyota Hilux to the US actually works: a step by step overview

Buying overseas or buying an already-imported vehicle

There are two common purchase routes: buy overseas and arrange shipping and compliance yourself, or buy a Hilux that a dealer or specialist has already imported and titled in the U.S. Each route has tradeoffs: doing it yourself can save on middleman fees but raises legal risk and administrative work, while buying an already-imported listing often costs more upfront but simplifies title and registration. If you plan to buy an already-imported unit, verify title status and ask for the compliance paperwork and service history before you pay.

Shipping, Customs entry, and initial inspections

After purchase, the vehicle moves by ocean freight or air freight to a U.S. port, where Customs entry and initial inspections occur. You will typically budget shipping, marine insurance, port handling, and Customs entry fees at the arrival port. Customs documentation includes the bill of lading, purchase invoice, and import entry forms; missing or incomplete paperwork can delay release and add cost. CBP guidance on vehicle entry

Role of a Registered Importer

A Registered Importer, or RI, handles DOT and EPA compliance modifications and the certified paperwork needed to register a non-U.S. vehicle in most states. RIs can retrofit safety equipment, certify emissions components, and file the forms required by federal agencies so you can title the vehicle. Using an RI tends to increase out-of-pocket fees but lowers the legal and technical risk of noncompliance. NHTSA importing guidance

Detailed cost breakdown: purchase price, shipping, duties, compliance and registration

Typical landed cost components

The landed cost of an imported Hilux combines the vehicle purchase price with shipping and marine insurance, Customs duties including the 25 percent light-truck tariff, Registered Importer compliance and modification fees, and state taxes and registration charges. Expect that duties and compliance work can materially increase the final outlay compared with the original purchase price. How the 25 percent tariff fits into totals

Imported light trucks are generally subject to the 25 percent light-truck tariff, often called the Chicken Tax, which is applied to the dutiable value and substantially raises the landed price. That tariff is separate from Customs entry fees and potential additional duties that depend on classification under the Harmonized Tariff Schedule. Buyers should plan for the tariff to be a major line item in the import math. Harmonized Tariff Schedule reference

Examples of possible cost ranges by scenario

Exact numbers depend on the vehicle year, condition, origin port and the importer you use, but typical added costs include international shipping and insurance, the 25 percent duty, Registered Importer fees for DOT and EPA work, and state title taxes. Depending on those variables, added outlays commonly range from a few thousand dollars to over ten thousand dollars beyond the purchase price. Compliance, Registered Importers and paperwork you cannot skip

DOT and EPA requirements explained

Imported vehicles must meet U.S. Federal Motor Vehicle Safety Standards and EPA emissions rules or be altered and certified by a Registered Importer or other approved facility. Compliance covers components, safety equipment, and emissions controls; paperwork must document the changes and certification to the agencies. Handling these matters correctly is essential to obtain a clear title and register the vehicle in most states. NHTSA importing guidance

What a Registered Importer does and documentation to expect

A Registered Importer evaluates the vehicle, performs required modifications, tests emissions or safety systems as needed, prepares certification paperwork, and submits forms to DOT and EPA. The RI provides documents you will need to show at titling, such as compliance letters and installation records. Working with an RI generally reduces the risk of noncompliance but adds service and certification fees. EPA importing guidance

Tariffs, duties and Customs procedures that raise the landed price

Understanding the Chicken Tax and HTS classifications

The 25 percent light-truck tariff is typically applied to imported light trucks, so classifying a Hilux under the correct Harmonized Tariff Schedule heading is important because classifications determine duty rates and applicable rules. Customs classification and valuation rules also affect how much duty is owed on the declared value. For precise duty calculation you may want to consult CBP guidance or an import specialist. Harmonized Tariff Schedule reference

How Customs entry works and common fees to expect

Customs entry requires submitting the import entry, commercial invoice, bill of lading, and other supporting documents; expect processing fees, broker charges, and possible port handling costs. An import broker or specialist can prepare the entry and advise on duty classification, but broker fees add to the landed cost. For accurate figures, collect written quotes for brokerage and port costs before shipment. CBP guidance on vehicle entry

Should you buy an already-imported Hilux or import one yourself?

Tradeoffs: cost, risk, and convenience

Buying an already-imported, titled Hilux often reduces paperwork and the need to coordinate RIs and Customs directly, but dealers and specialists typically charge for the convenience and to cover compliance work; that raises the purchase price compared with a direct overseas buy. Conversely, importing yourself can cut service fees but increases the administrative burden and potential compliance risk if you do not use a Registered Importer for required certification. NHTSA importing guidance

Checklist to evaluate an already-imported listing

Confirm title status and any salvage or branding, check emissions documentation if applicable for your state, and request a clear invoice that lists taxes and import fees the seller paid.
