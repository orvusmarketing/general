What the Bill of Rights is and how it relates to the Constitution

Definition: the first ten amendments, pocket constitution and bill of rights

The phrase "Bill of Rights" refers to the first ten amendments to the U.S. Constitution, and the National Archives transcript shows these amendments as a single amended text once ratified, not a separate document National Archives transcript.

These ten amendments were proposed by Congress in 1789 and largely ratified by the states in 1791, and the Library of Congress overview sets out that timeline and the original language used in those amendments Library of Congress overview.

The Bill of Rights refers to the first ten amendments, and after ratification under the Article V process those amendments became part of the Constitution's text; later judicial decisions then applied many of those protections to state governments through the Fourteenth Amendment.

The practical effect of ratification is that the amendments become part of the Constitution's text; primary transcriptions treat them as additions to the constitutional document rather than a separate standing paper National Archives transcript.

How amendments are added: the Article V process

Proposal routes: Congress or a constitutional convention

The Constitution's Article V sets out the formal routes to amend the text: either by a two-thirds vote in both houses of Congress or by a convention called by two-thirds of state legislatures, followed by ratification by three-quarters of the states; the Library of Congress summarizes how Congress used its proposal power for the Bill of Rights Library of Congress overview.

In practice, the Bill of Rights followed the congressional proposal route: Congress proposed a series of amendments in 1789 and sent them to the states for ratification, a process described in contemporary records and later transcriptions Library of Congress overview.

Ratification by three-quarters of the states and the 1789 example

Article V requires ratification by three-quarters of state legislatures or state conventions before a proposed amendment becomes part of the Constitution; the historical record for the Bill of Rights shows state-by-state ratification steps that completed in 1791 and thus finalized the amendments as constitutional text National Archives transcript.

Describing the amendment process in steps helps readers see that adding text to the Constitution is a formal, multi-stage procedure rather than an informal editorial change, and primary sources document the dates and votes involved in the 1789 to 1791 sequence Library of Congress overview.

Why the Bill of Rights is legally part of the Constitution

Textual status: ratified amendments become constitutional law

Once a proposed amendment is ratified under Article V, it becomes part of the Constitution's text; the National Archives provides the primary transcription of the Bill of Rights as ratified amendments that now sit within the body of constitutional law National Archives transcript.

Saying the Bill of Rights is "part of the Constitution" describes that legal status: the ratified amendments have the same standing in the document as the original clauses, and readers can confirm this by consulting official transcriptions and government-published editions govinfo pocket edition examples.

Difference between a textual amendment and later judicial interpretation

Textual change through Article V is different from judicial interpretation: amendments alter the Constitution's written text, while courts interpret that text and shape how it applies to specific cases, a distinction shown in legal encyclopedias that explain the amendment process and later case law separately Cornell LII on incorporation.

That difference matters because the Bill of Rights became part of the Constitution by ratification, and how those provisions operate in practice can further evolve through judicial decisions without altering the text itself Cornell LII on incorporation.

How protections in the Bill of Rights reached state governments: selective incorporation

The Fourteenth Amendment as the vehicle for incorporation

When first adopted, the Bill of Rights constrained only the federal government; it did not directly bind state governments, a point standard legal references explain as the historical baseline before post-Civil War developments Cornell LII on incorporation. For related background on the Fourteenth Amendment's role see the congressional essay on modern doctrine constitution.congress.gov and our constitutional rights hub.

After the Civil War, the Fourteenth Amendment provided the constitutional vehicle the Supreme Court used to apply many protections from the Bill of Rights to the states through a process called selective incorporation, and the legal encyclopedia and Supreme Court materials outline that doctrinal shift Cornell LII on incorporation.

Key Supreme Court cases that applied rights to the states

Incorporation proceeded incrementally across the twentieth century through individual cases that applied specific rights to the states one at a time, and legal summaries identify early and later decisions that built the doctrine Cornell LII on incorporation.

A modern example of the Court addressing incorporation questions is the opinion in McDonald v. City of Chicago, which discusses the application of the Second Amendment against states and is available from the Supreme Court's published opinion text McDonald v. City of Chicago opinion.

Common misunderstandings and legal limits

Mistake: thinking all amendments applied to states from the start

A frequent misunderstanding is to assume the Bill of Rights always bound state governments; historically that was not the starting point, and legal commentary explains that many protections did not apply to states until the Court's incorporation decisions began to extend specific rights Cornell LII on incorporation.

Readers should note that incorporation was selective and incremental; which specific rights the Court has applied to the states has been determined case by case rather than by a single textual amendment Cornell LII on incorporation.

The role of the Supreme Court and the potential for change

The scope of incorporation remains a matter of judicial development, so which protections apply to the states can change only through Supreme Court decisions or subsequent constitutional amendments, a point legal sources emphasize when discussing incorporation as case law Cornell LII on incorporation.

Because incorporation rests on interpretation of the Fourteenth Amendment and later precedents, questions about a particular right's state-level application are best answered by reviewing relevant Supreme Court opinions and authoritative summaries McDonald v. City of Chicago opinion.
