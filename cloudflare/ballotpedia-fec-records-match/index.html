What the FEC provides: the authoritative record for campaign finance and committees

Key FEC search tools and data portals

The FEC campaign finance data portal and the candidates and committees search are the authoritative sources for committee names, FEC committee IDs, and official filings, including forms and reporting periods FEC candidates and committees search. See FEC guidance on researching candidates.

Use the FEC site to pull the committee's legal name, its assigned committee ID, the treasurer and point of contact, and the list of filed reports. The FEC search returns filing types such as Form 1 for organization statements and Form 3 or Form 3X for periodic finance reports.

What an FEC filing proves and what it does not

An FEC filing is the legal record for finance totals, receipts, disbursements, and committee organization. When you cite a filing, include the filing ID, form type, reporting period, and the date it was submitted so readers can verify the same document on the FEC site FEC campaign finance data.

FEC filings show reported numbers for a coverage period but may be amended later. Filings prove what was reported to the commission on a given date; they do not interpret motivations, policy positions, or unfiled activity. For those kinds of narrative claims, rely on attributed statements from the campaign site or Ballotpedia summaries.

Step 2: search FEC by name and compare committee IDs

Use the FEC candidate and committee search to locate any committees associated with the candidate's name. When you find possible matches, record the committee's exact legal name and the FEC committee ID. The committee ID is the most reliable key to match records and avoid confusing similarly named committees FEC candidates and committees search.

If multiple committees appear for the name, check each Form 1 to confirm the committee's stated purpose and the name of the candidate it represents. That Form 1 will list the treasurer and describe the committee relationship, which helps confirm whether the committee is the main campaign committee, a joint fundraising committee, or a separate political committee.

Step 3: confirm matches and download filings

Once you have the committee ID match, open the listed filings for that committee. Download the PDF of Form 1 and the most recent Form 3 or 3X for the reporting periods you intend to cite. How to read the most important FEC filings and what numbers mean

Form 1: statement of organization

Form 1 establishes committee identity, including the committee legal name, committee ID once assigned, treasurer name, and the committee's purpose. Use Form 1 to confirm which candidate a committee is organized to support and to capture the committee's official contact information FEC candidates and committees search.

When a Form 1 is not available or when the committee is new, look for other filings that reference the committee name and the treasurer. Always note the filing date and the reported effective date for the organization information.

Form 3 and 3X: periodic finance reports

Form 3 and Form 3X are the periodic finance reports that list receipts, disbursements, and cash on hand for a reporting period. Identify the lines that report total receipts and total disbursements, and copy those numbers alongside the reporting period and the filing date OpenSecrets guide to reading FEC reports.

When you extract a total, note whether the filing is an original report or an amendment. An amended Form 3X will show changes to previously reported totals, so include amendment indicators and dates in your notes to avoid misreporting historical numbers.

Common fields to extract and record

For each filing record these fields: committee name, committee ID, form type, filing ID, reporting period, date filed, total receipts, total disbursements, cash on hand, and whether the filing is an amendment. These items let readers trace the exact source for any figure you publish FEC campaign finance data.

Always record retrieval dates for both the Ballotpedia profile and the FEC filings. Retrieval dates clarify when the source was accessed and are essential if numbers change in later filings.

When to flag uncertainty and how to note it

Flag uncertainty when a committee ID cannot be found or when filings reference differing committee names. In that case, require a Form 1 or another filing that directly ties the committee to the candidate before publishing finance totals FEC candidates and committees search.

When uncertainty remains, state the specific missing element and use conditional phrasing such as according to Ballotpedia or public filings show when you summarize what is known. Do not assert a finance total without a cited FEC filing.

Common mistakes and how to avoid them

Mixing similarly named committees

One frequent error is matching on candidate name only and not confirming the FEC committee ID. Committees with similar names can belong to different candidates or to separate entities, so always use the committee ID to disambiguate and avoid misattribution FEC campaign finance data.

A practical fix is to require a Form 1 that lists the candidate or a clear statement of purpose, then cross-check any periodic reports against that committee ID.

Relying on summaries without citing filings

Another common mistake is citing secondary summaries like a web overview without linking to the primary FEC filing for the reported totals. Always attach a PSD or PDF of the reported Form 3X or Form 3 and include the filing date and filing ID in your notes OpenSecrets guide to reading FEC reports.

Not tracking filing dates and amendments

Failing to track filing dates and amended forms leads to reporting old or corrected numbers. Always note whether a report is an amendment and archive the latest PDF for the reporting period to reflect the correct totals OpenSecrets guide to reading FEC reports.
