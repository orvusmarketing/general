Why model year matters when choosing a used Crosstrek

How safety, features and powertrain changes affect value

Over successive model years, manufacturers add or make optional safety equipment standard, change powertrains and adjust trim content, and those shifts change both crash test results and buyer expectations. The IIHS pages show how crashworthiness and available safety features can vary across model years and trims, so a newer model or a higher trim with optional active safety tends to reduce risk for owners who prioritize crash protection IIHS crashworthiness pages. See the IIHS 2025 results here.

Because optional safety equipment can be the difference between a higher and lower rating, always confirm the listed vehicle's trim and feature set rather than assuming all years are identical.

Depreciation patterns and asking price for a used Crosstrek depend more on model year, trim and mileage than on the badge alone. Kelley Blue Book data shows values vary substantially by those factors, so a later model with more features can command a premium that may be justified by lower repair risk and better resale Kelley Blue Book guidance. Trim choices also affect long-term costs: features such as larger wheels or roof hardware can slightly increase running costs, while higher trims may include safety tech that mitigates ownership risk in some years.

Safety, recalls and crash testing you must check

Using IIHS and NHTSA results to compare years and trims

IIHS model pages report crashworthiness and available safety features for specific model years and help you compare which trims include active safety equipment; those differences can affect both real-world outcomes and insurance costs, so prioritize trims with stronger ratings when safety is a top priority IIHS crashworthiness pages.

NHTSA maintains vehicle pages that summarize recalls and consumer complaints for each model year; use those pages as a starting point to see whether problems are concentrated in certain years or powertrain choices NHTSA vehicle pages. You can also review the NHTSA vehicle detail page here.

How to check for open recalls and complaint history

Do not assume a recall has been remedied simply because a listing is recent; check the vehicle's VIN for open recalls and service completion records. NHTSA's VIN-based recall checks are the authoritative source for open campaigns and are part of a prudent pre-contact workflow NHTSA vehicle pages.

When a model year shows more complaints on NHTSA pages, treat that as an indicator to inspect service history more closely rather than as an automatic disqualifier.

What owner-satisfaction and reliability scores show

Recent model-year Crosstreks tend to score above average on owner-satisfaction and reliability measures, which supports choosing newer years for lower maintenance risk; this pattern appears in recent model evaluations and owner-score summaries J.D. Power ratings.

Consumer Reports also tracks long-term reliability and ownership cost trends that show year-to-year differences, so combine general rankings with VIN-level evidence when judging a single car Consumer Reports model overview.

Recurring problems to watch for by year group

Aggregated owner reports and repair summaries list recurring issues buyers should verify in the history report. Repair-focused sources note transmission and CVT concerns and occasional oil-consumption reports on some older model years, so ask for service records that show CVT maintenance or oil-consumption fixes where relevant RepairPal reliability profile.

Confirming a full service history that documents regular maintenance is especially important for years with known recurring issues, and a lack of records adds risk to lower-priced offers.

Step-by-step checklist for evaluating a used Crosstrek

Pre-contact checks to run on the listing

Before you contact the dealer, run a vehicle history report to confirm title status, ownership history, accident history, service history and mileage checks. Use the VIN from the listing to get VIN-level details whenever possible.

Compare the listing price to local pricing context and any available price analysis label to see whether the listing is in a reasonable range for its year, trim and mileage Kelley Blue Book guidance.

For many buyers, the most recent three to five model years generally offer the best balance of improved reliability, updated safety equipment and reasonable depreciation. However, VIN-level checks, service history and trim details determine the actual risk for any single listing.

What to verify in-person and during a test drive

In person, inspect service records, confirm the trim and safety features match the listing, and perform a test drive that includes steady highway speeds and city conditions to surface CVT or oil-consumption symptoms.

Verify that any recalls shown in the VIN check were completed and ask the dealer for repair orders that document relevant fixes; when service history is incomplete, factor that uncertainty into your offer.

Common buyer mistakes and how to avoid them

Overlooking VIN-level checks and open recalls

Buyers sometimes rely on a model-year reputation and skip VIN-level checks. That can miss open recalls or a history of repairs tied to a particular vehicle, so always run a VIN-based recall and history search before you visit the dealer NHTSA recall lookup.

If the listing omits service records or lists multiple owners, take extra care to confirm maintenance and title status rather than assuming good maintenance history.

Focusing only on sticker price without ownership cost estimates

Focusing only on the sticker price can hide higher expected ownership costs. Use local pricing tools and estimate likely repairs for older years or units with higher mileage, and balance that against the savings on purchase price Kelley Blue Book evidence.

When in doubt, prioritize vehicles with consistent service history and fewer ownership transfers, as those signals tend to reduce surprise costs after purchase.
