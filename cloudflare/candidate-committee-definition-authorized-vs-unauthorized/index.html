What a candidate committee is and why the distinction matters

Basic legal definition: candidate committee definition

A candidate committee definition under federal law refers to an entity that is authorized and controlled by a candidate to receive contributions and make expenditures on the candidate's behalf. According to the Federal Election Commission, an authorized committee is an entity the candidate designates to carry out campaign activity and financial reporting FEC candidates and committees page.

Independent or unauthorized committees are separate groups that make expenditures to influence an election without coordination with the candidate. The legal difference matters because authorized committees operate within contribution limits and reporting rules, while independent groups file separately for independent expenditures and are regulated under a different set of requirements FEC coordination guidance and advisory opinions.

Why the distinction affects reporting and limits is straightforward. If outside spending is coordinated with a campaign it can be treated as an in-kind contribution and counted against contribution limits. That conversion changes who must report what, and may create compliance exposure for the candidate's committee FEC coordination guidance and advisory opinions. How authorized committees are organized, controlled and authorized

Authorization in writing and candidate control

An authorized committee is created when a candidate grants authorization and establishes control over the entity, typically in writing. Campaigns and compliance officers rely on clear, written designations to show the candidate authorized the committee and to document who makes strategic decisions FEC candidates and committees page.

Control over the committee matters because it determines which entity is responsible for filings and which receipts and disbursements are treated as candidate activity. Maintaining delegation records and authorization memos helps demonstrate where decisions originated and who approved expenditures FEC candidates and committees page.

Typical committee roles: treasurer and reporting official

Federal rules require an authorized committee to have a treasurer who is responsible for signing reports and ensuring accurate filings. The treasurer is the designated reporting official on FEC forms and is accountable for itemized receipts and disbursements under FEC filing rules FEC filing and reporting guidance.

Campaigns typically name other administrative roles as well, but the treasurer's signature and responsibility are central to compliance. Clear role descriptions reduce the risk of missed deadlines and make it easier to correct errors if they occur FEC filing and reporting guidance.

What activities an authorized committee can lawfully do

An authorized committee may raise funds within federal contribution limits, pay vendors for campaign goods and services, and make other expenditures in support of the candidate. Those activities are reported as candidate receipts and disbursements once they meet reporting thresholds FEC candidates and committees page.

Campaign websites and public filings usually show the committee's name, treasurer, and contact details. For campaigns, keeping those public records current helps reporters and voters verify who is operating on the candidate's behalf FEC filing and reporting guidance.

Independent or unauthorized committees and the FEC three-prong coordination test

Payment, content and conduct: the three prongs explained

Independent committees make expenditures not coordinated with a candidate. The FEC determines coordination using a three prong coordination test that examines payment, content and conduct to decide whether outside spending counts as a contribution FEC coordination guidance and advisory opinions.

The payment prong asks whether the outside group paid for the communication. The content prong looks at whether the communication meets certain content standards, such as expressly advocating for or against a candidate. The conduct prong considers whether there was material involvement, direction or cooperation between the campaign and the outside spender FEC coordination guidance and advisory opinions.

A candidate committee is an entity the candidate authorizes and controls to receive contributions and make expenditures on their behalf. Unauthorized or independent committees operate separately. The FEC determines whether outside spending is coordinated using a payment, content and conduct test, and coordinated spending is treated as an in-kind contribution subject to limits and reporting rules.

When the three prongs are met together, the communication is treated as coordinated and is therefore an in-kind contribution to the candidate, subject to contribution limits and source prohibitions FEC coordination guidance and advisory opinions.

When an outside communication becomes coordinated

A clear example is when an outside group buys an ad using language provided by the campaign and follows a shared media plan. If payment, matching content and campaign direction are present, the ad can be reclassified as coordinated spending and counted against the candidate committee's limits FEC coordination guidance and advisory opinions.

Advisory opinions and court decisions continue to refine how the test applies in digital and rapid response contexts. Practitioners note that the details often depend on fact specific evidence about how the communication was produced and who made the key decisions Ballotpedia overview of independent expenditures. Digital campaigning raises practical questions, such as whether shared data, targeting strategies or rapid information exchanges create conduct prong concerns. The FEC and courts have issued guidance and opinions that help but do not eliminate uncertainty in new platforms FEC coordination guidance and advisory opinions.

Campaigns facing novel digital issues are advised to document vendor relationships, decision timelines and any communications that could be interpreted as strategic direction. Registration, disclosure and reporting duties for candidate committees

When to register with the FEC

An authorized committee must register with the FEC once statutory thresholds or activity triggers are met, for example when the candidate raises or spends a certain amount in federal campaign activity. The FEC's registration guidance explains these triggers and the required steps for candidates and committees FEC filing and reporting guidance.

Registering promptly helps ensure the committee meets disclosure obligations and reduces the risk of enforcement for late filings. The registration typically includes naming a treasurer and providing basic contact information that will appear in public records FEC candidates and committees page.

Periodic disclosure reports: receipts, disbursements and donors

Authorized committees must file periodic disclosure reports that list receipts, disbursements and, in certain cases, itemized donor information. The FEC explains the content requirements for those reports and the thresholds that trigger itemization FEC filing and reporting guidance.

These disclosure reports are public records, and reporters or voters can use them to verify committee activity. Campaigns should keep internal records aligned with filed reports to make correcting mistakes simpler if an amendment becomes necessary FEC filing and reporting guidance.

Filing deadlines and common report types

The FEC sets periodic report deadlines and different report types, including quarterly reports and pre and post election reports, depending on activity. Missing deadlines can create the need for remedial filings or trigger enforcement attention under FEC procedures FEC enforcement overview.

When coordination is treated as a contribution: legal consequences and risks

Conversion to in-kind contribution and limits

If a communication or expenditure is found to be coordinated under the FEC test, it is treated as an in-kind contribution and counts against contribution limits for the authorized committee. This reclassification changes how the amounts are reported and who is liable for compliance FEC coordination guidance and advisory opinions.

When spending is reclassified as an in-kind contribution, campaigns must ensure the source is permissible and the amount does not exceed applicable limits. That means previously separate outside spending can create contribution-limit exposure for the candidate's committee FEC coordination guidance and advisory opinions.

Source prohibitions and contribution-limit exposure

Reclassified coordinated spending must also comply with source prohibitions. For example, corporate or foreign national funding rules remain relevant when an in-kind contribution is created by coordination FEC enforcement overview.

Campaigns that reclassify spending may need to correct reports and, in some cases, refund or otherwise resolve prohibited or excessive contributions under FEC rules. Timely remedial action can affect enforcement outcomes FEC enforcement overview.

FEC enforcement mechanisms and penalties

Failure to register, to timely file required reports, or to treat coordinated spending as contributions can lead to FEC enforcement investigations, settlement measures and civil penalties. The FEC provides procedures and examples of enforcement outcomes in its public guidance FEC enforcement overview.

The existence of enforcement procedures means campaigns should document decisions, correct mistakes quickly and consider voluntary disclosure when errors are discovered. That approach is consistent with FEC practice and can be important in resolving compliance issues FEC enforcement overview.

Practical compliance steps campaigns and compliance officers use

Authorization documentation and written policies

Recommended documentation starts with written authorization that shows the candidate designated the committee and identifies who may act on its behalf. Campaigns keep authorization letters, delegation records and internal policies to make responsibilities clear FEC candidates and committees page.

Written policies for vendor engagement and communications approvals reduce the chance that outside groups or vendors inadvertently create coordination. These policies also help internal teams follow a consistent record keeping practice Campaign Legal Center compliance overview.

Practical review steps include checking who paid for a communication, whether the content mirrors campaign materials, and whether campaign staff or vendors materially participated in creation. These checks align with the payment, content and conduct prongs used by regulators FEC coordination guidance and advisory opinions. (see Michael Carbonara homepage michaelcarbonara.com)

Documenting these reviews in decision logs, approvals and vendor contracts creates an audit trail if a future question arises. Good record keeping is a frequently recommended compliance practice in legal analyses and FEC guidance Campaign Legal Center compliance overview.

Typical errors, reporting pitfalls and enforcement examples

Common mistakes that trigger FEC attention

Common compliance errors include failing to register on time, missing reporting deadlines, and not reporting coordinated spending as contributions. These are the issues the FEC highlights in its enforcement guidance FEC enforcement overview.

Other mistakes involve weak documentation for vendor decisions or informal coordination that later appears as material involvement. Such gaps make it harder to defend a position if the FEC opens an inquiry FEC filing and reporting guidance.

Examples of failure to register or report and consequences

The FEC's public enforcement history shows enforcement through investigations, settlements and civil penalties for a range of violations. While outcomes vary by case facts, the existence of enforcement actions underscores the regulatory risk of noncompliance FEC enforcement overview.

Remedial steps often include amended reports, voluntary disclosures and, when necessary, corrective repayments or settlements. Timely remedial steps can influence enforcement resolutions under FEC practice FEC enforcement overview.

How to correct past reporting errors

Correcting errors typically means filing amended reports and documenting the reason for the change. The FEC provides instructions on amending filings and on voluntary disclosure procedures that campaigns can follow to address past mistakes FEC filing and reporting guidance.

Transparent correction and cooperation with regulators are common themes in enforcement settlements. Campaigns should maintain records explaining why a change was made and how it was calculated for future reference FEC enforcement overview.

Practical scenarios and short case studies to spot coordination

Scenario: paid ad by an outside group that mirrors campaign messaging

Imagine an outside committee runs a paid digital ad that uses the campaign's exact talking points and timing. If the ad is paid for by the outside group, replicates campaign content, and was placed with campaign knowledge or direction, the three prong test may be satisfied and the spending reclassified as coordinated FEC coordination guidance and advisory opinions.

In that case, the candidate's authorized committee may need to report the ad as an in-kind contribution and confirm the source complies with contribution rules. Documentation of the campaign's interactions with the outside group will be key to resolving questions Ballotpedia overview of independent expenditures.

Scenario: vendor coordination and shared strategy calls

A vendor that works for both a campaign and an outside group can create risks if strategy calls or shared materials influence an outside communication. The conduct prong examines whether the campaign provided material involvement or strategic direction that made the outside spending coordinated FEC coordination guidance and advisory opinions.

Practical steps include written vendor agreements that limit information sharing when appropriate, and logs showing who attended strategy meetings. These records help clarify whether a communication should be classified as independent or coordinated Campaign Legal Center compliance overview.

Checklist to apply the three prong test step by step

Step 1, confirm who paid. Step 2, compare the content to campaign messaging. Step 3, document any campaign involvement in planning or approvals. Step 4, if all three steps are present, treat the spending as a contribution and report accordingly FEC coordination guidance and advisory opinions.

Using the checklist consistently makes assessment quicker and creates a clear record for counsel or regulators if questions arise. Keep decision logs that show the facts supporting the assessment Campaign Legal Center compliance overview.

Conclusion: quick checklist and next steps for campaigns and voters

Three things campaigns should do now

First, document authorization in writing and name a treasurer who signs filings. Second, review communications for payment, content and conduct indicators before assuming independence. Third, maintain filing discipline to meet FEC deadlines and correct reports promptly if needed FEC filing and reporting guidance.

What voters and local reporters can check in public records

Voters and reporters can check FEC filings to confirm whether a committee is registered as an authorized candidate committee and to view periodic disclosures of receipts and disbursements. Public FEC records provide primary-source verification of committee status and activity FEC candidates and committees page. (see the campaign about page about)
