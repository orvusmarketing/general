What does clause 42 of the Magna Carta mean?

A concise guide to what clause 42 said in 1215 and how historians interpret it.

Clause 42 of the 1215 Magna Carta is a short passage often quoted in discussions about medieval rights language. Modern translations render the clause as permitting a subject to leave and return to England unharmed, provided he preserves allegiance to the king. Quick answer: what clause 42 says and why it matters

One-sentence summary

The standard modern English rendering of clause 42 reads that it shall be lawful for any man to leave and return to the kingdom unharmed and without fear, by land or water, while preserving his allegiance to the king.

The text appears in standard modern transcriptions and editions used by students and scholars, and it is typically cited as evidence that medieval law regulated who could travel and return under conditions of fealty rather than creating a modern, unrestricted personal right. Exact formulations in major modern transcriptions

The most accessible line-by-line English renderings used in teaching quote clause 42 as permitting any man to leave and return to the kingdom unharmed and without fear, by land or water, provided he preserves his allegiance to the king. This rendering is found in modern transcriptions that rely on the surviving diplomatic texts. Avalon Project transcription UEA clause 42 text

Translators choose specific phrasing for words that in medieval Latin carry several meanings, so one edition may prefer "leave and return" while another uses slightly different verbs. Those choices affect whether readers hear a broad personal liberty or a narrower, allegiance-tied promise. British Library presentation

How translations render key phrases such as 'leave and return' and 'preserving allegiance'

Two phrases shape interpretation: the clause's words for departing and coming back, and the condition about preserving allegiance. Translators often emphasize that the promise is conditional on retaining fealty to the crown, which changes legal meaning from an absolute permit to a regulated allowance. Readers should note that medieval legal language can be formal and tied to feudal relationships; a literal modern-sense translation can mislead unless readers consider the diplomatic context and the document's audience. 1215 context: baronial grievances, travel, pilgrimage and allegiance

Clause 42 appears within a negotiated list of concessions between King John and rebellious barons who raised grievances about royal conduct, including restrictions on movement, treatment of subjects abroad, and the safety of pilgrims and travelers. The clause addressed specific baronial concerns about being hindered or endangered while leaving or returning to England. National Archives learning resource

In 1215, travel had political and religious importance: nobles and knights moved for war, diplomacy, or pilgrimage, and exile and sanctuary meant that leaving the realm could be sensitive. The clause insists on the ability to leave and return without harm so long as fealty is preserved, a condition that reflects medieval priorities. National Archives learning resource

Because the Magna Carta was part of bargaining between groups within the kingdom, clause 42 is best read as a negotiated protection rather than a unilateral royal decree in the style of later statutory law. Legal force then and later: reissues, confirmations and changing effect

Why the 1215 charter had limited immediate enforcement

The 1215 Magna Carta was negotiated under duress and had limited immediate legal force because it reflected a political settlement during a crisis; enforcement depended on the balance of power and on subsequent royal action. Its clauses, including clause 42, did not operate in a vacuum and were subject to political reversal. National Archives learning resource FullFact discussion

After King John's death and ongoing political turmoil, Magna Carta was reissued and modified in later years, which changed how individual clauses were preserved or dropped from the legal record. Its patchwork reception matters when readers ask whether clause 42 had a stable, long-term legal effect. UK Parliament summary of reissues

How the 1216, 1217 and 1225 reissues altered or preserved clauses

Magna Carta was reissued with changes in 1216 and 1217 and then confirmed and substantially redescribed in 1225, and those reissues often reflected shifting royal priorities and the need to secure wider acceptance. Some clauses survived in later confirmations while others were omitted or rephrased, so the presence of a clause in 1215 does not guarantee its long-run statutory life. UK Parliament summary of reissues

That process means historians must be cautious attributing continuous legal force to any single clause from 1215. Legal consequences and administrative practice depended on subsequent confirmations as well as local enforcement and custom. National Archives learning resource

Common misconceptions and pitfalls to avoid

The most frequent error is to equate clause 42 with a modern, unconditional individual freedom of movement. That reading ignores the medieval framing that ties travel to preserved allegiance and ignores how jurisdiction and feudal duties limited the clause in practice. British Library overview

Another pitfall is relying on loose phrasing that removes the condition about fealty. When writers omit the allegiance clause, they create an impression the charter promised unqualified safety for leaving and returning, which is not how most scholars read the passage. J. C. Holt analysis

Finally, remember that legal practice matters. Even if the wording looks protective, enforcement depended on local officials and the political balance. Treating a short clause as a settled constitutional rule across centuries is usually an overclaim. UK Parliament summary of reissues
