Quick answer: which amendment protects religious freedom?

The constitutional guarantee that protects freedom of religion is found in the First Amendment. The Amendment states, in part, that "Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof," which sets out both the Establishment Clause and the Free Exercise Clause, and frames legal claims about religious liberty National Archives Bill of Rights transcript.

Voters should note that saying the First Amendment protects religious freedom does not resolve how courts apply that protection in every dispute, because later statutes and Supreme Court opinions shape the tests judges use.

How the First Amendment protects freedom of expression and freedom of religion

Text of the two religion clauses

The First Amendment includes two short clauses that together govern religion in U.S. constitutional law: the Establishment Clause, which prevents government from creating an official religion or favoring one faith over another, and the Free Exercise Clause, which protects individuals from laws that prohibit their religious practice. Relationship between free exercise and free speech, freedom of expression and freedom of religion

Free exercise and free speech are distinct constitutional guarantees, but they can overlap in practice: expressive acts can be religious in character, and some religious claims raise free-speech issues if the government limits religiously motivated expression. Key Supreme Court cases that shaped free-exercise doctrine: Sherbert, Smith, Hobby Lobby

Sherbert v. Verner and strict scrutiny

Sherbert v. Verner (1963) established that when the government imposes a direct burden on religious exercise, courts generally apply a strict-scrutiny inquiry requiring the government to show a compelling interest and narrow tailoring before denying an exemption, a foundational approach in free-exercise law Sherbert v. Verner opinion.

Employment Division v. Smith and neutrality principle

Employment Division v. Smith (1990) marked a major change by holding that neutral, generally applicable laws do not automatically require religious exemptions; under Smith, if a law is neutral and broadly applicable the state may enforce it without applying strict scrutiny in many cases Employment Division v. Smith opinion.

Burwell v. Hobby Lobby and RFRA implications

Burwell v. Hobby Lobby Stores, Inc. (2014) shows how statutory protections interact with the Constitution: the Court held that certain closely held corporations could seek exemptions under the Religious Freedom Restoration Act for some regulatory requirements, illustrating that RFRA can change the outcome in particular contexts Burwell v. Hobby Lobby opinion.

Together these opinions form the core of modern free-exercise doctrine: Sherbert set out a compelling-interest framework, Smith put limits on that approach for neutral laws, and subsequent statutory law has reopened some doors for exemptions in certain circumstances. Statutory framework: RFRA and how statutes change remedies

The Religious Freedom Restoration Act is a federal statute enacted to restore a higher level of judicial scrutiny for burdens on religious exercise in cases where RFRA applies; it requires that the government show a compelling interest and use the least restrictive means before substantially burdening religious exercise in covered contexts. See a Congressional Research Service primer The Religious Freedom Restoration Act: A Primer.

In practice, RFRA has altered the remedies available to claimants by requiring courts, in many RFRA cases, to apply a compelling-interest test instead of deferring to the neutrality principle announced in Smith; courts have used RFRA in notable cases such as Hobby Lobby to grant exemptions in specific circumstances Burwell v. Hobby Lobby opinion.

Whether RFRA applies in a particular dispute depends on statutory scope and jurisdictional questions; some claims are brought under state versions of RFRA or under the federal statute depending on the actor and the legal setting, and outcomes can differ across courts and factual records.

How courts decide religious-liberty claims today: tests and decision criteria

Judges generally look at three core questions when resolving religious-liberty claims: whether the challenged law is neutral and generally applicable, whether RFRA or a similar statute applies, and whether the claimant is an individual or an entity such as a closely held corporation. These lenses shape which legal test governs and what burdens each side must meet.

If a law is not neutral or targets religious conduct, courts often apply a strict-scrutiny test requiring the government to show a compelling interest and narrow tailoring, an approach that traces to Sherbert and is described in that opinion Sherbert v. Verner opinion.

When a law is neutral and generally applicable, Smith holds that the law may be enforced without providing a religious exemption; under that framework the government does not automatically face strict scrutiny, and the claimant must show why an exemption is nonetheless required Employment Division v. Smith opinion.

Where RFRA applies, courts substitute the statutory compelling-interest test for Smith's neutrality rule in covered contexts; RFRA can thus require a stronger showing by the government and may produce exemptions when strict scrutiny is satisfied, as reflected in later cases and commentary.

Because these pathways differ, outcomes in religious-liberty litigation are fact-specific: the same regulatory measure can produce different results depending on whether the court views the law as neutral, whether RFRA applies, and how the claimant frames the burden on belief or conduct.

Common misunderstandings and pitfalls about religious freedom

A frequent myth is that religious freedom gives an absolute right to ignore neutral laws; in reality, Employment Division v. Smith makes clear that neutral, generally applicable laws can be applied even when they incidentally burden religious practice, and courts will analyze whether specialized tests or statutes change that result Employment Division v. Smith opinion.

Another mistake is assuming that statutory protections like RFRA are universal; RFRA's coverage depends on whether the statute applies to the actor and setting, and state-level laws can differ, so statutory relief is not guaranteed in every instance.

Readers should also be cautious about equating corporate statements or slogans with legal rights; court decisions determine how corporate structure, ownership, and statutory coverage affect who may claim religious exemptions, as seen in Hobby Lobby and related litigation Burwell v. Hobby Lobby opinion.

Practical examples and scenarios: workplaces, schools, corporations, and digital spaces

Workplace accommodation disputes commonly illustrate the practical tests: an employee seeking an exemption from a neutral company policy must show how the policy burdens sincere religious practice and then invoke the relevant test, which may be Smith, Sherbert, or RFRA depending on the context and statutory coverage.
