What is the role of small business in developing countries? A practical, evidence-based guide

A concise, evidence-based overview of how SMEs shape jobs, finance and markets in developing countries.

Small businesses and microenterprises are a central topic in development policy because they touch jobs, incomes and local services. It is intended for voters, policymakers and practitioners seeking a concise, evidence-based view of SME roles in developing economies.

What we mean by small business and SMEs in developing countries

Authors and multilateral bodies use the term SMEs to group micro, small and medium enterprises for policy and measurement. The report summaries used in this article follow standard practice, where microenterprises are very small firms, small firms are larger than micro, and medium firms are larger still, with thresholds set by employment or turnover metrics used by institutions such as the OECD and World Bank OECD SME Outlook.

Definitions matter because they determine who is counted as a formal SME and who remains in the informal economy. Measured employment shares and GDP contributions depend on whether informal microenterprises are included or excluded, and that influences which policies are prioritized World Bank feature. About

Common metrics used in the literature include employment share, contribution to GDP, firm counts and formalization rates. These indicators help policymakers track inclusion, productivity and whether firms are accessing formal finance and tax systems OECD SME Outlook.

Why small businesses matter for the international economy

Small businesses contribute through several channels: they create jobs, support household incomes, and sustain local demand and services. Recent multilateral reviews emphasize these channels when they describe the developmental role of SMEs World Bank feature.

impact of small business on international economy

The impact of small business on international economy is strongest when firms are formalized and linked to larger markets, because formal firms can access finance, meet standards and participate in exports more readily. Multilateral analyses show that market linkages and standards increase SMEs ability to export and upgrade when public policy and buyer relationships exist UNCTAD policy brief.

Channels of impact: jobs, incomes, local demand

SMEs act as local employers and buyers of inputs; this creates circular demand in local economies and supports incomes for households. The degree to which this raises national outcomes depends on firm size, sector and formality, according to OECD and World Bank summaries OECD SME Outlook.

When SMEs influence national outcomes

SMEs are more likely to influence national GDP and export performance where they are formalized and integrated into value chains. The evidence stresses conditional effects: linkages and policy support matter for measurable national impact World Bank feature.

Employment and GDP contributions: what the evidence shows

Many developing countries show that SMEs and small firms provide the majority of employment, especially when informal microenterprises are included in counts. Multilateral reporting finds that employment shares are large in these contexts OECD SME Outlook.

Employment share does not always map directly to GDP share. Productivity differences between small informal firms and larger formal firms mean that a high employment share can coexist with a smaller share of GDP, and policy should weigh both jobs and productivity World Bank feature.

The persistent SME finance gap and its implications

A large global financing shortfall for micro, small and medium enterprises has been documented and remains a constraint on scaling and formalization. Analyses trace this gap back to widely cited assessments of MSME finance shortfalls IFC MSME finance gap report. See the IFC MSME finance overview here.

Limited access to credit and guarantees reduces firms ability to invest in equipment, to hire staff or to register formally. Where credit is scarce, firms may delay productivity-enhancing investments and remain informal to avoid costs associated with regulation and taxation OECD SME Outlook.

Common financing instruments recommended by reviews include targeted credit lines, partial credit guarantees and blended finance that reduce lender risk. These instruments aim to lower the cost of capital for viable small firms while encouraging formalization IFC MSME finance gap report. See OECD's financing-gap analysis here.

Non-financial constraints: infrastructure, regulation and digital access

Poor infrastructure, regulatory complexity and limited digital connectivity are repeatedly identified as major non-financial barriers to SME productivity and scale in developing regions UNCTAD policy brief.

Infrastructure gaps increase logistics costs and reduce the returns on investment, which interacts directly with finance: if transport and storage are weak, credit-financed expansion may not be profitable. This interaction between infrastructure and finance can hinder scaling.

Small businesses are major sources of employment and local income in many developing countries, but their wider economic impact depends on access to finance, formalization, infrastructure and market linkages; policy packages that combine these elements tend to perform better.

Regulatory complexity and tax compliance costs also deter formalization. Simplifying business registration and reducing compliance burdens are common policy recommendations because they lower the fixed costs of moving from informal to formal status OECD SME Outlook.

Digital access and skills matter for market reach. Where firms can use digital tools and meet digital standards, they can access wider markets, improve record-keeping and benefit more from online finance products, as noted in recent policy reviews UNCTAD policy brief.

SMEs and inclusive outcomes: women, youth and informal firms

SMEs and microenterprises can expand economic participation for women and youth, but evidence shows that women-owned microenterprises face larger financing and formalization gaps than male-owned firms in many countries ILO statistical summary.

Systematic reviews point to barriers that are specific to women and young entrepreneurs, such as lower collateral access, care responsibilities and limited networks, which affect the ability to formalize and scale Journal systematic review.

Targeted approaches, such as finance products tailored to women-led firms, mentorship and market linkages that reduce entry costs, are among the policy options suggested to improve inclusion UNCTAD policy brief.

How SMEs connect to value chains and export markets

Integration into local and regional value chains and adoption of standards increase SMEs' ability to export and upgrade, where public policies and buyer linkages exist. Reports highlight buyer-supplier relationships and standards as key mechanisms World Bank feature.

Lead firms and larger buyers can provide predictable demand, quality requirements and technical assistance that help SMEs meet export standards. However, costs of certification and logistics are practical barriers that must be managed UNCTAD policy brief.

A pragmatic policy package: finance, simplification, skills and linkages

Recent reviews converge on a policy package: expand targeted SME finance and guarantees, simplify registration and tax compliance, invest in digital skills and standards, and strengthen market linkages. These elements are presented as complementary in multilateral summaries OECD SME Outlook. See related topics on the strength and security page.

Finance works better when paired with training and market access. For example, credit combined with buyer introductions and skills training raises the chance that firms will invest productively and remain formal, rather than temporarily expanding and contracting UNCTAD policy brief.

Regulatory simplification can include single-window registration, lower thresholds for small firms, and clearer tax filing rules. Digitalization of processes reduces compliance costs and improves transparency, making formal status more attractive OECD SME Outlook.

What the evidence says about which interventions scale

Systematic reviews assess combinations of finance, training and market linkages and find that combined interventions often outperform isolated measures, although evidence varies by context. Reviews call for pilots and stronger evaluation to clarify which packages scale microenterprises into formal SMEs Journal systematic review.

Open research questions remain, including a precise, updated estimate of the post-pandemic finance gap and robust cross-country evidence on scaling in fragile contexts. Policymakers are advised to build monitoring and evaluation into programs to generate context-specific evidence OECD SME Outlook.

Decision criteria for policymakers and donors

Policymakers should use clear decision criteria: expected reach, likely impact on productivity or jobs, inclusion of disadvantaged groups, and monitoring capacity. These criteria help prioritize among finance, training and market-linkage options UNCTAD policy brief.

Consider trade-offs: universal programs can reach many firms but may dilute impact, while targeted instruments focus on vulnerable groups or high-potential sectors. Coordination with private buyers and local infrastructure planning improves cost-effectiveness OECD SME Outlook.

Common mistakes and implementation pitfalls to avoid

Frequent errors include offering finance without market linkages, one-size-fits-all training, and weak monitoring. These mistakes reduce uptake and limit scaling, according to systematic reviews Journal systematic review.

Corrective steps are practical: pilot interventions, sequence finance after buyer introductions, adapt training to sector needs and embed monitoring and evaluation from the start. These process steps reduce the chance of wasted resources OECD SME Outlook.

Practical country and sector scenarios for policymakers

Urban services and retail microenterprises: start with simplified registration, digital record-keeping and microcredit linked to local payment platforms. Indicators to track include formalization rate, number of firms accessing credit and change in sales World Bank feature.

Agricultural value-chain SMEs: focus on buyer linkages, storage and logistics, and standards adoption. Sequencing should prioritize storage and transport improvements, then certification support and buyer matchmaking. Key indicators include volumes sold to formal buyers and number of certified firms UNCTAD policy brief.

Conclusion: priorities for action and further reading

Key takeaways: SMEs are central to employment in many developing countries, a large finance gap limits scaling, and non-financial constraints such as infrastructure and regulation matter alongside credit. These summary points are emphasized across multilateral reports OECD SME Outlook.

Policy priorities are clear in the literature: pair finance with market linkages, simplify business processes, invest in digital skills and standards, and build evaluation into programs.
