What is the 14th Amendment right to due process?

A plain-language overview of the Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause and the key cases that define it.

What the Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause says and why it matters

Text of the clause

The Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause says that no state may deprive any person of life, liberty, or property without due process of law. For the text and original framing, see the amendment text at the National Archives National Archives amendment text. This constitutional line is the starting point for modern claims that state governments must follow fair procedures before taking important rights or possessions.

Short explanation in plain language - bill of rights due process

In plain terms, the clause requires that when a state acts to take away life, liberty, or property, the government must respect basic legal safeguards. "Life" covers serious deprivations such as capital punishment or extreme restrictions on movement. "Liberty" embraces freedoms of movement and certain personal rights. "Property" reaches both tangible assets and some government benefits. The clause anchors limits on state action and the protection of individual interests.

Why states are bound by it

The Fourteenth Amendment was adopted in 1868 to extend constitutional protections against states, not just the federal government. That historical adoption and its intent are recorded in primary materials from the period and summarized by archival sources, which explain why states became the target of these constitutional limits National Archives amendment text and the Constitution Center's explanation.

How the clause became central: historical adoption and early incorporation

Post Civil War context

Congress adopted the Fourteenth Amendment in the aftermath of the Civil War. Lawmakers intended it to protect newly freed people and to ensure that state governments could not enact laws that severely limited basic rights. The amendment created a constitutional pathway for individuals to challenge state practices on federal grounds, and archival records explain that historical purpose National Archives amendment text.

Early incorporation arguments

After adoption, one key question was whether provisions of the federal Bill of Rights applied to the states through the Fourteenth Amendment. Early legal debates and later court decisions developed the idea that some federal protections could be applied to state laws, a process now known as selective incorporation. This legal approach drew on constitutional text and evolving judicial interpretation.

Gitlow and the path toward applying the Bill of Rights to states

Gitlow v. New York is an early Supreme Court decision that helped establish incorporation as a principle. The case is widely cited as a turning point in how courts thought about applying free speech protections against state laws, and it marks the beginning of a selective approach to bringing Bill of Rights guarantees to the state level Gitlow v. New York summary at Oyez.

Procedural versus substantive due process explained

What procedural due process covers

Procedural due process focuses on the methods government must use before it deprives someone of life, liberty, or property. That typically means notice of the action and an opportunity to be heard before an impartial decisionmaker. The clause therefore guarantees fair procedures rather than particular outcomes in most cases.

The Fourteenth Amendment right to due process bars states from depriving any person of life, liberty, or property without appropriate legal procedures or, in some cases, without justification when fundamental rights are at stake. Courts distinguish procedural protections, which focus on notice and hearings, from substantive protections, which limit the state’s power to infringe recognized fundamental rights.

What substantive due process covers

Substantive due process differs. It asks whether the government has the constitutional authority to infringe on certain fundamental rights at all, regardless of the procedures used. Courts have sometimes used substantive doctrine to protect personal rights that the justices deemed fundamental. Because courts decide which rights are fundamental, this area of law is often contested and evolves with new decisions Cornell LII overview of the Due Process Clause.

Why the distinction matters in litigation

The procedural and substantive frameworks lead to different questions in court. Procedural claims bring a test about what procedures are required in context. Substantive claims bring analysis about whether a particular government action implicates a fundamental right and whether the state can justify that intrusion. Parties and judges therefore frame cases differently depending on which path they choose.

Core procedural protections: notice, hearings, and key cases

Basic procedural rights

At the core, procedural due process usually requires that a person receive notice of a government action and a meaningful chance to respond. These protections apply when a state action threatens recognized interests such as property or ongoing government benefits. The precise form of notice or hearing will vary by context and by the potential consequences of the government's act.

Goldberg v. Kelly and termination of benefits

Goldberg v. Kelly is a foundational case that shaped how courts treat benefit terminations. The Supreme Court held that a welfare recipient is entitled to a hearing before benefits are cut in many circumstances, because the loss of those benefits can be a serious deprivation of property or means of livelihood Goldberg v. Kelly summary at Oyez. That decision emphasizes that formal procedures matter when benefits or similar entitlements are at stake.

What notice and opportunity to be heard mean in practice

In practice, notice can be written communication that explains the reasons for the action and how to respond. An opportunity to be heard might mean a live hearing, an administrative review, or another process that lets the person present evidence. The required depth of the hearing depends on context and on the risk that an error will cause serious harm.

How courts decide what process is due: the Mathews v. Eldridge balancing test

The three Mathews factors

Mathews v. Eldridge set out a three factor balancing test that courts use to determine how much process is required: the private interest affected, the risk of erroneous deprivation through current procedures and the probable value of additional safeguards, and the government interest including administrative burdens. This framework helps courts tailor procedural protections to specific contexts Mathews v. Eldridge summary at Oyez.

Applying the test to administrative decisions

Under Mathews, a court weighs the claimant's stake against the costs to the government of additional procedures. For example, when a benefit is vital to a person's livelihood, the private interest is strong and the court may require more robust procedures. When the government faces large administrative burdens, the court may require simpler steps.

Limitations of the test

The Mathews balancing approach is not determinative in all cases. Judges still exercise discretion in weighing factors and may reach different results in similar contexts. The test is focused on procedural adequacy, not on whether a law is substantively permissible under the Due Process Clause.

Substantive due process: what it is and why it is contested

Core idea of substantive protection

Substantive due process identifies certain rights as so fundamental that government cannot infringe them except under strict conditions. When a right is recognized as fundamental, laws that burden that right face close judicial scrutiny. The doctrine therefore operates as a limit on state power as well as a guarantor of certain personal freedoms.

How courts recognize fundamental rights

Court recognition of a fundamental right usually looks to history, tradition, and the role a right plays in ordered liberty. Because standards for recognition are not fixed, the set of protected rights can change as justices interpret the Constitution and as precedent evolves. That interpretive process makes the doctrine a frequent subject of legal debate Cornell LII overview.

Dobbs as a recent example

Dobbs v. Jackson Women’s Health Organization is a recent high profile decision that altered previous precedent about abortion and showed that substantive due process boundaries can shift. The decision illustrates how courts may revisit earlier rulings and change which rights receive heightened protection, which is why substantive doctrine remains contested and dynamic Dobbs opinion PDF at the Supreme Court.

Incorporation and the Bill of Rights: how many protections apply to states

Selective incorporation explained

Selective incorporation is the method courts use to determine which Bill of Rights protections apply to the states through the Fourteenth Amendment. Rather than applying every federal guarantee in one step, the Court has applied individual rights over time, often based on whether a right is fundamental to our scheme of ordered liberty. This selective path shapes how state constitutions and laws interact with federal protections.

Key early cases that began incorporation

Early 20th century cases began applying certain Bill of Rights protections against state governments. Gitlow v. New York is an important early example in the free speech context, showing how the Court began to transfer restraints on federal action to state law. The practical result is that many protections once thought to bind only the federal government now limit state action as well Gitlow v. New York summary at Oyez.

Practical effect for state law and individual rights

The practical effect of incorporation is that state criminal procedure, free speech protections, and other rights can be enforced in state courts against state action. This means individuals challenging a state law may rely on incorporated federal guarantees to argue that a state practice violates the Constitution. The result has been gradual and selective application over many decades.

Standards and decision rules courts use when due process is at issue

Balancing tests versus strict standards

When due process is raised, judges choose among tools such as balancing tests like Mathews or more rigid standards when a fundamental right is at stake. Balancing compares competing interests and costs, while strict scrutiny or heightened standards apply when a law burdens a recognized fundamental right. The choice of approach can change the outcome of a case. See a collection of notable due process cases at Justia for examples of how courts have applied different standards.

How judges weigh conflicting interests

Judges consider the severity of the private interest, the likelihood of error under existing procedures, and the burden that additional safeguards would impose on the government. That weighing is often explicit in opinions and guides how much process the Constitution requires. Deference to legislative or agency judgments can also play a role in narrowing courts conclusions.

When courts defer to agencies or legislatures

Court deference appears when judges give weight to the practical expertise or policy choices of agencies and legislatures. Where administrative efficiency or complex factual determinations matter, a court may demand less procedural protection. The balance between efficiency and protection is a recurring question in due process cases and helps explain variation in judicial outcomes Mathews v. Eldridge summary at Oyez.

Common mistakes and misconceptions about due process

Mistaking due process for guaranteed outcomes

A frequent misconception is that due process guarantees a particular result. In reality, procedural due process guarantees a fair procedure, not a win on the merits. A hearing may be required, but the person who has the hearing may still lose if the evidence does not support their claim.

Confusing procedural steps with substantive rights

Another mistake is to conflate having procedural protections with having a substantive right. Procedure protects how a decision is made. Substantive rights define what the government can or cannot do. Understanding that distinction helps readers interpret cases and media coverage accurately.

Overgeneralizing from a single case

Readers also sometimes overgeneralize from one decision and assume it rewrites broad doctrine. Because courts issue narrow holdings and reasoning varies among opinions, single cases should be read for their holding and the logic used to reach it rather than for assumed sweeping rules Cornell LII overview of the Due Process Clause.

Practical scenarios: how due process works in common situations

Administrative benefit terminations

Consider a state agency that terminates a recurring benefit. Courts will look at the private interest at stake and at whether an erroneous termination could create serious hardship. Goldberg set a high bar for requiring a hearing in welfare terminations, and Mathews provides a balancing framework to decide what specific procedures are required in other administrative settings Goldberg v. Kelly summary at Oyez.

Property deprivation and takings

When the government takes property or interferes with ownership rights, due process protections can require notice and a method to challenge the action. State procedures for eminent domain, civil forfeiture, or license revocations often trigger procedural due process concerns because they may affect core property interests.

Criminal procedure and pretrial rights

In criminal contexts, due process interacts with other constitutional protections such as the right to counsel and the right to a fair trial. Pretrial detention, bail decisions, and rules governing evidence can all raise due process questions about whether the accused received adequate procedural safeguards.

What this doctrine means for ordinary people and state policies

Protections in daily government interactions

For most people, due process matters when they interact with state agencies, receive public benefits, face licensing actions, or are subject to criminal process. It means the state must follow fair steps before taking away important interests and that those steps often include notice and an opportunity to respond. See our constitutional rights hub for related material.

Limits on state power

Substantive doctrine can limit certain state actions altogether by shielding fundamental liberties. Because courts decide which liberties are protected, the scope of these limits changes over time with new decisions. For discussion of the amendment's role in protecting civil rights, see how the Fourteenth Amendment protects civil rights and civil liberties.

Why the doctrine affects public services and regulations

Because due process governs how states make decisions that affect rights or property, it shapes administrative design and the way public services are delivered. Agencies design procedures knowing courts will assess whether those steps are constitutionally adequate.

Conclusion: what to watch next in due process law

Ongoing doctrinal debates

Procedural protections remain well established, but the boundaries of substantive due process are active areas of debate. Recent decisions illustrate that courts may revisit earlier precedents and change the set of protected rights.

How future cases may change the scope of rights

Future Supreme Court rulings can expand or contract protections, and lower courts will continue to apply balancing frameworks and precedent to new facts. Watching major opinions is therefore essential for understanding how doctrine will evolve.
