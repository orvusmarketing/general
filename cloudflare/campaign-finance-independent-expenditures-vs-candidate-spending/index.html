campaign finance: quick definition and why it matters to voters

Campaign finance questions often start with a simple distinction: who controls the money and whether messaging is coordinated. According to the Federal Election Commission, an independent expenditure is spending that expressly advocates for the election or defeat of a clearly identified candidate and that is not coordinated with that candidate's campaign FEC guidance on coordinated and independent expenditures.

Candidate spending, by contrast, is controlled by an authorized candidate committee and appears on regular committee reports filed to the FEC. These filings show receipts, disbursements and many in-kind entries and are the official record of candidate-controlled activity FEC data portal.

Why this distinction matters to voters: independent expenditures can bring large amounts of outside messaging into a race while remaining legally separate from a candidate's committee. That separation affects how quickly and where those amounts are reported and what a voter can learn from the filings OpenSecrets outside spending data.

Understanding these categories helps voters trace who paid for ads or mailings, how close payments came to a primary or general election, and whether there were disclosures to check in federal or state systems.

How U.S. law distinguishes independent expenditures and coordination tests

The FEC offers guidance on what counts as coordination and what stays independent, focusing on whether communications are planned with, directed by, or materially assisted by a campaign FEC guidance on coordinated and independent expenditures.

Legal tests for coordination are fact-specific; investigators look at timing, message content, and conduct such as shared vendors or direct communications. Because determinations rely on evidence, disputes can end up in enforcement actions or appeals where the factual record is central CRS legal background and enforcement issues.

State rules can vary and sometimes change filing deadlines or disclosure requirements. For races that involve state and federal filings, check both the FEC and the relevant state election office records to get a complete picture Ballotpedia independent expenditure overview.

How candidate spending and independent expenditures are reported differently

Candidate-authorized committees file periodic committee reports that list receipts, expenditures and in-kind contributions; those reports are submitted to the FEC and searchable in the FEC data portal FEC data portal.

Independent expenditures are reported by the spender and appear in separate independent-expenditure filings, which identify the payer, the recipient of the communication when applicable, and the amount spent under FEC rules FEC guidance on coordinated and independent expenditures.

Independent expenditures are paid and reported by outside organizations and are not coordinated with a candidate’s authorized committee, while candidate-controlled spending is conducted and reported by the candidate’s authorized committee.

To decide which report to open first for a particular ad or mailer, ask whether the communication was paid for by the candidate committee or by an outside group that lists the expenditure on its own filings.

Outside-tracking organizations also publish cycle summaries that help identify likely independent spenders and totals, but those summaries should be cross-checked with the primary filings for details and timestamps OpenSecrets outside spending summaries.

Why outside spending increased and the role of Super PACs

Analysts describe growth in independent vehicles, including Super PACs, after the Citizens United decision as a major driver of increased outside spending; these entities accept unlimited contributions for independent political activity and often appear as large spenders in cycles since that ruling Brennan Center analysis of Super PACs and independent expenditures.

Common outside spenders include political action committees that operate independently, nonprofit groups that engage in political communications, and issue-oriented organizations that report independent expenditures when they expressly advocate for or against a candidate. OpenSecrets catalogs these spenders and explains its methodology for cycle totals OpenSecrets outside spending data and methodology.

While these vehicles expanded the channel for outside messaging, they also raised complex questions about coordination and enforcement, because the line between advice and independent action can be contested in specific cases CRS legal background and enforcement issues.

Where voters can check filings: FEC data portal, OpenSecrets, and state sites

The FEC data portal is the primary federal source for searching committee reports and independent-expenditure filings; it allows keyword searches by candidate, committee, and spender and provides access to the underlying documents that support reported amounts FEC data portal.

OpenSecrets provides cycle-level tallies and breakdowns that make outside spending easier to scan, including lists of top independent spenders and summaries of spending by cycle; use these summaries to find likely targets for deeper review in the primary filings OpenSecrets outside spending data.

Decision criteria: what voters should look for when evaluating outside spending

Ask whether the communication contains explicit advocacy. If an ad says vote for or vote against, it is likely an independent expenditure if paid by an outside group and not coordinated with the campaign FEC guidance on coordinated and independent expenditures.

Consider timing. Large payments filed right before a primary or general election can signal influence on the campaign calendar and are worth closer inspection in the detailed filings OpenSecrets outside spending data.

Check for direct ties or communications. Repeated payments to the same vendors as a campaign, shared vendor staff, or public reports of private communications between a spender and a campaign can be evidence to investigate further; such fact-specific questions are the kinds that can lead to enforcement reviews CRS legal background and enforcement issues.

Common mistakes and enforcement questions voters should watch for

A frequent error is assuming any ad that mentions a candidate is coordinated. Many ads are independent expenditures because the spender paid and filed for them separately. Always check the filing source to confirm who reported the payment FEC guidance on coordinated and independent expenditures.

Coordination determinations are often contested and can be pursued through FEC enforcement or appeals. If a determination seems disputed or unclear, look for enforcement entries in the FEC or legal summaries that describe the grounds for contesting coordination claims CRS legal background and enforcement issues.

Guide to primary sources for checking filings

Start with the primary filing documents

Be cautious with aggregated tracker totals. Outside-spending trackers often combine many itemized filings into cycle totals, which is useful for scanning but can obscure the timing or the individual document that explains a payment OpenSecrets outside spending data.

When in doubt about a specific payment or vendor, open the primary filing in the FEC portal and read the item line to see dates, payees, and disclosures that provide the clearest record of who funded the communication FEC data portal (see the contact page).

Practical examples and scenarios: how to read a filing and spot red flags

Start by locating an independent-expenditure item in the FEC portal. Look for the spender name, the date, the amount, and descriptive fields that explain whether the payment was for advertising, consulting, or material distribution; those fields give immediate context for further questions FEC data portal.

Use OpenSecrets to find large spenders quickly, then follow the spender name into the FEC records to read the full filings. OpenSecrets’ summaries can point you to which groups to inspect in the primary documents, but do not replace the filings themselves OpenSecrets outside spending data.

Red flags include repeated payments to vendors that also appear on a candidate committee report, payments dated right after strategic campaign events, or filings that obscure the true payer behind a network of affiliated organizations. These indicators merit reviewing the primary filings and, if necessary, enforcement records for more detail CRS legal background and enforcement issues.

Takeaway and next steps for voters
