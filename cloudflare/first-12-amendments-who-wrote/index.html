What the phrase "first 12 amendments" refers to

The phrase "first 12 amendments" refers to the set of amendments the First Congress proposed on September 25, 1789, and sent to the states under Article V of the Constitution. The House journal records the joint resolution that bundled the proposals for state consideration and transmission under the constitutional amendment process, and that action is the foundation for how historians count the original twelve articles House journal.

Of those twelve proposals, ten were ratified by the states and were declared part of the Constitution on December 15, 1791, now commonly called the Bill of Rights. The National Archives provides a transcript and ratification summary that shows which articles were adopted in 1791 and the effective date when they were recognized as amendments National Archives transcript.

Two of the original twelve did not become part of the Bill of Rights in 1791. One article, an apportionment provision, was not ratified and remains outstanding. The other, a congressional-compensation proposal, was left unratified at the time but was later completed and became the Twenty-seventh Amendment in 1992, following state ratification more than two centuries after 1789 National Archives summary of the amendments.

In short, when readers ask about the "first 12 amendments," they mean the package the First Congress sent to the states in 1789, ten of which were ratified in 1791 and two of which had different later outcomes. These details are visible in the primary legislative records and the archival summaries that document ratification paths House journal.

Short answer: who wrote the first 12 amendments

James Madison prepared the draft amendments that formed the core of the twelve articles offered to Congress in 1789 and is widely regarded as the primary author of those proposals; his draft manuscripts and notes survive in editions such as the Founders Online collection Founders Online collection. For additional context on Madison's congressional presentation, see Madison's speech in support of amendments.

That said, Congress debated and revised the proposals; the package transmitted to the states reflects both Madison's drafting and the legislative editing recorded in House and Senate journals, so authorship is best described as a drafting role plus sponsorship within the First Congress Constitution Annotated.

How Madison prepared the draft amendments

Madison worked from notes, earlier state proposals, and drafts produced in mid-1789; several manuscripts in his papers show dated draft text and reading notes that scholars use to link him to the 1789 package. Those drafts are preserved and made available in documentary editions that reproduce his handwriting and revisions Founders Online collection.

The surviving draft manuscripts show that Madison collected language and ideas he considered important for protecting individual rights and clarifying limits on federal power. Researchers treat these drafts as the documentary basis for saying Madison prepared the proposals, while recognizing that the drafts do not by themselves prove every word in the transmitted articles came unchanged from his pen Original Meanings (Princeton University Press).

Madison's papers also include notes on intent and references to debates in state ratifying conventions and earlier proposals; those contextual materials help historians explain why he framed certain protections as amendments rather than as parts of the original Constitution, and they guide comparison between his drafts and later legislative wording Founders Online collection.

How the First Congress processed the 1789 proposals

The House journal records that the First Congress agreed to propose a set of amendments on September 25, 1789; that journal entry and the accompanying committee and floor proceedings show the procedural steps the House used to consider and consolidate proposals before sending them to the Senate House journal.

In practice, the drafting stage in Madison's papers was followed by committee review, debate on the floor, and some rewording to accommodate votes and legal drafting conventions. The Senate considered related language and in some cases consolidated several suggested articles into a smaller set of transmitted articles, according to legislative summaries and annotated constitutional histories Constitution Annotated. The result of those steps was a package of twelve proposed amendments that reflected both Madison's initial draft choices and the editing processes of committee and floor action in each chamber, which together determined the final text sent to the states under Article V House journal.

What counts as authorship: evidence and criteria

Historians and legal scholars rely on several types of documentary evidence to attribute authorship: dated draft manuscripts, explicit sponsorship records in legislative journals, contemporaneous correspondence, and printed committee reports. These categories let researchers move from plausible claims to evidence-supported statements about role and contribution Founders Online collection. Because Congress debated and edited the proposed articles, authorship of the transmitted text can reasonably be described as shared: Madison supplied draft language and House sponsorship, while the legislative process produced final phrasing through committee work, floor amendments, and Senate consideration Constitution Annotated.

Timeline: from proposal in 1789 to ratification in 1791

Key dates anchor this timeline: the First Congress proposed the twelve amendments on September 25, 1789, and the ten articles that were ratified were declared part of the Constitution on December 15, 1791. These milestones are visible in the congressional records and the National Archives ratification transcript House journal.

Between those dates, the proposed articles circulated to the states for ratification under Article V, and state conventions voted at different times and on different schedules, producing a sequence of state approvals that led to the required number for adoption of the ten ratified articles National Archives transcript. The two proposals left unresolved in 1791 had separate fates: one remained unratified and outstanding, while the compensation article resurfaced over time and achieved final ratification in 1992 as the Twenty-seventh Amendment, reflecting a unique long-term path from proposal to adoption National Archives summary of the amendments.

The unratified apportionment article and the 27th Amendment story

One of the original twelve proposals concerned apportionment and was not ratified in the period that produced the Bill of Rights; it remains an outstanding proposed amendment because enough states did not approve it at the time the others were ratified House journal.

The congressional-compensation proposal, also from the 1789 package, was not ratified with the ten articles in 1791 but later completed the long pause in ratification when states finished their approvals and the amendment was certified in 1992 as the Twenty-seventh Amendment National Archives summary of the amendments.

How scholars assess Madison9s role and remaining research questions

Contemporary scholarship credits Madison as the primary drafter of the proposals offered to the First Congress while emphasizing the legislative editing that shaped the final package. Documentary editions and interpretive histories provide the basis for that consensus and explain the balance between individual drafting and institutional revision Original Meanings (Princeton University Press).

At the same time, scholars continue to investigate detailed attribution questions: which exact phrases in the transmitted articles come from Madison's manuscripts and which phraseologies were introduced in committee reports or floor amendments. Those questions are addressed by comparing manuscript drafts, House journals, and Senate records in documentary editions and annotated constitutional histories Constitution Annotated.

Jack N. Rakove and other historians model how to weigh manuscript evidence against legislative records, showing both why Madison's drafting role is convincing and why some wording changes require careful archival tracing to attribute precisely Original Meanings (Princeton University Press).

Common errors and misconceptions to avoid

A common mistake is to credit Madison with the final wording of the transmitted articles without noting the legislative edits recorded in the House and Senate journals. That shortcut ignores the committee reports and floor votes that shaped the text ultimately sent to the states.

Another error is to assume all twelve original proposals became the Bill of Rights. In fact, only ten were ratified in 1791, one apportionment article was not ratified, and the compensation article followed a very different, much longer ratification path. For accurate summaries, check the National Archives ratification transcript rather than a simplified account National Archives transcript.

How to read the primary sources yourself

Find Madison's draft manuscripts in the Founders Online collection, where images and transcriptions of his 1789 drafts are available; the site includes dates and contextual notes that help identify which drafts relate to the amendment proposals Founders Online collection.

For the congressional record, consult the Library of Congress House journals for the joint resolution and floor entries of September 1789, and use the Constitution Annotated for a modern, annotated guide to how the chambers processed amendment business House journal. You can also consult our constitutional-rights hub for additional material and links.

When comparing manuscripts and journals, look for three features: the draft dating and handwriting that tie a draft to an author, sponsorship and motion records in the journal that show who presented language to the House, and editorial entries that record amendments or consolidations made in committee or on the floor Constitution Annotated.

Practical example: tracing a clause from Madison9s draft to the transmitted articles

Step 1, find the clause in a dated Madison draft in Founders Online and note the exact wording and manuscript variants. The draft image and transcription give the starting point for comparison Founders Online collection.

Step 2, search the House journal entries and any committee reports for the corresponding motion or reported language; record the journal volume, page, and date so you can cite the legislative step that considered the clause House journal.

Step 3, compare the Senate messages and the final transmitted articles to identify wording changes, and annotate each difference with a short note that cites the draft image and the journal entry. This method keeps the chain of evidence clear and lets you show which edits may reflect committee or floor action rather than original drafting Constitution Annotated.

How journalists and educators should report authorship claims

Use cautious attribution language. A neutral model sentence is: "According to his drafts in Founders Online, James Madison prepared the proposals later offered in the House." That phrasing names the primary source and ties authorship to documentary evidence rather than an absolute claim Founders Online collection.

When space allows, add a short clause noting legislative editing, for example: "Madison prepared draft language, and the First Congress debated and edited the proposals before sending them to the states." That balance helps readers understand the shared nature of authorship without overstating individual credit Constitution Annotated.

Conclusion: what we can reliably say about who wrote the first 12 amendments

Based on surviving manuscripts and the legislative record, James Madison prepared the draft language that formed the core of the twelve amendments proposed by the First Congress in 1789; this conclusion rests on dated draft manuscripts in Madison's papers and the documentary editions that reproduce them Founders Online collection.

At the same time, Congress edited and consolidated proposals before transmitting them to the states, so the transmitted articles reflect both Madison's drafting and the legislative process recorded in House and Senate journals.
