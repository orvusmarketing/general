Florida vote by mail rules: how signature verification works

County officials verify the voter signature on the ballot certificate by comparing it to the signature on the voter registration record. This comparison is performed by local staff following county procedures that operate within state guidance Division of Elections vote-by-mail.

Signatures may appear mismatched for many ordinary reasons, including differences in handwriting over time, use of a different pen, or a full name versus a shortened signature. If a signature or other defect is found, the cure process explained

When a county detects a signature issue or other defect such as a missing oath, officials are required to notify the voter and offer an opportunity to cure the problem. Notification methods and cure steps vary by county. Common reasons Florida mail ballots are rejected

Late arrival is a frequent reason a mail ballot is rejected. Whether a late ballot can be accepted depends on mailing deadlines, postmarks, and county rules, so check both the Division guidance and your county's instructions for specifics on what counts as timely Division of Elections vote-by-mail.

Missing signatures or a missing oath on the ballot certificate are routine causes for rejection. If a certificate lacks the voter signature or the required attestation, the county will typically issue a notice and offer an opportunity to cure where allowed Ballotpedia Florida absentee voting.

A signature mismatch is another leading documented cause of rejection. Nonpartisan observers summarize that common mismatches include names written differently over time and signatures that do not include recognizable elements used on the registration record, and counties describe review processes on their pages Broward County vote by mail.

If your mail ballot is rejected, options and next steps

If a mail ballot remains un-cured and is rejected, a voter may be able to cast a provisional ballot in person on Election Day. Provisional ballots allow a voter to participate while the county resolves whether the original mail ballot can be counted under local rules Florida Statutes, Chapter 101.
