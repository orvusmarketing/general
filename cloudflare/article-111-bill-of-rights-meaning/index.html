What "Article 111" can mean: a clear definition and context

Start by treating article numbers as coordinates, not universal rules. The phrase article 111 bill of rights alone does not identify a legal text or show what is created. The meaning depends on the named constitution and on how the article is numbered.

Readers should name the constitution or instrument before interpreting an article. Best practice is to check the authoritative text and translation rather than relying on secondhand summaries, because notation differences such as Arabic numerals and Roman numerals change what a cited article refers to. Why article numbers are not universal

Constitutions use their own numbering systems. An article number in one instrument can cover a topic that is entirely different in another instrument. The same numeric label can therefore create confusion if the constitution is not named.

What readers usually mean by "Article 111"

Many readers cite Article 111 when they mean an article numbered 111 in a particular national constitution. Others mistake notation and intend a differently numbered article, such as Article III in the U.S. Constitution. Avoid ambiguous shorthand by naming the constitution in your first sentence.

article 111 bill of rights

When you see the phrase article 111 bill of rights, pause and ask which constitution is referenced and whether the notation uses Arabic numerals or Roman numerals. That one check prevents a common source of error.

How constitutional notation and numbering work

Constitutions use several organizational patterns: articles, chapters, sections, and schedules. The basic unit is the article, but some documents number articles sequentially while others bundle numbered parts under headings. Encyclopedias and research guides explain these conventions in plain terms Encyclopaedia Britannica and the ICL resource at the University of Bern ICL

Roman numerals are a separate convention. For example, the U.S. Constitution labels its articles using Roman numerals. Reading Roman numeral III as Arabic 111 is a notation error that may change the substance of a claim.

Because numbering is a drafting choice, the same article number can address institution creation in one country and a narrow procedural duty in another. Treat the number as a locator and verify the actual text before concluding that it creates an office or process.

A step-by-step framework to verify what an cited Article 111 creates

Step 1: Identify the constitution or instrument. Record the country or the specific charter and the edition or amendment date, if known. This simple identification often resolves the uncertainty about whether the citation refers to an Arabic 111 or a Roman numeral III.

Step 2: Locate the authoritative text or translation. Prefer official government publications or reputable translations such as the Constitute Project. If the text has been amended, check the amendment history to confirm the article's current wording Constitute Project

What Article 111 creates depends entirely on the named constitution and the article text; it can create a temporary council, prescribe a procedural duty, or mean something else depending on the instrument and notation.

Step 3: Read the article and nearby provisions. The article's immediate context, cross references, and chapter headings show whether the provision creates an institution, prescribes a procedure, or records a single duty. Note amendment dates and any transitional provisions.

Decision criteria: how to judge what the article actually creates

Signal 1: Text that creates an office will typically name an office, define its powers, and set appointment or tenure rules. If the article lists duties without naming an office, it is more likely prescribing a procedure than creating an institution.

Signal 2: Look for language about 'shall' and 'is established' as indicators of creation, while procedural language often uses 'shall assent', 'shall refer', or 'shall be presented'. When in doubt, quote the exact wording and avoid paraphrase without citation to the primary text National Constitution Center

Signal 3: Use amendment history and cross references to test whether the provision is standalone or part of a broader institutional framework. Commentary helps to interpret unclear drafting choices, but only the primary text creates legal authority.

Common mistakes and pitfalls when someone cites Article 111

Confusing Article 111 with Article III is one of the most frequent mistakes in English language reporting. The two look similar when typed, but one is Arabic notation and the other is Roman, and they can refer to materially different subjects.

Another common pitfall is failing to name the constitution or to cite the source. Reporting that 'Article 111 creates X' without a direct link or quoted text leaves readers unable to verify the claim. Always attach the exact article text or a primary source link when possible National Constitution Center

Deep dive: what Article 111 creates in the Constitution of Iran

The post-1989 text of the Constitution of the Islamic Republic of Iran includes a provision numbered Article 111 that sets out a temporary council to carry out the duties of the Supreme Leader when the office is vacant. The provision is explicitly transitional and temporary in character Constitute Project

Practically, the clause means the constitution supplies a mechanism to ensure continuity of leadership until the Assembly of Experts convenes and chooses a new Supreme Leader. Secondary commentary places this provision within Iran's broader succession framework, noting it is a stopgap measure rather than a permanent office Encyclopaedia Iranica

When summarizing this Article 111, reporters should quote the exact constitutional text and cite the authoritative translation. That approach makes clear that the article establishes a temporary council and not a new permanent institution.

Deep dive: what Article 111 covers in the Constitution of India

Article 111 of the Indian Constitution addresses the President's assent to bills passed by Parliament. The article sets out the procedural options available to the President when a bill is presented for assent and therefore governs a legislative step rather than creating an office Constitute Project

The practical effect is that Article 111 is part of the legislative machinery: readers should treat it as procedural text that signals how laws reach final form. This counters any assumption that the article creates an institution or an office.

For precise reporting, quote the article's text and indicate whether you are using an official translation or a secondary commentary. That transparency prevents readers from assuming procedural language creates institutional powers.

Deep dive: why "Article 111" is sometimes confused with Article III in U.S. contexts

The U.S. Constitution writes its articles in Roman numerals; Article III creates the federal judiciary and federal courts. If someone refers to Article 111 when they mean U.S. law, they are usually misreading Roman III as Arabic 111 Legal Information Institute

The substance of Article III is about judicial power and the structure of the federal court system. A notation error that substitutes 111 for III changes the topic entirely and can lead to inaccurate reporting.

How journalists and researchers should cite Article 111 correctly

Quote the exact article text and provide a direct link to the authoritative source. Include the constitution name, the translation used, and the amendment date if relevant. That minimal metadata lets readers verify your summary quickly.

When you rely on commentary, attribute interpretation to the commentator and keep the primary text central. Example attribution language includes: according to the constitution, the article states; the official translation reads; legal commentary notes.
