The actual text of the Seventh Amendment (verbatim) and a plainred-language reading

Verbatim transcription from the Bill of Rights

The following is the exact wording of the Seventh Amendment as transcribed in the Bill of Rights, a primary source for the amendment text National Archives Bill of Rights transcription.

In Suits at common law, where the value in controversy shall exceed twenty dollars, the right of trial by jury shall be preserved, and no fact tried by a jury, shall be otherwise re-examined in any Court of the United States, than according to the rules of the common law.

Line-by-line plain-language paraphrase

"In Suits at common law" refers to civil actions that, at the time of the founding, were handled as actions at law rather than equitable proceedings. Modern readers can think of this phrase as delimiting the kinds of civil claims where the jury right traditionally applies.

"Where the value in controversy shall exceed twenty dollars" is historical language describing a threshold that the founding text used. It does not mean courts apply a literal twenty-dollar rule today; interpretive summaries explain the clause as historical phrasing that helped define the scope of early jury practice Legal Information Institute summary.

"The right of trial by jury shall be preserved" states that when a claim falls within the Amendment's scope, the jury's role in deciding facts must be maintained and protected.

"No fact tried by a jury, shall be otherwise re-examined in any Court of the United States, than according to the rules of the common law" bars courts from re-deciding facts that a properly constituted jury has found, except in the limited ways that common-law practice allows. Authoritative annotations explain how that protection shapes modern judicial review of jury findings Constitution Annotated discussion and the GPO Constitution Annotated PDF GPO-CONAN PDF.

What the Amendment protects: jury factred-finding and limits on judges

The jury's factred-finding role

The Amendment safeguards the jury as the primary finder of facts in qualifying federal civil cases. That core interpretation appears in leading legal summaries and annotations that trace the text to its purpose of preserving jury determinations Legal Information Institute summary.

Does the Seventh Amendment protect all civil suits?

The Seventh Amendment's verbatim wording appears in the Bill of Rights transcription and protects the right to trial by jury in certain civil suits while restricting courts from re-examining jury-found facts. For the exact wording and interpretive guidance, consult the National Archives transcription and authoritative annotations such as the Constitution Annotated.

In practice, the right protects fact-finding in those civil claims that fit the law-versus-equity distinction and related doctrines. Short examples can clarify the difference between claims where a jury typically decides facts and those where equitable remedies reduce or remove the jury role.

Limits on judicial re-examination

The clause that restricts re-examination prevents courts from substituting their own factual conclusions for those reached by a jury, except where established common-law procedures permit it. The Constitution Annotated describes how courts balance verdict finality with limited judicial relief Constitution Annotated discussion.

Common-law exceptions include narrowly defined post-trial procedures that allow courts to set aside or adjust verdicts in particular circumstances. Those procedures are constrained so that judicial oversight does not nullify the jury's fact-finding role.

Scope and limits: when the Seventh Amendment applies and when it does not

Suits at common law versus equitable remedies

The Amendment traditionally applies to suits at law, while equitable remedies have historically proceeded without a jury. The Constitution Annotated explains this law-versus-equity line and how it affects a party's right to a jury Constitution Annotated discussion.

In modern practice the distinction often turns on the remedy sought. If a plaintiff seeks monetary damages, a jury right is more likely to apply. If the remedy is injunctive or other equitable relief, a jury right may be limited.

Federal-only application and incorporation

The Seventh Amendment is primarily a federal protection and has not been broadly incorporated to apply against the states. The Legal Information Institute and Constitution Annotated note that states generally set their own civil jury rules through state constitutions and statutes Legal Information Institute summary.

That means readers should check both federal procedure and state law to determine whether a state-level case offers a similar jury right.

Historical background: English common law and why drafters included it

Roots in English jury practice

The Seventh Amendment reflects a long English common-law tradition that valued jury fact-finding as a check on judicial power. Historical summaries link the text to English practice and to the framers' intent to preserve that role National Constitution Center overview.

At the founding, preserving the jury's power to decide facts was a mechanism to limit judicial encroachment and protect litigants' rights. The historical language in the amendment shows that lineage.

Founding-era debates and ratification

The Seventh Amendment was ratified as part of the Bill of Rights on December 15, 1791, a date recorded in the National Archives transcription that serves as the primary text source National Archives Bill of Rights transcription.

While history informs interpretation, modern doctrine has evolved through later case law and procedural rules that shape how the Amendment operates in 21st century litigation.

Practical steps: how a party typically demands a jury in federal civil court

Timing and form of a jury demand

Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 38 requires a party to demand a jury trial in a specified form and within particular timeframes; failure to follow the rule can risk waiver of the right Federal Rules of Civil Procedure Rule 38.

Typical steps include checking the court's local rules, filing a written demand in a timely manner, and confirming the demand is entered on the docket. Parties often note the demand on the civil cover sheet or in an initial responsive pleading when required.

How courts may re-examine jury facts: motions, standards, and limits

Judgment as a matter of law and new trials

Court tools that affect jury verdicts include motions for judgment as a matter of law and motions for a new trial. These remedies are narrowly circumscribed so that they do not override the Seventh Amendment's protection of jury fact-finding, as explained in doctrinal annotations Constitution Annotated discussion.

Judgment as a matter of law asks the court to find that no reasonable jury could have reached a given factual conclusion. Motions for new trial question the reliability of the verdict process, but the Amendment limits courts from substituting their own factual findings without following common-law rules.

Appellate review limits

On appeal, courts review legal claims and may review whether the trial court correctly applied standards for post-trial relief. Appellate oversight is constrained by the principle that jury findings deserve deference except in well-defined legal circumstances.

Because the balance between finality and review is fact- and procedure-specific, annotations and the FRCP remain the right places to check precise standards and procedures.

State courts and incorporation: does the Seventh Amendment bind states?

Incorporation doctrine overview

The Seventh Amendment has not been broadly incorporated against the states, so its federal wording operates chiefly in federal courts. The Constitution Annotated and legal summaries describe how incorporation doctrine affects the Amendment's reach Constitution Annotated discussion.

State courts rely on their own constitutions and statutes for civil jury rights, and those state rules can vary from the federal approach.

How states handle civil jury rights

In many states, similar protections exist through state constitutional language or statutes, but the scope and procedures differ. Readers should check their state constitution and state procedural rules for precise guidance.
