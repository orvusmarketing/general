What are american civic values? A clear definition and context

American civic values refer to the shared principles that guide public life and citizen behavior in the United States, including commitments to civic duty, the rule of law, tolerance, participation, respect for rights, and equality. According to federal guidance on civic education, these values are central to how schools and civic programs describe what citizens should know and practice U.S. Department of Education civics resources.

Examples of civic values include civic duty, rule of law, tolerance, participation, respect for rights, and equality. These values guide behavior and institutions, and they are taught alongside knowledge and skills so people can apply them through voting, volunteering, civil discussion, and other civic activities.

At a basic level, values are beliefs or standards about how public life should operate, distinct from factual knowledge about institutions or the practical skills people use when engaging in civic life. The Library of Congress frames civic examples such as rights and responsibilities in a way that helps educators connect historical documents to present-day civic norms Library of Congress primary source set.

Commonly listed examples of american civic values include civic duty, the rule of law, tolerance, broad participation, respect for individual rights, and equality. Legal and professional organizations also emphasize that the rule of law and respect for rights are foundational to orderly public life and functioning institutions American Bar Association resources on rule of law.

Common examples listed by education and civic organizations

Education and civic organizations typically use short lists of values to guide curricula and public programs. These lists function as reference points for classroom activities, community discussions, and public outreach, and often mirror the values named above in federal and library resources U.S. Department of Education civics resources.

How values differ from civic skills and knowledge

Values describe what citizens consider important. Civic knowledge is factual information about how government works. Civic skills are practices like deliberation, media literacy, and civic reasoning. Curriculum guidance stresses that teaching works best when these three elements are paired, so learners can apply knowledge through skills while guided by civic dispositions National Council for the Social Studies guidance.

Legal and institutional foundations

Legal and professional bodies frame certain values as core to public order. For example, bar associations and legal educators highlight the rule of law and respect for rights as essentials that support peaceful civic life and protect individuals in the public square American Bar Association resources on rule of law.

Why civic values matter: civic health, public knowledge, and participation

Civic values matter because they shape how people interact with institutions and one another. Where those values are upheld, communities tend to have clearer expectations about rights, responsibilities, and acceptable public behavior. National reports show that many Americans continue to support democratic norms, even as civic knowledge gaps persist Pew Research Center analysis.

Gaps in civic knowledge can make it harder for citizens to use their rights effectively or to judge claims about policy and public officials. Civic-health reports document uneven participation and knowledge across localities, which helps explain why civic values are an ongoing topic for education and community programs National Conference on Citizenship civic health index.

Beyond individual knowledge, civic values undergird democratic norms such as peaceful transitions of power, respect for minority rights, and open deliberation. These norms rely on a baseline acceptance of institutions and procedures; professional bodies argue that protecting rights and upholding the rule of law are necessary to sustain those norms American Bar Association resources on rule of law.

Since 2024, coalitions and grant initiatives have increased attention to civic education and to teaching core american civic values, but the measurable impact of those investments is still being tracked. Education leaders note that funding and new coalitions are steps toward strengthening civic learning, even as researchers continue to evaluate outcomes U.S. Department of Education civics resources.

How civic education teaches civic values: competencies, skills, and dispositions

Contemporary civic-education guidance recommends a competencies approach that pairs factual knowledge with civic skills and civic dispositions. The model aims to help learners move from knowing about institutions to participating effectively and respectfully in civic life National Council for the Social Studies guidance.

The competencies approach used by curriculum guidance

Under a competencies model, curricula offer facts about government alongside activities that build discussion skills, evidence evaluation, and media literacy. The goal is to form citizens who can reason about civic issues and make informed choices, rather than memorizing facts alone U.S. Department of Education civics resources.

Key civic skills: deliberation, media literacy, civic reasoning

Schools and civic programs list practical skills such as deliberation, evaluating sources, and media literacy. These skills help people identify reliable information, weigh competing claims, and participate in civic conversations without undermining respectful discourse National Council for the Social Studies guidance.

Civic dispositions: respect, tolerance, participation

Civic dispositions are habits of mind and character, including respect for others, tolerance of differing views, and a willingness to take part in community life. Educators and libraries often design activities to practice these dispositions alongside lessons about rights and responsibilities Library of Congress primary source set.

Everyday practices that express civic values

Many civic values are most visible in ordinary actions. Voting is a central example: it expresses participation and civic duty by allowing citizens to choose representatives and influence public policy Library of Congress primary source set.

Volunteering and community service demonstrate care for the common good. Local boards, nonprofit work, and neighborhood initiatives are concrete ways people practice values like responsibility and solidarity in daily life U.S. Department of Education civics resources.

Contacting elected officials and attending public meetings are practical forms of accountability and participation. These actions allow citizens to raise concerns, request information, and push for changes while reinforcing the connection between voters and government institutions Library of Congress primary source set.

Civil discussion, including respectful engagement across differences, reflects values of tolerance and respect for rights. Programs that teach deliberation and media literacy also stress the importance of checking information before sharing it and of listening to opposing views National Council for the Social Studies guidance.

A practical framework for evaluating civic claims and policies

To evaluate a claim or policy against civic values, use decision criteria such as adherence to the rule of law, protection of individual rights, inclusiveness of participation, and the presence of clear evidence. These criteria help separate slogans from practices that actually safeguard democratic norms American Bar Association resources on rule of law.

Next, check sources and evidence. Apply media literacy skills: identify the origin of a claim, look for primary documents or filings where appropriate, and review neutral public records when a candidate or official makes a factual claim U.S. Department of Education civics resources.

When evaluating candidate claims about fundraising or committee activity, consult public filings such as FEC records rather than secondary summaries. Combining document checks with civic reasoning yields a clearer view of whether a claim aligns with stated values U.S. Department of Education civics resources.

Common mistakes and pitfalls when discussing civic values

One common mistake is conflating slogans with measurable outcomes. Phrases that sound like values can be useful as rhetoric, but they do not substitute for evidence or program evaluations; readers should look for documented outcomes before accepting claims about impact Pew Research Center analysis.

Overstating program impacts without evidence weakens civic discussion. Civic-health reports show mixed results across communities, so it is important to check program evaluations and avoid broad claims about success when data are limited National Conference on Citizenship civic health index.

Polarizing language and dehumanizing phrasing can undermine civic dispositions such as respect and tolerance. Maintaining neutral, fact-focused language and avoiding absolute guarantees helps preserve space for constructive debate and shared problem solving National Council for the Social Studies guidance.

Practical scenarios and next steps for readers

Scenario 1, school board: If a local school board discusses a civics curriculum, attend the meeting, review the proposed materials, and ask how the curriculum pairs knowledge with skills and dispositions. Use primary resources to ask specific questions about learning goals Library of Congress primary source set.

Scenario 2, neighborhood meeting: If neighbors are debating a local ordinance, listen to differing views, request factual documents, and suggest a brief deliberative format where each side presents sources and proposed outcomes. This approach helps center evidence and respect in local decisions U.S. Department of Education civics resources.

Scenario 3, contacting a representative: If you decide to contact a member of Congress, prepare a concise note that states the issue, cites one factual source, and asks a specific question. This practice models respectful, accountability-focused civic participation Library of Congress primary source set.

By linking values with knowledge and skills, communities can make civic participation more inclusive and effective.
