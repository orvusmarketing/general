What a congressman does: three core duties at a glance

Lawmaking

Early in any description of what does a congressman do is a straightforward list of institutional responsibilities: members write and consider legislation, review proposed laws, and participate in votes that shape federal policy and funding. According to a plain description of the legislative process, lawmaking includes introducing bills, committee review, amendment, and floor consideration before a bill becomes law Congress.gov legislative process

Oversight

Members also exercise oversight, which means monitoring how federal programs and agencies implement laws and spend funds. Oversight can take many forms, from asking questions in committee to requesting agency records that reveal whether a program is operating as intended. The House explains these institutional roles as part of its core responsibilities House of Representatives overview

Representation and constituent services

Representation includes direct help for people who live in a member's district, often called constituent services or congressional casework, which is an ongoing office function that many members prioritize alongside lawmaking and oversight. Institutional guidance describes constituent services as a routine part of a member's office operations CRS constituent casework overview

Casework typically focuses on individual issues a constituent faces with a federal agency, such as benefit processing or information requests. When offices take a case, they act as an intermediary to obtain information, request expedited handling, or refer a constituent to the appropriate agency unit. This practical role helps translate a member's oversight role into assistance for specific residents House of Representatives overview

Offices open a case, obtain authorization if needed, refer the matter to the agency liaison, follow up, and inform the constituent of outcomes; agencies like SSA and VA maintain designated congressional inquiry processes.

How casework fits into an office's daily work

Why offices do casework

Constituent services serve two purposes: they address individual problems and they surface patterns that can inform oversight or legislation. Offices collect recurring issues to identify possible agency or program problems that merit further review or committee attention CRS constituent casework overview

Who handles constituent requests in a district office

Most district offices assign dedicated staff to handle intake and case management, often under titles like constituent services representative or district caseworker. These staffers are the primary point of contact for constituents seeking help with federal agencies and they coordinate with staff who specialize in particular program areas when needed House of Representatives overview

Basic intake and recordkeeping practices

When a constituent reaches out, an office typically opens a case file, records the issue, assigns a case number, and requests any needed authorizations to contact agencies on the constituent's behalf. Offices follow documented intake procedures to maintain privacy and to make sure agencies can respond to inquiries efficiently CRS constituent casework overview (Managing Congressional Constituent Service Inquiries)

Good intake practices reduce delays. Constituents who provide necessary identification, contact details, and any relevant benefit or claim numbers help staff open and route cases faster. District staff commonly track case progress in an office database and give constituents a case number to reference in future inquiries. Step-by-step casework workflow used by most offices

Intake and authorization

First, the office confirms who is making the request and whether they are the constituent or an authorized representative. Many agencies require a signed consent or authorization before they will release private records to a congressional office, so the intake stage often includes instructions for submitting that form SSA congressional inquiries guidance

Agency referral and liaison

Next, staff create a case file and refer the matter to the appropriate agency liaison or the agency office that handles congressional inquiries. Agencies such as the Social Security Administration maintain formal channels and contacts to respond to congressional requests, which can shorten information flow when used correctly SSA congressional inquiries guidance

Follow-up and closure

Offices track progress through follow-ups with the agency and with the constituent. When the agency responds, the office updates the case file, notifies the constituent, and closes the case after documenting the resolution or next steps. Offices often provide a case number and a summary of actions taken to keep the constituent informed CRS constituent casework overview

Throughout the workflow, staff balance privacy requirements, the constituent's requested outcome, and the agency's authority. The need for signed authorizations and complete documentation is a common reason an intake cannot proceed until the constituency provides the necessary forms SSA congressional inquiries guidance

Working with federal agencies: SSA, VA, and other common partners

How agencies respond to congressional inquiries

Federal agencies usually designate a congressional liaison or office to handle requests from members of Congress and their staff. The Social Security Administration and the Department of Veterans Affairs publish clear procedures for how they accept and respond to congressional inquiries, which offices use to route constituent cases appropriately SSA congressional inquiries guidance

Designated congressional liaisons and expected benefits

Using a designated liaison often speeds communication because the agency maintains a point of contact familiar with congressional procedures. For constituents, this can mean faster confirmation of case status or clearer instructions about additional evidence the agency requires VA congressional inquiries and assistance

When an agency may require additional documentation

Even with a congressional referral, agencies may still ask for forms, medical evidence, or other documentation before taking action. Constituents should expect that some matters require agency-specific paperwork and that staff will advise which documents are necessary to move the case forward VA congressional inquiries and assistance

Timing and outcomes: what to expect and common limitations

Typical timelines by issue complexity

Timelines vary widely. Simple information requests can resolve in days to weeks, while appeals, benefit adjudications, or cases that require agency investigation can take months. There is no published uniform median across agencies, so constituents should plan for variable timeframes when seeking assistance SSA congressional inquiries guidance

What offices can and cannot do

Congressional staff can request information, escalate a file within an agency, and help a constituent understand next steps, but they cannot unilaterally change agency decisions. Offices may ask the agency to review a decision or to explain a process, and they can direct constituents to appeals routes such as agency review boards or inspectors general when appropriate CRS constituent casework overview

When to escalate beyond the office

If a case remains unresolved after sustained follow-up, practical escalation options include contacting the agency ombudsman or the inspector general. The office can advise these steps and provide contact points, but escalation is typically a later-stage measure after the office has exhausted routine inquiry channels SSA congressional inquiries guidance

Common mistakes and pitfalls for constituents seeking help

Missing authorization or documentation

A leading cause of delay is not providing signed consent or sufficient documentation. Agencies commonly require explicit written permission before releasing protected records to a member's office, so failing to supply a completed authorization form often pauses a case at intake SSA congressional inquiries guidance

Unclear request scope

Vague or overly broad requests slow intake. A clear, specific description of the problem, including relevant dates, claim or benefit numbers, and the desired outcome, helps staff route the case correctly and set realistic expectations for resolution CRS constituent casework overview

Expecting immediate decisions

Constituents sometimes expect a congressional office to produce a fast agency decision. In reality, offices can open inquiries and request review, but they cannot guarantee a change in an agency's decision. Advice and follow-up are typical outcomes rather than immediate reversals House of Representatives overview

Practical example scenarios: step-by-step for common cases

SSA benefits query example

1) Intake: A constituent calls the district office with a question about a Social Security benefit payment. Staff ask for the constituent's name, claim number if available, and a photo ID. They send an authorization form to allow the office to speak with the SSA on the constituent's behalf. The SSA describes its congressional inquiry process on its website SSA congressional inquiries guidance

2) Agency referral: Once the signed authorization arrives, staff open a case file, assign a case number, and forward the inquiry to the SSA congressional liaison. The liaison confirms receipt and provides an expected timeframe for a status update.

VA benefits escalation example

1) Intake: A veteran asks for help with a benefits appeal. The office requests the claimant's VA file number, any recent decision letters, and a signed release form so the VA can share records. The VA notes that congressional inquiries follow published processes and may need supporting medical documents VA congressional inquiries and assistance

2) Follow-up: District staff work with the VA liaison to clarify what documents are missing and whether the appeal requires additional review steps. Because appeals often involve complex evidence reviews, timelines can be longer than for simple status checks.

Non-benefits request: passport processing

1) Intake: For passport delays, a constituent provides the application number and proof of identity. The office confirms the timeline and refers the case to the appropriate State Department or passport agency contact.

2) Outcome: The passport agency commonly responds with processing status or instructions for missing documentation. The speed of response depends on the agency workflow and whether expedited handling is possible.

Wrapping up: where to find help and what to ask next

Contacting your member's office

To begin, contact your representative's district or Washington office and ask for the constituent services or casework team. Provide a concise problem statement and request what documents the office needs to open a case. Public House materials describe standard office roles and how they serve constituents

Documents to have ready

Typical documents include a photo ID, any benefit or claim numbers, relevant decision letters, and a signed authorization form permitting the office to communicate with the agency on your behalf. Having these items ready speeds intake and reduces back-and-forth requests SSA congressional inquiries guidance

When to contact an agency directly

If the office advises that an appeal or a formal agency process is required, contact the agency's appeal office, ombudsman, or inspector general as appropriate. Congressional staff can guide those next steps, but some matters require direct engagement with the agency's review or appeal mechanisms CRS constituent casework overview
