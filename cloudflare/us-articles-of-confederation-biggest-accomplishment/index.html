Quick answer: the biggest accomplishment in one paragraph

Short thesis - us articles of confederation

The single most durable accomplishment of the Articles of Confederation was creating a legal and administrative framework for western lands and state admission, chiefly through the Land Ordinance of 1785 and the Northwest Ordinance of 1787, which organized surveying, sale, and a clear path from territory to statehood Congressional Research Service report on the Northwest Ordinance.

How historians summarize the accomplishment

Historians stress that these ordinances produced institutional practices that outlasted the Confederation itself, shaping later federal territorial policy even after the Constitution addressed other weaknesses National Constitution Center essay on the Articles. What the Articles of Confederation were and how the government functioned

Structure of the Confederation Congress

The Articles of Confederation created a union of sovereign states with a single legislative body, the Confederation Congress, which carried out national duties but had limited central authority, as shown in the original documents hosted by the National Archives Articles of Confederation and related records at the National Archives.

Powers the national government had and lacked

The Confederation Congress could conduct diplomacy, manage western lands, and admit new states, but it could not levy a general tax, had no separate executive, and lacked strong enforcement mechanisms, constraints that scholars link to the system's practical limits Encyclopaedia Britannica entry on the Articles of Confederation.

How the Articles enabled settlement and governance of western lands

The Land Ordinance of 1785: surveying and sale

The Land Ordinance of 1785 established a standardized rectangular survey system that divided new territory into townships and sections for orderly sale and settlement; that practical method reduced disputes over claims and created predictable procedures for transferring public land Congressional Research Service report on the Northwest Ordinance and the Library of Congress guide Northwest Ordinance guide.

The Articles' most durable accomplishment was establishing a procedural framework for western land surveying, sale, and a clear route from territory to statehood through the Land Ordinance of 1785 and the Northwest Ordinance of 1787.

How the Ordinances set a path from territory to state

The Northwest Ordinance of 1787 created a staged process in which territories first received appointed governments and could later achieve elected self-government and statehood once population thresholds were met, embedding a repeatable model for expansion National Archives records on the Articles and related enactments and the milestone Northwest Ordinance text at the National Archives Northwest Ordinance milestone.

What the Northwest Ordinance added: rights and slavery restrictions

Civil liberties included in the Ordinance

The Northwest Ordinance included provisions protecting religious freedom and trial by jury, framing early commitments to civil liberties in territorial governance and influencing later federal practice Library of Congress resources on the Northwest Ordinance.

The explicit prohibition on slavery in the Northwest Territory

The Ordinance contained an explicit ban on slavery in the Northwest Territory, which had immediate consequences for settlement patterns there and became a notable early federal constraint on slavery in new states Congressional Research Service report on the Northwest Ordinance.

Diplomacy and the Treaty of Paris under the Confederation

What the Treaty of Paris (1783) accomplished

The Confederation government negotiated and concluded the Treaty of Paris in 1783, securing British recognition of American independence and establishing international boundaries that were essential for early U.S. diplomacy and trade Encyclopaedia Britannica discussion of the Treaty and diplomacy.

Limits on Confederation diplomacy

Despite diplomatic successes, the Confederation could not reliably enforce treaty terms or regulate commerce without fiscal power, a limit scholars note when assessing the government's international effectiveness Journal article on diplomacy and the Confederation.

Why the Articles struggled: fiscal and enforcement weaknesses

No power to tax and consequences for war debt

Under the Articles the national government lacked authority to levy general taxes and depended on state contributions, which left it unable to retire war debt and created sustained fiscal stress that motivated calls for reform National Archives primary texts and notes.

Weak enforcement and interstate trade problems

Without a strong executive or effective enforcement tools, the Confederation Congress struggled to ensure compliance with national decisions and to regulate interstate commerce, practical defects highlighted by contemporaries and later analysts Diplomatic History article on debt and diplomacy.

How historians assess the Articles today

Consensus: mixed legacy

Modern syntheses characterize the Articles' record as mixed, with durable accomplishments on territorial policy paired with fiscal and enforcement failures that the Constitution later addressed National Constitution Center essay on the Articles' legacy.

Open questions scholars still debate

Scholars continue to debate the balance between Confederation-level action and state initiatives in diplomacy and how directly the Ordinances shaped later federal territorial practice, questions that recent essays and reports frame for further research CRS report on territorial government and the Northwest Ordinance.

Common mistakes and misconceptions

Overclaiming the national government’s power under the Articles

A common error is to assume the Confederation could tax or enforce national law like a later federal government; contemporaneous limits in the Articles themselves show a different balance of power Articles of Confederation text at the National Archives.

Misreading the Ordinances as federal policy identical to later constitutional law

Another mistake is reading the Land and Northwest Ordinances as identical in force to later constitutional arrangements; they were important precedents, but their legal and political context differed from the post-1789 constitutional order National Constitution Center on what the Articles left behind.

Concrete examples and short case studies

How a surveyed township was sold under the 1785 system

Under the 1785 system, a township was divided into 36 sections, with section numbering and a standard lot size, and parcels were offered for public sale under set terms, a process that made individual transactions more predictable and reduced competing claims CRS explanation of the surveying and sale system.

A territorial path to statehood under the Northwest Ordinance

A territory typically moved from an appointed government to an elected assembly and, after meeting population thresholds and other conditions, applied for statehood, a staged sequence that the Ordinance codified and that later expansions often followed National Archives resources on the Ordinances.

How to evaluate accomplishments versus failures: a brief checklist

Criteria to use when weighing historical impact

Use clear criteria: durability over time, legal precedent, effective enforcement, and long-term policy effects; these measure whether an action counts as a lasting accomplishment or a short-term success.

How to apply the checklist to the Articles

Applied to the Articles, the land ordinances score highly on durability and precedent, while diplomatic and fiscal outcomes score unevenly because enforcement and funding were weaker, a judgment reflected in recent syntheses Essay on the Articles' mixed legacy.

Conclusion: the Articles left a mixed but consequential record

Summary takeaway

The best short summary is that the Articles of Confederation most durably delivered a framework for western settlement and state admission, especially through the Land Ordinance of 1785 and the Northwest Ordinance of 1787, even as fiscal and enforcement weaknesses limited other national powers CRS report on territorial government.
