The Supreme Court decision and its central holding

The Supreme Court decided the case on March 9, 1964, and it reversed the Alabama judgment, explaining that the First Amendment places limits on state libel awards involving public officials Law Cornell (LII) opinion text.

The Court announced that public officials seeking damages for defamatory falsehoods about their official conduct must show the statement was made with "actual malice," defined as knowledge of falsity or reckless disregard for the truth, as summarized in case resources Oyez case summary. What the 'actual malice' standard means in practice

The Court?s definition requires a plaintiff who is a public official to show a defamatory statement was made with knowledge that it was false or with reckless disregard for whether it was true, as the opinion sets out Law Cornell (LII) opinion text.

In practice, courts contrast actual malice with ordinary negligence; negligence concerns careless mistakes, while actual malice requires a higher state of mind regarding the truth of the statement, a distinction legal summaries explain Oyez case summary.

Examples courts consider include cases where reporting relied on sources known to be unreliable or where obvious indicators of falsity were ignored; such fact patterns help illustrate how reckless disregard may be shown in specific suits Law Cornell (LII) opinion text.

Common misunderstandings about the parties and the holding

A common error is thinking Sullivan was named in the advertisement; in fact, he was not named, and the suit concerned alleged reflections on his official conduct, a detail clarified in the opinion Law Cornell (LII) opinion text.
