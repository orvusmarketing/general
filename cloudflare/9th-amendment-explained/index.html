What the Ninth Amendment says and why it was included

The Ninth Amendment states that listing certain rights does not mean other rights do not exist, and it appears as part of the Bill of Rights adopted in the founding era.

The Amendment’s short text was intended as a reassurance that the Constitution does not deny unenumerated rights retained by the people, according to an annotated review of the amendment text Ninth Amendment - U.S. Constitution Annotated, and the Constitution Center offers interpretations of the Ninth as well Interpretation: The Ninth Amendment - Constitution Center.

The phrase is concise, and early supporters framed it as a clarifying statement so that enumerating rights would not be read to destroy or limit other rights retained by the people, a point emphasized in scholarly overviews of the Bill of Rights The Ninth Amendment and Unenumerated Rights - Congressional Research Service.

In plain terms, the Ninth does not list specific new rights. It signals that the framers meant the people to retain other rights beyond those enumerated in the first ten amendments, an idea reflected in foundational commentary Ninth Amendment - U.S. Constitution Annotated.

Historical origins and ratification process

The Ninth Amendment emerged during the 1789 Bill of Rights drafting as delegates and commentators debated how to protect liberties not spelled out in the Constitution’s main text.

Drafting records and legal summaries show the language was deliberately brief, meant as a textual reassurance rather than a detailed list of additional rights, a point highlighted in congressional research summaries The Ninth Amendment and Unenumerated Rights - Congressional Research Service. Ratification of the Bill of Rights in the early 1790s placed the Ninth alongside other amendments designed to limit federal power while acknowledging retained rights, which legal historians and analysts note when describing founding-era debates Ninth Amendment - U.S. Constitution Annotated. The ratification history connects to discussions of the first ten amendments as a group.

How courts have used the Ninth Amendment

In practice, judges and courts through 2026 have seldom based a major Supreme Court ruling solely on the Ninth Amendment; rather, the amendment commonly appears as interpretive support alongside other constitutional provisions.

Scholarly reviews of Supreme Court practice emphasize that courts more often ground unenumerated-rights holdings in doctrines such as substantive due process and refer to the Ninth as supplementary reasoning United States v. Carolene Products Co., 304 U.S. 144 (1938).

Use these sources to read opinions directly

Readers researching court treatment will commonly find the Ninth cited in opinions as a supporting passage rather than as the primary constitutional footing for a ruling, a trend noted in legal commentary and case summaries The Ninth Amendment: History and Interpretation - SCOTUSblog.

That practical pattern means the Ninth is part of the constitutional conversation about unenumerated rights, but it is usually one piece among several doctrinal and textual arguments a court might use, as case law overviews make clear United States v. Carolene Products Co., 304 U.S. 144 (1938).

Griswold v. Connecticut and the Ninth Amendment

Griswold v. Connecticut is the Supreme Court decision most often associated with judicial protection of unenumerated privacy interests and it remains central to modern privacy doctrine discussions.

The Court in Griswold found a protected privacy interest in marital relations, and the decision’s reasoning drew on concepts of privacy across multiple provisions; a separate concurrence explicitly invoked the Ninth Amendment when discussing retained rights Griswold v. Connecticut, 381 U.S. 479 (1965).

Legal overviews stress that Griswold’s protection of privacy did not rest solely on the Ninth; the opinion and scholarly summaries show the majority relied on a broader view of constitutional guarantees while the concurrence highlighted the Ninth as an additional textual source Griswold v. Connecticut - Case overview.

Because of Griswold, later cases about privacy and personal autonomy refer back to its reasoning, but commentators caution against saying Griswold established a standalone Ninth Amendment doctrine Griswold v. Connecticut, 381 U.S. 479 (1965).

United States v. Carolene Products, Footnote Four, and its influence

United States v. Carolene Products contains the famous Footnote Four that scholars often cite when discussing judicial protection of certain claims and heightened scrutiny for discrete groups.

Footnote Four shaped how scholars and courts think about review standards and the role of the judiciary in protecting particular rights or minorities, but legal summaries note it does not by itself create a broad Ninth Amendment doctrine United States v. Carolene Products Co., 304 U.S. 144 (1938).

Scholars point out that Footnote Four addresses when courts should apply different levels of scrutiny and why certain claims may deserve closer attention; it is often used in broader doctrinal debates rather than as a direct Ninth Amendment ruling The Ninth Amendment and Unenumerated Rights - Congressional Research Service.

As a result, readers should understand the influence of Carolene Products as doctrinal rather than as an explicit recognition of specific unenumerated rights under the Ninth alone United States v. Carolene Products Co., 304 U.S. 144 (1938).

Scholarly debates: can the Ninth support new rights on its own?

Scholars disagree about whether the Ninth Amendment can serve as an independent source for recognizing new rights, and major overviews present both sides of the debate.

One scholarly view treats the Ninth as a textual affirmation that the people retain unspecified rights that deserve protection, a stance reflected in detailed legal summaries and commentary The Ninth Amendment and Unenumerated Rights - Congressional Research Service. Additional commentary and discussion appear in materials such as the Federalist Society video on retained rights Retained by the People: The Ninth Amendment - Federalist Society.

Other commentators are cautious, arguing courts should rely on established doctrines such as substantive due process or other constitutional provisions rather than use the Ninth as the principal basis for new rights, a position found in contemporary analysis The Ninth Amendment: History and Interpretation - SCOTUSblog.

That debate leaves open a core question for future courts: whether to treat the Ninth as primarily interpretive or as an independent textual warrant for unenumerated rights, a point legal overviews identify as unsettled The Ninth Amendment and Unenumerated Rights - Congressional Research Service.

What the Ninth Amendment means for voters and public debate

For readers and voters, the Ninth signals that the Constitution allows for rights beyond those expressly written in the first ten amendments, but it does not predict which unenumerated rights courts will recognize in future cases Ninth Amendment - U.S. Constitution Annotated. Common mistakes and what to avoid when reading Ninth Amendment claims

A common error is treating the Ninth as a guaranteed, self-executing source of rights that courts will apply on its own; case law summaries show courts rarely use it as the principal basis for new doctrines United States v. Carolene Products Co., 304 U.S. 144 (1938).

Another mistake is citing Griswold or Carolene Products as definitive Ninth Amendment victories without noting the concurrence or footnote context in those decisions; reading the opinion text clarifies what reasoning the Court actually used Griswold v. Connecticut, 381 U.S. 479 (1965).
