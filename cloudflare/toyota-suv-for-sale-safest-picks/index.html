What 'safety' means in crash tests and daily use

Safety is not a single measurement. Crashworthiness looks at structure and roof strength, while active systems help avoid or reduce the severity of crashes. When comparing a used Toyota, check both agency test results and whether modern driver assist features were fitted to that model-year and trim. How IIHS and NHTSA tests differ and what to look for in ratings

Key IIHS test areas to check (small overlap, side, roof strength)

IIHS focuses on individual crash modes and the vehicle structure, including small overlap, moderate overlap, side impact, and roof strength. Those specific test names show how the car behaves in different crash scenarios and where structure holds up or lets down; use the IIHS model pages to inspect the exact results for the model-year you are considering IIHS ratings MotorTrend coverage

NHTSA star ratings and rollover risk

NHTSA provides overall star ratings that summarize frontal and side crash outcomes and include a rollover risk measure, which is useful for large SUVs and taller vehicles; the star rating offers a quick comparison but does not replace the detailed IIHS crash-mode breakdowns NHTSA vehicle safety ratings NHTSA's 2026 testing list

When you compare listings, pull the model-year page on both agency sites. Agency pages are model-year specific and note optional equipment or structural changes that can change a rating from one year to the next IIHS ratings

How to verify advanced driver assistance features on the exact trim or VIN

Which ADAS features most affect safety outcomes

Prioritize automatic emergency braking, lane-keeping assist, and blind-spot monitoring when safety is a top goal. These features help prevent common crash types or reduce severity when a collision is imminent. Confirming they are present on the exact vehicle reduces the guesswork when choosing between similar listings IIHS vehicle pages

Where to confirm if a feature is standard for a trim or only optional

Check the manufacturer spec sheet for the model-year and trim, request the dealer's window sticker or spec sheet, and use a VIN-based vehicle history report to confirm factory options. Why body-on-frame and unibody construction matter for family safety

Structural differences and crash implications

Unibody construction integrates the body and frame into a single shell, which tends to allow designers to optimize crush zones and occupant protection in many modern crash tests. Body-on-frame uses a separate frame and body, which can be beneficial for heavy-duty use but often results in different crash behaviors in standardized tests NHTSA vehicle

When a body-on-frame SUV might still be appropriate

If your primary needs are towing or serious off-road use, a body-on-frame SUV can be appropriate. In those cases, mitigate safety trade-offs by selecting a trim with the most complete ADAS package and by confirming crash-test results for that model-year IIHS ratings

Relying on badges instead of checking ratings and VIN

Do not assume a model is safe because it is a Toyota. Brand reputation helps narrow options but model-year specifics determine safety equipment and crash-test outcomes; verify ratings and trim-level features for the exact vehicle NHTSA vehicle safety ratings

Assuming older body-on-frame SUVs have modern safety kit

Older body-on-frame SUVs may lack modern ADAS as standard equipment. If you are considering a 4Runner or an older Sequoia, confirm what systems are present and check crash-test pages for that model-year before accepting a trade-off for towing or off-road capability NHTSA vehicle
