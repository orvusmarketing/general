Why Congress proposed the first 12 amendments

In the wake of the Constitution's 1788 adoption, the new Congress responded to concerns about individual rights and the scope of federal power by proposing a set of amendments for the states to consider. On September 25, 1789, the 1st Congress passed a joint resolution proposing twelve amendments, which the states were asked to ratify; this joint resolution is the foundational record for the original 12 constitutional amendments and helps explain why the package was sent to the states rather than enacted immediately Congress.gov joint resolution.

The proposals reflected political compromises and public concern in several states about limits on federal authority, individual liberties, and the structure of representation. Lawmakers framed the twelve proposals as a single set to simplify the submission to state legislatures, allowing each state to consider the package within its own constitutional process.

What the proposals aimed to address

Members of Congress sought to address several concerns raised in state ratification debates, including protections for speech, religion, fair legal process, and limitations on the federal government. The package included two provisions that did not join the core set certified in 1791 but remained part of the original submission to the states.

Which of the first 12 amendments were ratified by 1791 (the Bill of Rights)

Ten of the twelve proposed amendments were ratified by the states and certified on December 15, 1791; those ten are collectively known as the Bill of Rights, and the National Archives maintains a transcription and certification record for that date Bill of Rights transcription, National Archives.

The sequence of state ratifications occurred over several months in 1789-1791 and reached the threshold required under the Constitution for amendment adoption. Archival timelines show how the cluster of state approvals produced the December 1791 certification used as the authoritative point of record.

The ten amendments ratified and certified in 1791

The ten ratified items secured protections for freedoms of religion, speech, press, assembly, and petition; protections in criminal prosecutions; and limits on federal power in specified areas. The certified text and date are preserved in the National Archives transcription and the Library of Congress collections for direct consultation Bill of Rights transcription, National Archives and the Constitution Center's amendments list Constitution Center.

How one of the original amendments became the Twenty-seventh Amendment

One of the twelve proposals in 1789 addressed congressional pay adjustments. That proposal did not form part of the ten-item Bill of Rights adopted in 1791 but remained among the original submissions and later became the Twenty-seventh Amendment after a long, staggered sequence of state ratifications culminating centuries later Amendments 11-27, National Archives, and the House history is summarized at House history.

The congressional pay amendment: text and long path

The apportionment amendment that remains pending

The other proposal among the original twelve, commonly called the apportionment amendment, aimed to adjust how representatives would be apportioned among the states; primary summaries and reference works describe its text and intended change without asserting future outcomes Constitution Annotated on Amendment XXVII.

This apportionment proposal has never reached the number of state ratifications required for adoption and therefore remains pending under the usual historical accounting of the twelve original proposals. Reference summaries note the pending status while pointing readers to state legislative records for current status checks Encyclopaedia Britannica on the Bill of Rights.

Text and intended change to representation

The apportionment proposal sought to set a formula or constraint for how seats would be allotted among states as population changed. The exact wording and the policy aim are preserved in the 1789 joint resolution record and discussed in reference materials that track the original submissions.

Why it has not reached full ratification

The apportionment amendment has not achieved the necessary cluster of state approvals; historians and reference sources treat the proposal as still pending unless and until the required number of states act to ratify it. Because of the long interval since 1789, legal analysts note that any future ratification might raise interpretive questions.

How amendment ratification works: legal criteria and procedure

Amendment proposals begin in Congress as resolutions; if passed, they are transmitted to the states, where state legislatures or conventions consider ratification under the process described in Article V of the Constitution. The 1789 joint resolution is the procedural starting point for the original twelve proposals Congress.gov joint resolution.

Once state ratifications accumulate to the number required by Article V, the amendment is generally treated as adopted and certified by appropriate archival or legislative offices. Archival records for the Bill of Rights and for the Twenty-seventh Amendment illustrate how certification and recorded dates are used as reference points Constitution Annotated on Amendment XXVII.

Congressional proposal versus state ratification

Congress sets the proposed text and the transmission method; state legislatures vote according to their own procedures. In the 1789-1791 episode, the Congress sent the twelve proposals as a single package and the states acted on individual texts as they saw fit.

Certification and archival recording

When an amendment meets the ratification threshold, repositories like the National Archives and reference resources record the adoption date and preserve the certified text for public consultation. Those certified transcriptions serve as the primary references used in reporting and scholarship.

Common misconceptions about the first 12 amendments

A frequent error is to assume all twelve proposals were adopted together as the Bill of Rights. In fact, only ten were ratified and certified as the Bill of Rights in 1791; another became the 27th Amendment much later, and one remains pending, so the twelve did not all share the same fate Bill of Rights transcription, National Archives.

Readers sometimes interpret "pending" to mean the proposal is invalid or forgotten. Pending means the proposal has not achieved the required ratifications and remains on the historical record as an outstanding item.

Legal questions scholars raise about late ratification

Scholars note issues such as whether ratification deadlines, altered state constitutions, or changes in federal structure affect the operative status of a very old proposal. Those concerns make the apportionment proposal an unsettled matter if states acted today.
