What counts as a federal law: definition and constitutional context

The core rule is constitutional: bills and joint resolutions become federal law only after they pass both the House and the Senate in identical form and are presented to the President, a principle found in Article I, Section 7 of the Constitution and explained in primary guides to the lawmaking process. In plain terms, a bill or a joint resolution that the chambers agree on and the President signs becomes public law; other measures have different status and effect. The distinction between those categories is central to understanding whether single-chamber action can change federal law. How Our Laws Are Made

Simple resolutions and concurrent resolutions serve internal Congressional purposes but do not create binding federal law and do not require presentment to the President, a point the official guides make clear when they contrast those measures with bills and joint resolutions. Step-by-step: how a bill normally becomes law

First, a member introduces a bill and it is assigned to committee for study, hearings, and possible amendment, which is the primary vetting stage in each chamber. Committees commonly hold markup sessions before deciding whether to report a bill to the floor. How Our Laws Are Made See the flowchart for a visual guide.

After committee review, the bill goes to the full chamber for debate and votes. Passage in one chamber is only the halfway point when the text will likely differ from the other chamber's version; enactment requires identical text before presentment. How Our Laws Are Made

If the House and Senate pass different versions, they typically resolve those differences by one of a few methods: one chamber can amend its bill to match the other, the chambers can exchange messages with amendments, or they can appoint a conference committee to negotiate a compromise text to return to both chambers for final approval. This reconciliation of text is routine in the legislative process. How Our Laws Are Made

Once both chambers have agreed on identical language, the enrolled bill is presented to the President for signature or veto; if signed, it becomes public law and is recorded as such in public law publications and the Congressional Record. A recent enacted law illustrates this sequence and the public record for those steps. Public Law No: 119-21 See how a bill becomes law.

Because the sequence depends on bicameral agreement, a bill that clears the House alone remains unfinished business until the Senate acts and the President either signs or returns the measure. That is why tracking both chambers matters when following a proposal. How Our Laws Are Made

Special procedures the House can use - what they do and do not change

Simple and concurrent resolutions: purpose and limits

Simple resolutions allow one chamber to set its rules or express views; concurrent resolutions may coordinate actions by both chambers but neither creates binding federal law nor requires presentment to the President. How Our Laws Are Made

Budget reconciliation: scope, limits, and the Byrd Rule

Budget reconciliation is a special process that can speed Senate consideration of certain spending, revenue, or debt-limit changes, but it still requires Senate passage and is constrained by the Senate's Byrd Rule and other procedural limits. The Byrd Rule is a Senate-enforced limit that can bar provisions not closely tied to budgetary change from staying in a reconciliation bill, and the decision often rests on the Senate’s parliamentarian and Senate procedures. Reconciliation Process and the Byrd Rule

Discharge petitions: forcing House floor action

A discharge petition in the House lets a majority of Representatives bring a bill out of committee for floor consideration without the committee's consent, but it does not remove the need for Senate approval or presidential presentment to make a law. In short, these procedures can change how and when the House acts, and they can affect the pace of consideration, but they do not change the constitutional need for bicameral agreement and presentment for bills and joint resolutions that will become law. How Our Laws Are Made

When House action is not enough: decision points and legal limits

To decide whether a House action alone will bind the country, ask whether the measure is a bill or a joint resolution that requires bicameral passage and presentment; if it is not, it will not become binding federal law. U.S. Constitution transcript

Determinations about whether a provision fits reconciliation or will survive the Byrd Rule are made case by case and depend on Senate procedures, the parliamentarian's advice, and the votes available in the Senate. These are procedural, not constitutional, gatekeeping steps. Congressional Research Service overview of reconciliation Related commentary: Appropriations Watch.

Legal questions about enactment can reach the courts in some circumstances, but the foundational procedural steps that create a public law are established in congressional rules and the Constitution. Common mistakes and misconceptions people make about single-chamber action
