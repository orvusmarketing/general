What candidate information pages from neutral public sources such as FEC and Ballotpedia include

Candidate information pages from neutral public sources such as FEC and Ballotpedia typically compile a core set of facts so readers have a single place to start research. These pages normally present a basic biography, the office sought, party if applicable, filing and fundraising summaries, election results, and a list of cited sources that link to primary documents and official records.

Ballotpedia's public documentation says editors prioritize primary records such as campaign websites, FEC reports, state election office records and official results when compiling candidate pages, and that practice guides how entries are built and documented on the site Ballotpedia About page

Definition of a candidate information page

A candidate information page is a compiled reference that brings together public records and contemporary reporting about a person running for office. It is designed to show who the candidate is, what office they seek, and which public sources support those facts.

In practice a candidate page links outward, letting readers move from the summary to the primary records that provide legal and numeric detail.

Types of facts typically listed

Typical entries include the candidate's stated residence and biography, party affiliation, list of offices sought in the current cycle, filing and fund-raising summaries for committees, recent election results where relevant, and a citation list of the documents used to verify each claim.

Because these pages aggregate material, reading the citation list and following the links is the safest way to confirm dates and amounts shown in the summary.

A step-by-step framework to check a candidate page and its primary sources

Open the candidate page and scan the source list first. That source list shows which filings and documents the page used and is the quickest way to find the primary records you should open.

Next, click the linked FEC report or state filing to confirm the reporting period and totals in the summary. Note the filing dates and any report ID so you can compare like for like when reconciling numbers.

Check the sources before you rely on a candidate summary

Take a moment to open the page's citation list and click the most recent FEC or state filing linked there to check the date and totals yourself.

Open linked filings

Once you have the primary documents open, compare the exact figures and the reporting period to the compiled summary. If the compiled page shows rounded or summarized numbers, the primary document will have the precise totals and the dates that matter for verification.

Also check the candidate's campaign website or published statements for biographical claims or stated priorities; these are primary sources for what the campaign itself says. For state-level status, open the state qualifying list to confirm a candidate's official filing or qualifying date.

Initial page scan

Start by looking for a clear source list and a visible last-updated date. If those are present, you can often find the primary documents with one or two clicks.

If the page lacks sources or a date, treat the summary as incomplete and move directly to the campaign site and the FEC or state election site to confirm the core facts.

Tracing claims to primary documents

When you find a claim you want to verify, note which document the candidate page cites for that claim and open it. For fundraising numbers, find the specific FEC report and check the reporting period and the totals on that report to confirm the summary value shown on the compiled page FEC help pages for candidates and committees

For biographical details that could affect eligibility or residency, check the campaign site and state qualifying filings, which often list filing dates and confirmations from the state election office.

Verifying currency and update dates

Note the candidate page's last-updated date and compare it to the dates on the primary documents you open. If a primary filing is more recent than the last-updated date, the compiled page may not reflect the newest records and should be treated cautiously.

How to verify fundraising and filings on FEC-linked candidate information pages

FEC reports are the authoritative source for federal campaign finance and include the reporting period, committee name, filing ID and numerical totals for receipts and disbursements. Always open the linked report to confirm the period and figures shown in a compiled summary FEC help pages for candidates and committees.

Match the totals on the Ballotpedia summary to the specific FEC filing. If the compiled page shows a rounded figure or aggregates several reports, the FEC filing will show the exact amounts and the dates that define each reporting period.

Reading FEC report summaries

FEC report summaries list the committee name, reporting period and totals. Use those fields to verify the scope of a number on a compiled page and to find the original filing by ID when you need to be exact about a date or figure.

If you see a discrepancy, check whether the compiled page used a different reporting period or combined several committee reports into a single summary; the underlying FEC filings will reveal the correct reporting window and figures.

Cross-checking totals and reporting periods

When comparing totals, always confirm the reporting period on the FEC form. Numbers that appear different often reflect different end dates or interim reports rather than an error in the original filing.

For precise work, note the FEC filing ID and the filing date before drawing conclusions. That allows you to trace back to the exact document if you or a reader needs to verify the claim further.

State-level checks: what to look for in Florida candidate qualifying lists

Ballotpedia cross-checks state qualifying lists to confirm whether a person has officially filed or qualified in a given jurisdiction; the Florida Division of Elections publishes qualifying guidance and lists that show filing dates and related details Florida Division of Elections candidate qualifying information

When a candidate is newly filed or when a page shows ambiguous status, open the state qualifying list to confirm the filing date, whether a fee was paid or a qualifying petition was accepted, and any official notation about district or office.

Where Florida qualifying lists appear

The Florida Division of Elections maintains candidate qualifying information on its site, including forms and public lists that indicate when a candidate filed and whether they met filing requirements.

Those state records are primary for confirming whether a candidate is officially qualified to appear on a primary or general election ballot.

How Ballotpedia uses state records

Ballotpedia uses state election office records to verify candidate status and to document qualifying dates. When state lists are cited, they provide a reliable point of comparison to the compiled entry.

If Ballotpedia and the state list differ, the state filing is the authoritative source for qualification status and dates.

Ballotpedia editorial practices and sourcing criteria explained

Ballotpedia's editorial guidance requires citations for factual claims and emphasizes primary records over secondary reporting, stating that contemporaneous news should supplement but not replace primary documents in verifying facts Ballotpedia editorial policy

The policy means that when editors document a claim, they should show the public record or an official statement that supports it, with news coverage used for context or additional reporting when needed.

Editorial policy on citations

The editorial policy calls for clear citations tied to each factual claim. That lets readers follow the trail from a compiled summary to the underlying records used to verify it.

When you see a citation on a candidate page, follow it to the original document to check the dates, scope and exact language cited by the editor.

Use of news reports versus primary records

Ballotpedia uses news reports to provide context or to document contemporaneous events but treats them as secondary to filings and official records. News reporting can be helpful for timing or quotes, but the primary record should be the basis for numeric and legal facts.

For that reason, readers verifying important details should open the primary documents linked on the page rather than rely solely on summarized news excerpts.

Common errors and pitfalls when using candidate information pages

One common error is assuming a compiled summary is up to date; entries may lag behind the most recent filings, so check the filing dates on the primary documents to ensure currency Poynter analysis of Ballotpedia

Another pitfall is misreading rounded summaries. Compiled pages sometimes present rounded or aggregated numbers; open the FEC form to see exact figures and to identify the reporting window of those totals.

Out-of-date entries and recycled content

Some pages can carry forward older summaries that are not updated after a new filing. Always check the last-updated date and compare it to the filing date on the primary document.

If you suspect an entry is out of date, pull the primary filings and note their dates before citing or using the compiled numbers.

Misreading summaries and rounding issues

When a compiled page shows simplified totals, the original filing will show line-by-line figures and exact numbers. Use the primary report for precise figures rather than a rounded summary.

Reporting differences often reflect different cutoffs or aggregated committee reports; the primary documents explain those differences.
