What is the Due Process Clause? A clear definition and scope

Text of the clauses

The Fifth Amendment, part of the Bill of Rights, provides that no person shall be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law; the National Archives hosts an authoritative transcription of the amendment's text and placement in the Bill of Rights National Archives Bill of Rights transcript.

The Fourteenth Amendment, adopted after the Civil War, includes a Due Process Clause that likewise protects against state deprivations of life, liberty, or property; readers can review the amendment text and historical framing at the National Archives National Archives Fourteenth Amendment.

In short, both clauses bar government officials from depriving people of life, liberty, or property without due process, but the Fifth constrains federal actors while the Fourteenth constrains state and local actors. This practical divide - between federal and state reach - is the baseline rule courts apply when choosing which clause a plaintiff should invoke.

due process amendment

That basic allocation means a challenge to a federal agency or a federal law is ordinarily pleaded under the Fifth Amendment's due process protection, while a challenge to a state statute or local ordinance invokes the Fourteenth Amendment; the textual sources and ordinary practice reflect that separation of sovereign constraints National Archives Bill of Rights transcript.

Historical background: how the clauses reached different scopes

The Fifth Amendment was ratified with the Bill of Rights in 1791 and initially targeted the federal government, reflecting concerns about central power during the founding era; the National Archives provides the amendment text and historical context National Archives Bill of Rights transcript.

The practical difference is which sovereign is constrained and which tests and remedies apply: the Fifth limits federal actors and the Fourteenth limits state and local actors; procedure-focused claims typically use Mathews and seek process, while substantive claims can invalidate laws and require historical and liberty-focused analysis.

The Fourteenth Amendment was adopted in 1868 during Reconstruction and added new constraints on state governments, including its Due Process Clause; the amendment's text and adoption history are documented by the National Archives National Archives Fourteenth Amendment.

Those different ratification dates help explain why the Fifth appears in the original Bill of Rights while the Fourteenth followed a later constitutional expansion of protections directed at state action. Procedural due process: what courts ask and the Mathews test

Procedural due process concerns whether government follows adequate procedures before depriving someone of life, liberty, or property. Courts ask what process is due, not whether the underlying decision was correct, and they use established tests to weigh competing interests in administrative contexts.

The leading test for many procedural-due-process claims against federal actors is Mathews v. Eldridge, which directs courts to balance three factors: (1) the private interest affected, (2) the risk of erroneous deprivation and the value of added procedures, and (3) the government's interest, including fiscal and administrative burdens; the Supreme Court's opinion explains the balancing framework Mathews v. Eldridge opinion.

Use as an initial triage tool

Goldberg v. Kelly is a key procedural-due-process decision showing that some government-provided benefits are treated as entitlements that require pre-deprivation hearings; courts have used the case to recognize that when an interest is sufficiently important, immediate procedural protections are necessary Goldberg v. Kelly opinion.

When litigants raise procedural claims, the Mathews balancing test and Goldberg-style entitlement analysis guide whether a hearing, notice, or appeal must come before the government cuts off a benefit or right. Courts apply these principles across many administrative settings, including benefit terminations and licensing decisions.

Substantive due process: protected rights and recent doctrinal shifts

Substantive due process protects certain fundamental rights from government interference, not merely the fairness of procedures. Courts ask whether a claimed right is deeply rooted in the Nation's history and tradition or is necessary to ordered liberty when deciding if heightened scrutiny applies.

The Supreme Court's decision in Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization is a prominent recent example of the Court revisiting how substantive-due-process review applies to at least one high-profile right; the opinion altered the Court's prior framework for analyzing that right Dobbs v. Courts identify fundamental rights through methods that look to history, tradition, and whether a right is essential to ordered liberty, and they have used those approaches to decide which substantive claims receive strict review and which do not.

Because substantive review can invalidate laws rather than merely require process, litigants and commentators watch doctrinal shifts closely; recent decisions have prompted debates about how lower courts should evaluate claims that implicate personal autonomy, family relationships, or other weighty interests. The practical difference: when to invoke the Fifth or the Fourteenth Amendment

The basic practical rule is straightforward: when a plaintiff challenges federal executive or legislative action, courts expect a claim under the Fifth Amendment; when a plaintiff challenges state or local action, courts expect a claim under the Fourteenth Amendment. That allocation follows from the text and ordinary litigation practices described in primary sources National Archives Fourteenth Amendment. See an overview at the Constitution Center.

Remedies and likelihoods differ depending on whether the claim is procedural or substantive. Procedural claims typically seek notice, a hearing, or reinstatement of benefits; substantive claims may seek to invalidate a statute or regulation that infringes a protected right.

The Supreme Court has also treated the Fifth Amendment as imposing Equal Protection-like limits on federal action in cases such as Bolling v. Sharpe, where the Court applied a constitutional constraint against federal actors that is similar to the Fourteenth Amendment's Equal Protection principles Bolling v. Sharpe opinion. A decision framework for litigants and courts: procedural or substantive?

Start with a short checklist to frame any due process claim: identify the government actor, decide whether the claim challenges procedures or the underlying government decision, and select the appropriate standard of review. That basic triage helps plaintiffs focus pleadings and evidence.

If the claim is procedural, Mathews v. Eldridge supplies the balancing factors courts use to decide what process is due; evaluating the private interest and the risk of error is often decisive in benefits and administrative disputes Mathews v. Eldridge opinion.

For substantive claims, courts commonly ask whether the asserted right is deeply rooted in the Nation's history and tradition or fundamental to ordered liberty and then apply an appropriate level of scrutiny. That inquiry determines whether the government must justify an intrusion with strict scrutiny or whether more deferential review applies.

Putting these steps together creates a clear path: (1) identify the actor and clause, (2) classify the claim as procedural or substantive, (3) apply Mathews if procedural, (4) apply the historical-and-liberty analysis if substantive, and (5) seek remedies matched to the classification.

A frequent error is conflating procedural and substantive arguments in a way that blurs the standard of review; courts expect clarity about whether the complaint seeks procedural protections or substantive relief, and mixing the two can undermine a claim Mathews v. Eldridge opinion.

Another common mistake is naming the wrong defendant. Suing a federal agency when the action was taken by a state actor, or vice versa, can misplace the constitutional clause and lead to dismissal or loss of a meritorious claim.

Litigants also err by relying on slogans or general rhetoric instead of pointing to doctrinal tests and controlling precedents; grounding arguments in the relevant cases and tests improves clarity and results in fewer procedural setbacks. Recent doctrinal changes, such as the Dobbs decision, make careful citation and precise framing more important than ever Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization opinion.

Mathews provides a three-factor balancing test for procedural due process: the private interest at stake, the risk of erroneous deprivation and value of additional safeguards, and the government's interest including administrative burdens.

Understanding which due process clause applies is a matter of identifying the government actor and whether the claim challenges process or substance. For most practical disputes, Mathews guides procedural claims and the history-and-liberty inquiry guides substantive claims.
