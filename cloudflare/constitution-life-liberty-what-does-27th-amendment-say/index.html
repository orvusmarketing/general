What the 27th Amendment says and where to find its text (constitution life liberty)

The 27th Amendment forbids laws that change the compensation for members of Congress from taking effect until after the next election of Representatives.

The National Archives posts the modern text and the ratification record for the amendment.

The full official text and context are preserved in government records and annotated legal guides, which provide the wording used today and the states counting toward ratification National Archives amendments page.

How the amendment reached ratification: the long history behind it (constitution life liberty)

The 27th Amendment began as one of the amendments proposed by the First Congress in 1789 and then remained unratified for many years.

Historical summaries note that the language was part of the original 1789 proposed amendments but received little attention for more than a century before a renewed campaign of state ratifications late in the 20th century Library of Congress background on Amendment XXVII.

In the late 1900s some state legislatures revisited the old proposal and began sending ratification documents to federal authorities, a process that gradually increased the tally of ratifying states.

Those state actions produced the final count of ratifications on May 7, 1992, when the required number of states had acted and the amendment was declared ratified, a timeline preserved in National Archives records National Archives amendments page.

Origin in 1789 and dormancy

Proposed in the First Congress, the amendment sat largely dormant as some states did not act and as political attention moved elsewhere.

The long dormancy is documented in archival and legislative histories that track the proposal from its 1789 origin through the 19th and 20th centuries.

Core rule: how the amendment affects congressional pay timing (constitution life liberty)

The central rule in the 27th Amendment is procedural: it delays the effective date of any law that changes congressional compensation until after the next election of Representatives.

Put another way, if Congress passes a law changing pay, that change cannot legally take effect until after voters have had an intervening opportunity to elect House members, protecting against immediate self-enrichment.

For authoritative language and the plain statement of this rule, the Constitution Annotated records the amendment and explains its operative clause and effect Constitution Annotated - Amendment XXVII.

Practical mechanics: when a pay change takes effect

Mechanically, a pay-change law that is signed before a House election would remain pending until after that election concludes and the newly elected Representatives are seated, unless states or Congress establish additional procedures.

To make this concrete, consider a short hypothetical.

It states that laws changing congressional compensation cannot take effect until after the next election of Representatives, thereby delaying pay changes until after an intervening House election.

Suppose Congress passes a pay increase in July of a congressional election year; under the amendment that increase would only take effect after the voters elect the next House, which typically happens that November and results in a new House seated the following January, meaning the pay change would not affect the current Representatives until after that election cycle.

This timing protects voters by ensuring a direct electoral opportunity occurs before any pay change takes effect for the sitting delegation.

Legal context and precedent on delayed ratifications

Coleman v. Miller (1939) is the leading Supreme Court case that addresses long-delayed ratifications and frames timing disputes as political questions for legislatures and Congress rather than issues for courts to resolve in most cases Coleman v. Miller.

The Coleman decision left primary responsibility for resolving timing and procedural disputes about amendments to the political branches, a principle that continues to shape how scholars and officials view prolonged ratification efforts.

Because the 27th Amendment was ratified long after Coleman, courts have rarely issued major rulings interpreting the amendment itself, and authoritative explanation often appears in annotated guides and legal commentary rather than in Supreme Court opinions. See a concise summary at Study.com.

The Constitution Annotated and legal treatises summarize the narrow judicial record concerning the amendment and point to the broader doctrinal issues raised by delayed ratifications Constitution Annotated - Amendment XXVII.

Coleman v. Miller and the political question framing

In Coleman the Court declined to decide many of the timing questions on judicial grounds, treating them as matters for state legislatures and Congress, and that posture informs later debates about deadlines and rescission Supreme Court opinion text and in legislative briefings CRS legal briefing on ratification and rescission.

Scholarly and annotated views on judicial gaps

Legal commentary points out that because courts have not issued a clear, modern ruling directly interpreting the 27th Amendment, much analysis relies on the Constitution Annotated and scholarly work that weighs possible interpretations and procedural rules Legal overview at Cornell Law.

As a result, unresolved questions such as whether Congress may set time limits for ratification remain active topics in law reviews and government briefings rather than settled judicial law.

Common misunderstandings and pitfalls when people ask 'what does the 27th Amendment say'

One common error is to imagine the amendment guarantees broad policy results beyond its narrow procedural rule; it does not change when Congress may legislate on policy, it only delays the effective date of pay adjustments until after an intervening House election.

Another mistake is assuming the courts have resolved whether states may rescind ratifications or whether Congress can impose deadlines; those procedural points remain debated in scholarship and government reports CRS legal briefing on ratification and rescission.

Practical examples and scenarios where the amendment matters

Scenario one: a pay increase passed shortly before an election. If Congress approved a raise in the months before a regular House election, the 27th Amendment would require that the raise not take effect until after the voters have elected the next House and those Representatives are seated, delaying any benefit to the existing members National Archives amendments page.

Scenario two: a reduction or restructuring of pay. If Congress enacted a restructuring that reduced certain pay elements, the same timing rule would apply and those changes would only take effect after the next House election, which can affect whether the change applies to the then-current delegation.

In both examples the amendment's practical effect is procedural: it alters the timing of implementation without prescribing specific salary levels or budgetary policy, and it therefore functions as a check on immediate self-dealing by sitting members.

For voters and observers, the amendment creates a predictable rule about when pay laws become operative and highlights the role of electoral accountability between passage and effect.

Conclusion: what the amendment means today and where debates remain

Today the 27th Amendment is best understood as a narrowly targeted constitutional rule that delays the effective date of laws changing congressional pay until after the next election of Representatives, a point made clear in archival texts and annotated guides National Archives amendments page and the Bill of Rights and civil liberties explainer.

Its unusual history, proposed in 1789 and ratified in 1992, and the reliance on Coleman v. Miller for timing disputes, mean that the amendment sits at the intersection of archival fact and unresolved procedural law Coleman v. Miller case overview.
