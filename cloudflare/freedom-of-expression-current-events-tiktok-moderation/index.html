Freedom of speech versus private moderation: the legal distinction

What the First Amendment protects, freedom of expression current events

The phrase freedom of expression current events refers to ongoing public discussion about how speech is governed online and in public life. In U.S. law, the First Amendment limits government action that restricts speech, not the choices private companies make about what they host or remove, a point explained in legal overviews of the amendment Cornell Law School on the First Amendment and our First Amendment explainer.

The state action doctrine explains why that difference matters: constitutional protections attach when a government or its agent acts to restrict speech, but they do not automatically apply to private platforms enforcing their terms of service, a distinction that legal history and government documents set out National Archives guidance on the Bill of Rights.

The state action doctrine explained

The state action doctrine focuses analysis on whether a private actor's conduct can be fairly attributed to the state, and courts examine factors such as government coercion or close entanglement when deciding that question. This is why most content-moderation disputes involving private platforms are resolved under contract, consumer, or platform policy frameworks rather than as constitutional free speech claims Cornell Law School on the First Amendment.

That legal boundary matters for public debate: when people say a private company "censored" speech, it can describe a content removal under a platform's rules but does not by itself prove a constitutional violation unless a court finds state action.

How TikTok's rules determine what content is removed or limited

Overview of community guidelines and enforcement

TikTok publishes Community Guidelines that list categories of content the platform may remove or limit, including material the company defines as dangerous, harassing, or disinformation, and the guidelines describe enforcement tools such as takedowns and labeling TikTok's Community Guidelines.

Those rules operate through terms of service and safety policies that give TikTok discretion to apply measures such as age restrictions, account limits, temporary removal, or permanent bans when content crosses the listed categories, and the platform documents the enforcement mechanisms it uses TikTok's Community Guidelines.

When platforms enforce these rules, the action is private moderation: the platform decides which rules to adopt and how to implement them, and users agree to those terms when they use the service. That contractual and policy framework is the practical ground for most moderation outcomes TikTok's Community Guidelines.

Examples of content categories that platforms may remove

Common content categories that trigger enforcement on many platforms include safety violations, harassment, hate speech, and certain forms of false or misleading information; TikTok's published categories help users understand what behavior or content may lead to removal or restriction TikTok's Community Guidelines.

Enforcement can also include nonremoval steps such as age gating, content labeling, or limiting distribution, which change how broadly content is seen even without complete takedown, and those options are part of the same private decision-making process.

Recommendation systems and amplification: why visibility matters even without removals

How the For You feed amplifies some posts

Platforms do not only remove content; they also decide which items to amplify through recommendation systems, and TikTok's For You feed promotes posts using engagement signals and other inputs that influence reach, a pattern explored in peer reviewed analysis of recommendation systems peer reviewed study on recommendation systems and reporting by the BBC How social media algorithms shape speech.

Because amplification determines which posts reach large audiences, creators can see major differences in visibility that are not tied to removals but to algorithmic ranking and selection.

TikTok is a private company that enforces its terms and controls recommendation systems; the First Amendment restricts government censorship, so constitutional free speech protections do not automatically apply to private moderation.

Regulatory and government responses to TikTok: not a First Amendment remedy

Device bans and government restrictions

Federal and many state governments in the U.S. have restricted or banned TikTok on government devices, citing security and data privacy concerns as the rationale for those policies, which is a regulatory or administrative response rather than a constitutional judgment about private moderation Reuters report on government device bans. Those device bans show how public institutions can limit official use of an app for security reasons while leaving private citizens free to use the service under the platform's own rules.

Regional regulation such as the EU Digital Services Act

The European Union's Digital Services Act imposes new transparency, risk-management, and reporting duties on large online platforms, creating obligations that change how platforms must handle content moderation and disclose practices to regulators and the public European Commission on the Digital Services Act.

Regulatory frameworks like the DSA do not convert private moderation into government censorship, but they do shift some responsibilities from purely internal policy choices toward externally enforced transparency and safety requirements.

Practical implications for users and creators

Steps to preserve reach and copies of content

These steps are practical risk-management measures; they do not alter the legal distinction that the First Amendment applies to government action rather than private moderation.

Balancing legal frameworks and platform duties

Where constitutional law ends and regulation begins

Constitutional protections stop applying when a purely private actor enforces its rules without state action, but regulation can require platforms to take or disclose certain steps without turning them into state actors, a separation made clear by legal summaries and regulatory texts Cornell Law School on the First Amendment and our constitutional rights page.

In practice, that means courts, regulators and legislators play different roles: courts assess constitutional claims and state action, while regulators can set obligations for transparency, risk mitigation, and reporting.

How transparency and risk management rules shift responsibilities

Laws such as the Digital Services Act create specific duties for large platforms to manage systemic risks and publish transparency reports, shifting some governance toward regulatory oversight and away from purely private, opaque decision-making European Commission on the Digital Services Act.

Those regulatory duties aim to make moderation and recommendation processes more visible to users and authorities while preserving that platforms still operate under private governance frameworks.

Common misunderstandings when people say TikTok 'censors' free speech

Mistaken assumptions about constitutional protection of private platforms

A frequent mistake is to treat private moderation as if it were government censorship; that conflates platform policy enforcement with state action and overlooks the legal requirement that a government actor must be involved for the First Amendment to apply Cornell Law School on the First Amendment.

Another common error is expecting a single universal standard across platforms; in fact, each service has its own policies and enforcement practices that users accept when they join.
