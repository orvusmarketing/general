Quick answer: What laws did FDR make and why it matters

Franklin D. Roosevelt's administration enacted a set of laws in the 1930s that reshaped banking, labor, wages, and social safety nets. Readers asking "What laws did FDR pass?" should expect a mix of immediate crisis measures and later structural reforms, and the phrase fdr 2nd bill of rights appears in historical discussion as a separate concept that requires primary-source citation.

Key, commonly cited statutes include early banking measures such as the Emergency Banking Act and the Banking Act of 1933, the Social Security Act of 1935, the National Labor Relations Act of 1935, and the Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938. How historians group FDR's laws: First New Deal, Second New Deal, then wartime measures

Historians and reference works commonly divide Roosevelt's legislative program into phases. The First New Deal covers immediate relief, recovery, and banking measures enacted in 1933 and 1934, while the Second New Deal includes structural social and labor reforms in 1935 and 1936. For an accessible overview of this periodization, see the Encyclopaedia Britannica New Deal summary Encyclopaedia Britannica overview of the New Deal.

The two-phase framing matters because the types of laws and the policy aims differ. Early laws aimed to stabilize banks and restore confidence. Later statutes shifted toward long-term social programs and labor protections. Readers should treat wartime legislation that followed as a separate set of priorities tied to mobilization and financing.

FDR's administration enacted emergency banking reforms in 1933, the Social Security Act in 1935, labor protections like the National Labor Relations Act in 1935, and the Fair Labor Standards Act in 1938, among other statutes; for precise language consult primary statutes and archival records.

Understanding these phases helps when someone asks what category a specific statute fits into and whether it was a crisis response or a structural reform.

What each phase emphasized

The First New Deal emphasized emergency banking measures, short-term relief programs, and regulatory changes designed to restore economic functioning. For a list that groups statutes by program type, consult the compiled New Deal statute list maintained by the Gilder Lehrman Institute Gilder Lehrman Institute list of New Deal statutes.

The Second New Deal emphasized social insurance, labor rights, and standards for wages and hours. That framing helps explain why key laws like the Social Security Act and the National Labor Relations Act appear in the mid 1930s while minimum-wage rules followed later.

Why the two-phase framing matters

Using the First/Second New Deal labels clarifies research and citation choices. If you need clause-level language, identify when the law was enacted, then consult the statute text and committee reports from that session of Congress.

Banking and financial reforms: Emergency Banking Act and the Banking Act of 1933

The Emergency Banking Act, passed in March 1933, was an early measure to stabilize a failing banking system and to restore public confidence in deposits; the National Archives hosts a primary-document entry for this act that reproduces key materials and context OurDocuments / National Archives on the Emergency Banking Act.

Shortly after, Congress passed the Banking Act of 1933, commonly known as Glass-Steagall, which restructured banking regulation and laid the groundwork for federal deposit insurance; the FDIC provides a clear overview of the Banking Act and its deposit insurance role FDIC overview of the Banking Act of 1933.

At a high level, these laws operated on two fronts: immediate stabilization, through emergency authority and temporary measures, and structural safeguards, through new regulatory boundaries and insurance that reduced the risk of bank runs.

Emergency Banking Act (March 1933)

The Emergency Banking Act authorized measures to reorganize insolvent banks and to reopen sound institutions under supervision. Banking Act of 1933 and FDIC, Glass-Steagall

The Banking Act of 1933 separated certain commercial and investment banking activities and created the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation to insure bank deposits and reduce runs. For a concise institutional history and description of deposit insurance, see the FDIC's overview FDIC overview of the Banking Act of 1933.

Social Security Act of 1935: federal old-age and unemployment insurance

The Social Security Act of 1935 established federal old-age insurance and unemployment insurance as national programs. The Social Security Administration provides a concise historical summary of the act and its principal provisions Social Security Administration historical page on the Social Security Act.

At enactment, the scope of benefits and covered populations was more limited than later decades, and subsequent amendments expanded coverage. For clause-level language and precise eligibility rules at enactment, consult the statute text and the SSA historical archives.

What the Social Security Act created

The 1935 law set up old-age retirement insurance financed through payroll taxes and a federal-state framework for unemployment insurance. For authoritative background and the original statutory discussion, see the Social Security Administration historical entry Social Security Administration historical page on the Social Security Act.

Who it covered at enactment and where to find the law text

Labor law reforms: National Labor Relations Act (Wagner Act) of 1935

The National Labor Relations Act of 1935 created statutory protections for collective bargaining and established the National Labor Relations Board as the federal agency charged with enforcing the law. A clear legal overview and interpretive context are available from Cornell Law School's Legal Information Institute Cornell Law School Legal Information Institute on the National Labor Relations Act.

The Wagner Act changed labor relations by making certain employer practices unlawful and by giving workers a federal forum to seek remedies. For enforcement history and the original statutory language, consult the NLRB records and the statute text.

What the Wagner Act established

The statute protected collective bargaining, prohibited certain unfair labor practices by employers, and authorized a board to oversee elections and remedy violations. For statutory text and a functional summary, see the Cornell Law overview Cornell Law School Legal Information Institute on the National Labor Relations Act.

How it changed labor relations and enforcement

By creating a federal enforcement body, the law moved some labor disputes from courts into an administrative process designed around elections and remedies. Researchers interested in enforcement patterns should consult NLRB historical records and contemporaneous reports.

Fair Labor Standards Act (1938): wages, hours, and child-labor rules

The Fair Labor Standards Act set national minimum-wage, overtime-pay, and child-labor standards for covered workers and was a later New Deal era statute that addressed working conditions and labor standards. For context about the law and its passage, see Encyclopaedia Britannica's summary of New Deal legislation Encyclopaedia Britannica overview of the New Deal.

Coverage and thresholds under the FLSA have changed with later amendments and administrative regulation. For precise definitions of covered employers and workers at enactment, consult the statute text and later amendments in federal law records.

Key provisions summarized

At a high level, the law created a federal minimum wage and rules for overtime pay, and it limited certain forms of child labor in covered industries. Researchers should trace the original language in the statute text to avoid attributing later changes to the 1938 law itself.

Limits and coverage at enactment

The reach of the FLSA at enactment left out certain sectors and depended on definitions the statute supplied. To understand who was covered in 1938, use primary legal texts and administrative records that show how coverage evolved.

How these laws were passed: legislative mechanics and political context

Many New Deal laws were enacted under pressure from an economic emergency, which led to rapid legislative action in some cases and more deliberation in others. Passage involved Congress, committees, and executive proposals, and political dynamics shifted over time as urgent stabilization needs gave way to debates about long-term social policy and labor law. For clause-level committee reports and recorded floor debates, researchers should use congressional records and archival repositories.

Congress, committees, and emergency procedures

Emergency procedures and strong executive leadership affected the speed and shape of some early statutes, especially banking reforms in 1933. To trace the committee work and statutory amendments, consult the original session records and committee reports in the archives.

Public support, political conflict, and the role of the presidency

Public opinion, party control in Congress, and presidential initiatives all influenced which measures advanced and how they were framed. Avoid attributing motives without direct documentary evidence from the period.

Common errors and myths when writing about FDR's laws

Frequent mistakes include attributing later amendments to the original statute, using campaign slogans as factual descriptions, or asserting uniform effects without evidence. To avoid these errors, verify dates and statutory language in primary texts and note amendments separately.

For instance, a claim that Social Security immediately covered all workers confuses later expansions with the original 1935 scope. Always check the original statute and documented amendments when making coverage claims Social Security Administration historical page on the Social Security Act.

Attribution errors and anachronisms

Do not use modern program names or later policy frameworks to describe the law at enactment. Instead, use the statutory language and contemporaneous sources to describe what the law created at the time.

Overclaiming effects

Avoid asserting that any single law was solely responsible for broad economic outcomes. Use evidence from implementation records and later scholarship to support causal claims.
