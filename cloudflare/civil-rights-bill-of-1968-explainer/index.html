What the Civil Rights Act of 1968 covers: a concise definition and context

The Civil Rights Act of 1968, enacted April 11, 1968, includes the Fair Housing Act, which makes it unlawful to discriminate in the sale, rental, or financing of dwellings on several protected bases, and it remains the foundation of federal housing nondiscrimination law as of 2026 Congress.gov public law text.

The Fair Housing Act sets out core prohibitions that apply across transactions and brokerage activities. For a plain summary of the Act's current structure and enforcement approach consult the federal agency overview HUD overview of the Fair Housing Act.

The Fair Housing Act: core prohibitions explained

In practice the Fair Housing Act prohibits certain conduct related to the sale, rental, financing, and brokerage of dwellings, including refusing to rent or sell, offering different terms, and discriminatory advertising; HUD guidance and the statute describe these covered actions in practical terms HUD overview of the Fair Housing Act.

Examples that commonly appear in HUD materials and case summaries include refusing to rent to a qualified tenant because of a protected characteristic, listing different rental terms for different applicants, or steering buyers to particular neighborhoods. These examples illustrate how the statutory prohibitions apply to everyday housing transactions.

What conduct is unlawful under the Act

The Act identifies categories of conduct that can form the basis for a complaint, such as a failure to rent, a refusal to make a reasonable accommodation, or discriminatory lending terms. HUD guidance explains how these categories are applied in administrative reviews HUD overview of the Fair Housing Act.

Sale, rental, financing, and brokerage examples

Practical examples include a broker placing discriminatory restrictions in listings, a lender applying different lending standards on the basis of a protected characteristic, or a property owner imposing unequal rules for tenants. These illustrations mirror the types of complaints HUD and DOJ handle in administrative and judicial forums DOJ overview of the Fair Housing Act.

Who is protected under the Fair Housing Act: the listed classes

The Fair Housing Act lists protected characteristics: race, color, religion, sex, national origin, familial status, and disability. That list is reflected in the statute and in federal guidance U.S. Code statutory findings.

Familial status generally covers families with children and affects rules about who may occupy housing, while disability protections create obligations such as reasonable accommodations and, in some cases, accessibility requirements for design and construction; HUD materials explain these practical implications HUD overview of the Fair Housing Act.

The seven protected characteristics

The statutory list of protected characteristics is used in agency intake, investigations, and enforcement decisions. Agencies and courts rely on those categories when assessing whether a complaint falls within the Act's scope U.S. Code statutory findings.

How familial status and disability protections work in practice

Family status protections generally bar policies that exclude or treat families with children differently, and disability rules require reasonable accommodations and may require accessible features in certain new construction or alterations. HUD guidance describes how administrators and advocates frame such claims HUD overview of the Fair Housing Act.

How enforcement works: the roles of HUD and DOJ

Enforcement of the Fair Housing Act is shared mainly between HUD, which handles administrative complaints, investigations, and efforts at conciliation, and the Department of Justice, which brings litigation and civil enforcement actions when appropriate HUD overview of the Fair Housing Act.

HUD typically receives and processes intake, evaluates complaints under administrative rules, and attempts conciliation and administrative resolution before some matters move to litigation or referral; DOJ can sue to enforce the Act in federal court when administrative remedies are insufficient or when a civil enforcement action is warranted DOJ overview of the Fair Housing Act. HUD administrative complaints, investigations, and conciliation

When a person files a HUD complaint the agency follows administrative procedures that can include an investigation, attempts at conciliation between parties, and, in some cases, administrative hearings or referral to DOJ; HUD's intake procedures and timelines are described on its site HUD overview of the Fair Housing Act.

DOJ litigation and civil enforcement

The Justice Department enforces the Fair Housing Act through civil litigation when cases warrant federal court intervention. DOJ materials summarize the department's authority to bring suits and the types of civil enforcement actions it pursues DOJ overview of the Fair Housing Act.

Remedies and penalties available under the Act

The statute and implementing guidance identify remedies including injunctive relief, monetary damages, civil penalties, and attorney's fees, available through HUD administrative processes and federal court actions DOJ overview of the Fair Housing Act.

Administrative resolutions may emphasize remedies such as conciliation agreements and corrective measures, while federal courts can award injunctions, damages, and civil penalties under the statutory framework; HUD and DOJ descriptions set out how these remedies are typically pursued HUD overview of enforcement options.

Administrative resolutions versus court remedies

In many cases HUD seeks voluntary conciliation that can include negotiated changes and measures to address discrimination. If conciliation fails or the case presents broader public interest issues, DOJ or a court may pursue more formal remedies in litigation DOJ overview of the Fair Housing Act.

Types of relief a complainant can seek

Complainants may seek corrections to discriminatory practices, injunctive relief to stop ongoing discrimination, monetary compensation where permitted, and reimbursement of attorneys' fees. Specific availability and procedures depend on whether the matter proceeds administratively or to federal court HUD overview of the Fair Housing Act.

How courts have shaped the law: key decisions and limits

Jones v. Alfred H. Mayer Co. and private conduct

Jones held that Congress could reach certain private acts that discriminated on the basis of race, and that ruling is often cited in discussions about the Act's reach into private housing transactions. The decision remains a reference point when courts consider whether statutory prohibitions apply to particular private conduct Jones v. Alfred H. Mayer Co. decision.

After intake HUD may request additional information, open an investigation, and try conciliation. Possible administrative outcomes

Administrative outcomes range from no finding to negotiated agreements that change practices, to referrals for further enforcement. The specific remedy depends on case facts and procedural posture.

Common legal theories and claims under the Fair Housing Act

Enforcement and litigation often rely on two familiar legal theories: disparate treatment and disparate impact. Disparate treatment involves intentional differential treatment based on a protected characteristic, while disparate impact focuses on policies that are neutral on their face but have unjustified discriminatory effects HUD overview of enforcement theories.

Claims related to design and construction under disability rules arise when housing fails to meet required accessibility standards for covered properties or when reasonable accommodations are denied; agencies and courts apply statutory and regulatory guidance to evaluate such claims U.S. Code statutory findings.

Disparate treatment and disparate impact

Both theories have been used in administrative complaints and court cases. Enforcement agencies consider the factual record and applicable legal standards when deciding which theory to pursue in a given matter DOJ overview of the Fair Housing Act.

Design and construction claims under disability rules

Design and construction claims often center on whether new construction or alterations meet accessibility standards and whether reasonable accommodations were properly considered. HUD guidance outlines common approaches to these questions HUD guidance on disability and accessibility.

Algorithmic bias and housing technology

Automated systems can create patterns that may mirror discriminatory outcomes even when there is no explicit human intent. Evaluators look at whether such systems have unjustified disparate impacts on protected groups and whether adjustments or oversight can mitigate those effects.

Source-of-income protections and local rules

Source-of-income protections, such as rules about accepting housing vouchers, vary by jurisdiction.
