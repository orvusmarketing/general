December 15 1791: Why the Bill of Rights ratification matters

December 15 1791 is the date when the first ten amendments to the U.S. Constitution were officially ratified, a set of protections that came to be called the Bill of Rights. According to the National Archives transcription, those amendments were recorded and preserved as part of the founding documents of the Republic National Archives Bill of Rights transcription.

The Bill of Rights lists protections that limit federal power and protect individual liberties. These include freedoms of speech, religion, press, and assembly, along with procedural protections such as trial by jury and due process. What the Bill of Rights actually says: a section by section guide

The First Amendment protects religion, speech, the press, assembly, and petition. That short list forms a core part of American civic language and is the opening provision of the Bill of Rights, as shown in the archival transcription National Archives Bill of Rights transcription.

Amendments two through ten cover separate protections and procedures. They include the right to keep and bear arms, protections against quartering soldiers, protections against unreasonable searches and seizures, and guarantees of fair criminal process, such as the right to a speedy and public trial and protection against cruel and unusual punishment. Some amendments set procedural rules for criminal prosecutions, like indictment by grand jury, trial by jury, and the right to counsel and confrontation. Others are narrowly targeted to specific subjects and reflect the concerns of late 18th century legislators. How ratification happened: timeline up to December 15 1791

After the Constitution was adopted in 1788, several state ratifying conventions and legislatures urged the new Congress to add specific guarantees. In response, Congress proposed a set of amendments that were sent to the states for ratification during 1789 and 1790, and the ratification process continued into 1791 with state legislatures acting at different times. The National Archives records show the final ratification tally that produced the December 15 1791 date National Archives Bill of Rights transcription. The milestone narrative is also available from the National Archives Bill of Rights (1791).

States ratified at different speeds, and the required number of states completed their ratification steps by mid December 1791, which is why that date is used as the formal ratification date for the first ten amendments. The First Bank of the United States: charter and context in 1791

Congress passed the act to incorporate the Bank of the United States in 1791, creating a national banking institution with capital subscribed under congressional terms. The original act appears in the Statutes at Large and is recorded in the congressional enactment notice Congress.gov enactment.

The First Bank fit within Alexander Hamilton's plan to stabilize federal finances, manage debts, and create a credible national credit profile. The Library of Congress overview explains how the bank related to broader Hamiltonian fiscal measures and why Congress approved a charter in 1791 Library of Congress overview on Hamilton and the Bank.

The bank's creation gave the federal government tools to receive deposits and make limited loans, and it influenced debates about federal power and regional economies. Common mistakes and misconceptions when reading about 1791

A frequent mistake is to confuse proposal dates with ratification dates. The proposed amendments circulated earlier, but the formal ratification that completed the process is recorded as December 15 1791 in the archival transcription National Archives Bill of Rights transcription.

Another error is to treat 1791 measures as sole causes of later events.
