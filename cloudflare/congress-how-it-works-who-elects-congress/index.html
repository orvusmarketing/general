What it means to elect Congress: a short definition and national framework

Why the distinction between House and Senate matters

When people ask who elects Congress, they usually mean how voters choose members of the two chambers, the House of Representatives and the Senate. In the modern system, members of the House are chosen by direct popular vote in single-member districts, and since the 17th Amendment, Senators are chosen by direct popular vote statewide. For a primary source explanation of the amendment and its effect on senatorial elections, consult the National Archives overview of amendments National Archives' amendment listing.

The distinction matters because the two chambers operate on different geographic scales and different term lengths, which affects how elections are scheduled and how voters engage. House members represent districts and face voters every two years, while Senators are elected statewide for staggered six year terms. This structural difference shapes campaign calendars and voter attention.

Who votes and who can run: eligibility, registration, and state roles

Constitutional eligibility for voters and candidates

The Constitution and federal law set baseline eligibility for voters and candidates: voters must be U.S. citizens and meet state residency and age rules to register, while candidates for the House and Senate must meet the constitutional minimum ages and residency thresholds to hold office. For current guidance on registration and voter eligibility, consult the federal voter portal Vote.gov registration and eligibility.

Beyond those constitutional minimums, most practical questions are handled by state law. That means states define registration deadlines, identification requirements, and the detailed residency rules that determine how and where a person may vote or run for a seat.

How states handle registration, ID, and residency rules

State procedures vary: some states allow same-day registration, others require registration weeks before an election; some accept a range of ID documents, others expect a narrower list. Because administration is state-run, check your state election office for the exact rules that apply to you, including deadlines and acceptable forms of identification.

If you are planning to register or to verify your eligibility to run for office, use vote.gov as a starting point and then consult your secretary of state or local election office for the precise, current steps and deadlines in your jurisdiction.

How candidates reach the ballot: primaries, types, and state variation

Common primary types and what they mean for voters

States run different primary systems that determine how party nominees reach the general-election ballot. Common types include closed primaries, where only registered party members may vote in a party's primary; open primaries, where any registered voter may choose which party primary to enter; semi-closed systems that permit unaffiliated voters to participate while restricting party members; and top-two or nonpartisan blanket primaries that advance the top two vote-getters to the general election regardless of party. The National Conference of State Legislatures summarizes these primary types and how they work in different states NCSL overview of primary types, and state primary dates for 2026 are listed by the NCSL 2026 state primary election dates.

Each primary type changes the strategic path for candidates and the choice set for voters. In a closed primary, for example, independent voters may not weigh in on that party's choice unless they change registration, while in a top-two system voters may see two candidates of the same party on the general-election ballot. For local examples of how primaries affect candidate lists, see coverage of Florida's 25th primary FL-25 primary effects.

Members of the House are elected by direct vote within single-member districts and Senators are elected statewide by direct popular vote since the 17th Amendment; states run primaries, registration, and certification so voters should confirm deadlines and procedures with state election offices and national portals.

How nomination rules change the path to the general election

Because states set nomination rules, the route to the general ballot differs by state and by cycle. Some states require party conventions or signature thresholds in addition to primary votes; others rely only on primary results or petition filings. These differences mean a candidate who reaches the ballot in one state might face a different set of requirements in another.

Before a primary, confirm your state’s system and any filing deadlines with the relevant state election office or with nonpartisan trackers that summarize state rules. This reduces confusion about who appears on the ballot and why particular names are listed for the general election.

Timing and the election calendar: general election date, terms, and cycles

When federal elections are held and why the date is uniform

Federal general elections are held on a uniform date set by statute: the Tuesday after the first Monday in November. This date applies to House races and to regularly scheduled Senate contests in their election years. For the statutory framework and administration guidance, see resources from the U.S. Election Assistance Commission and related federal guidance EAC resources on federal dates and administration. For a plain-language overview of when the 2026 midterms will occur and their purpose, see the U.S. Vote Foundation guide When are the 2026 midterm elections.

Having a uniform date helps coordinate federal, state, and local schedules, but the campaigning and nomination cycles that lead to that date vary by state and party rules. Voters should check calendars early so they do not miss registration, absentee, or early voting deadlines.

How congressional terms and the opening of new Congresses are set

Terms for members of Congress and the start dates of new Congresses are settled by federal law and standing rules. House members serve two-year terms and Senators serve staggered six-year terms; the legal framework determines when those terms begin and when successor members take office. These schedules matter for understanding when newly elected members assume duties and when vacancies must be filled.

As a simple example, regular general elections in November lead to new terms that begin on dates set by statute, so winning a November contest does not always mean immediate seating; state certification processes and federal start dates both matter for the timing of service.

Vacancies and replacements: special elections and temporary appointments

How House vacancies are usually filled

When a House seat becomes vacant, states generally require a special election to fill the seat, though the timing and procedures are set by state law. Consult state election offices or summaries of state statutes for the exact process that would apply in a particular vacancy. Ballotpedia provides an overview of vacancy rules and how states handle special elections Ballotpedia overview of congressional vacancies.

Special elections can change the typical cycle for a district, and they usually follow accelerated deadlines for filing and ballots. Voters and potential candidates should check state guidance if a vacancy arises in their district.

How Senate vacancies may be handled differently

By contrast, Senate vacancies may be filled by gubernatorial appointment or by special election, depending on state statutes and the 17th Amendment framework. The combination of state law and the constitutional amendment means there is no single nationwide rule for temporary Senate appointments, and practices continue to vary through 2026. For an explanation of how the 17th Amendment shapes these options, see the U.S. Senate resource on election procedures U.S. Senate explanation of senate elections.

If you want to know how a specific state handles a Senate vacancy, consult that state's law and the relevant election office, or use national summaries that list state procedures for vacancies and appointments.

Where to find official results and candidate information

State certification and official listings

Certified results and official candidate listings are published by state election authorities; those state sites are the authoritative source for who won and when certificates are issued. For federal resources that guide administration, see the EAC and related federal pages that point to state certification procedures EAC resources on administration and certification.

Because certification is a state action, national aggregators summarize certified outcomes but the ultimate record is the state certificate. When accuracy matters, use the state site that issued the certificate as the primary reference.

Federal and nonpartisan aggregators that summarize filings and results

National aggregators such as the Federal Election Commission and Ballotpedia collect filings, candidate lists, and summaries that make it easier to track races across states and cycles.
