Why ask whether the Petition of Right is still relevant?

The Petition of Right was a 1628 parliamentary petition protesting King Charles I's breaches of law, including forced loans, arbitrary imprisonment, billeting of soldiers, and the use of martial law, as shown in primary transcriptions and institutional descriptions British Library and on a Parliamentary collection page Parliament.

Readers ask about relevance because the petition of right is often invoked today in debates about executive power and parliamentary consent, where its use may be symbolic or instructional rather than a claim to direct legal enforcement Encyclopaedia Britannica.

Yes, as a historical precedent and teaching tool about limits on executive power, but it rarely provides direct enforceable rights in modern courts without supporting statute or case law.

What the Petition said in 1628: the four core complaints

The Petition sets out four core complaints. First, Parliament protested forced loans and other methods of raising revenue without parliamentary consent; the text states these practices as breaches of law Avalon Project, Yale.

Second, the Petition challenges arbitrary imprisonment and stresses the need for lawful process that would later be associated with habeas corpus protections in constitutional history Encyclopaedia Britannica.

Third, it objects to the billeting of soldiers in private homes and the use of martial law powers seen as inconsistent with ordinary law; the primary text describes these complaints plainly British Library.

Fourth, the Petition protests other forms of royal overreach such as forced loans and impositions that bypassed Parliamentary authority, and Parliament drafted the document to record and resist those breaches of law Avalon Project, Yale.

Symbolic use versus enforceable law: how modern institutions treat the Petition

Parliamentary briefings and academic commentaries commonly treat the Petition as influential in political-constitutional discourse and as a teaching tool about limits on executive power House of Commons Library.

At the same time, modern courts and statutes usually rely on later legislation and case law, so direct enforcement derived solely from the Petition is rare and depends on supporting statutory or judicial authority Cambridge Historical Journal.
