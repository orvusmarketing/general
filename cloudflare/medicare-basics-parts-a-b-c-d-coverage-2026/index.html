What Medicare is and how it is organized

Brief definition and federal role

Medicare basics describe the federal health insurance program for people 65 and older and for certain younger people with disabilities. The program sets baseline covered services under federal rules and guidance, which are published by CMS and Medicare.gov Medicare.gov overview of Part A and Part B.

Medicare basics overview

Administratively, Medicare is managed through federal programs with enrollment and benefit rules coordinated between CMS and the Social Security Administration. For enrollment and benefit questions, Social Security handles sign-up while CMS publishes benefit definitions.

The program is commonly discussed in four parts: Part A for hospital coverage, Part B for outpatient and physician services, Part C for Medicare Advantage plans run by private insurers, and Part D for prescription drug coverage. These parts together form the baseline for coverage decisions under federal rules.

Readers should treat CMS and Medicare.gov as primary sources for current coverage lists and limits because plan details and allowed services are defined at the federal level and implemented by CMS. You can also review related materials on our Affordable Healthcare hub.

Breakdown of Parts A, B, C and D: what each covers

Part A: hospital insurance-what it covers and typical cost features

Part A covers inpatient hospital stays, skilled nursing facility care after a qualifying hospital stay, some home health care, and hospice care. This component of Original Medicare establishes core inpatient and facility benefits under federal rules Medicare.gov overview of Part A and Part B.

For many people who have paid Medicare payroll taxes for at least 10 years, Part A is often premium-free. Other cost items such as deductibles and coinsurance still apply and vary by service type.

Part B: medical insurance-services and enrollment basics

Part B covers medically necessary outpatient services, visits to physicians, outpatient therapy, preventive services and certain durable medical equipment. The program sets which services are covered and how Medicare pays providers under federal guidelines Medicare.gov overview of Part A and Part B.

Part B normally requires a monthly premium, which can vary with income and be adjusted annually. Enrollment timing and potential late-enrollment penalties are handled through Social Security enrollment rules.

Part C: Medicare Advantage-how private plans package benefits

Part C, often called Medicare Advantage, refers to private insurance plans that must cover at least the services in Parts A and B. These plans frequently include extra benefits and many offer drug coverage bundled in the plan design CMS Medicare Advantage overview.

Medicare Advantage plans vary by county and by insurer. They use provider networks, formularies and plan-level cost sharing that differ from Original Medicare. Enrollment in Medicare Advantage plans rose substantially through 2024 and 2025, and by 2025 more than half of beneficiaries were enrolled in these plans according to enrollment data and issue briefs.

Part D: prescription drug coverage and formularies

Part D offers prescription drug coverage through private plans that are approved by CMS. Each plan publishes a formulary that lists covered drugs, tiers and utilization rules, and these details can change annually Medicare.gov Part D prescription drug coverage.

Part D plans set monthly premiums and cost-sharing rules that differ by plan and by income. When selecting a plan, reviewing the plan's formulary for specific medications is essential to understand expected out-of-pocket costs.

Who is eligible and how enrollment windows work

Age and residency eligibility rules

Eligibility generally begins at age 65 for U.S. citizens and certain lawful residents who meet residency requirements. People under 65 may qualify through specific disability or medical criteria.

Exact eligibility details and exceptions are administered by federal agencies; readers should consult Social Security and Medicare resources to confirm personal eligibility and pathways.

Initial, General and Special Enrollment Periods

Medicare has defined enrollment windows. The Initial Enrollment Period typically surrounds a new enrollee's 65th birthday, the General Enrollment Period covers later sign-up opportunities, and Special Enrollment Periods may apply for life events such as employer coverage changes. These periods affect when coverage begins and whether penalties apply SSA guidance on when and how to sign up.

Missing the appropriate window can lead to delayed coverage or late-enrollment penalties. For timing tied to work-based coverage and other exceptions, plan documents and SSA guidance explain the specifics.

How Medicare costs work: premiums, deductibles and out-of-pocket exposure

Typical premium structures for Parts A, B and D

Part A is often premium-free for people with sufficient Medicare payroll tax history, while Part B and Part D require monthly premiums that vary by income and may be adjusted annually Medicare.gov overview of Part A and Part B.

Part D premiums depend on the chosen drug plan and can include income-related adjustments. Exact premium amounts for 2026 must be checked on plan pages and Medicare.gov plan listings for each county.

How Medicare Advantage plan costs may differ

Medicare Advantage plan premiums, deductibles and out-of-pocket maximums vary significantly by plan and county. Some Medicare Advantage plans have low or zero premiums but use narrower provider networks and cost-sharing rules that can affect out-of-pocket exposure CMS Medicare Advantage overview.

When comparing plans, evaluate total expected spending: premiums plus likely cost-sharing for services you expect to use. For many people, the combination of premium and out-of-pocket limits determines the best option.

Income-related adjustments and late-enrollment penalties

Some Medicare premiums are adjusted for higher-income enrollees. In addition, late-enrollment penalties can apply for Parts B and D if someone delays enrollment without qualifying for a Special Enrollment Period. Check SSA and Medicare resources for how those rules apply to individual circumstances SSA guidance on Medicare enrollment.

Because amounts and rules change, verify current premium figures and penalty calculations on official plan documents and government pages before deciding.

Comparing Original Medicare plus Medigap and Part D versus Medicare Advantage

Provider access and network differences

Original Medicare plus a Medigap policy and a standalone Part D plan generally allows broader access to providers who accept Medicare. Medigap policies are designed to fill cost-sharing gaps under Original Medicare and are portable in most states.

Medicare Advantage plans typically use networks that can limit provider choice and require referrals in some plan types. For people who prioritize keeping a specific provider, Original Medicare with Medigap can be easier to use across providers and states CMS Medicare Advantage overview.

Coverage of extra benefits and drug coverage bundling

Many Medicare Advantage plans include extra benefits not in Original Medicare, such as vision, hearing or fitness benefits, and often bundle Part D drug coverage into a single plan option. These extras vary by plan and county and are subject to CMS rules.

Original Medicare requires separate Part D coverage for prescriptions and optional Medigap for supplemental cost-sharing protection, which can be a simpler model for those who want predictable provider access.

How premiums and out-of-pocket risks typically compare

Total cost exposure depends on plan premiums, expected health care use and tolerance for network limits. Medicare Advantage plans may offer lower premiums but higher variable cost-sharing, while Original Medicare combined with Medigap can provide more predictable out-of-pocket limits in some cases KFF issue brief on Medicare Advantage enrollment trends.

Because plan designs differ by county, comparing county-level plan summaries and the Summary of Benefits documents is essential before enrolling.

How to choose: a practical checklist and decision framework

Step-by-step questions to assess needs

Start by listing your priorities: preferred providers, regular prescriptions, expected health care use, monthly premium tolerance, and interest in extra benefits. Use these priorities to filter plan options.

Ask concrete questions: Do you need nationwide provider access? How many and which drugs do you take regularly? Are you comfortable using a network of clinicians? The answers guide whether Original Medicare or Medicare Advantage or a hybrid approach better fits your needs.

How to check plan details in your county

Use the Medicare.gov plan finder and CMS county plan pages to see 2026 plan listings for your area, including Part D formularies and Summary of Benefits documents that list costs and covered services Medicare.gov sign up and plan change resources or use the Medicare plan compare tool at Medicare.gov plan compare.

Compare at least three plans side by side and read the Part D formulary for each drug you take to spot tier placement and prior authorization rules. Check provider directories to confirm network participation.

When to consult a licensed counselor or SHIP

For personalized assistance, contact your State Health Insurance Assistance Program (SHIP) or a licensed agent. SHIP counselors provide free, local help and can explain plan details and enrollment forms. Learn more about our approach on the About page.

When in doubt about plan rules or the timing of enrollment, a counselor or the SSA enrollment line can clarify how different choices affect premium and penalty calculations.

Common mistakes and pitfalls to avoid when signing up

One frequent error is missing an enrollment window and facing a late-enrollment penalty. Confirm your Initial, General or Special Enrollment Period before you delay enrollment SSA guidance on Medicare enrollment.

Original Medicare covers Part A and Part B benefits with optional Medigap and Part D for added protections and typically broader provider access. Medicare Advantage is offered by private insurers, must cover Parts A and B, and often bundles extra benefits and drug coverage but can use networks that limit provider choice.

Another common pitfall is assuming all drugs or all providers are covered; formulary restrictions and provider networks can cause surprise bills or denied claims. Always verify coverage for critical drugs and clinicians.

Plan-level rules such as prior authorization, step therapy and utilization management can affect access to certain services and drugs. Check the plan's utilization policies and appeals processes before you enroll Medicare.gov Part D prescription drug coverage.

Practical examples and scenarios

Scenario A: you have many regular prescriptions

If you take multiple daily medications, review Part D formularies for each plan and check tier placements and copay structures. Some plans place expensive drugs on higher tiers or require prior authorization, which affects overall cost Medicare.gov Part D prescription drug coverage.

For prescription-heavy situations, a plan with a favorable formulary for your drugs can reduce total spending even if the monthly premium is higher.

Scenario B: you travel frequently or split time between states

If you spend significant time in multiple states, Original Medicare can offer broader provider access across state lines, while Medicare Advantage networks are usually limited to a plan's service area and can restrict out-of-area care CMS Medicare Advantage overview.

Travelers should confirm network portability and emergency care rules for any Medicare Advantage plan under consideration.

Scenario C: you prefer a specific specialist or hospital

When a particular specialist or hospital is critical to your care, check whether that provider accepts Original Medicare or participates in the Medicare Advantage plan's network. Medigap portability can make it easier to keep the same provider across changes in location.

Confirm network listings and hospital participation before choosing a plan to avoid unexpected provider changes.
