How to check recalls and technical service bulletins (TSBs) before you buy

Start by asking the seller for the VIN and then check NHTSA recalls and investigation pages for that VIN. NHTSA maintains recall and complaint databases that you can search for open recalls and for investigations that may apply to a vehicle, and this VIN-level check is the primary authoritative step recommended for any used purchase NHTSA recalls and investigations for Chevrolet Colorado. If you prefer a quick VIN lookup, you can also use our VIN decoder tool VIN decoder for an initial check.

Understand the difference between a safety recall and a technical service bulletin. A safety recall is an action required because the manufacturer or regulator has found a safety defect. A TSB is a manufacturer-issued guidance for dealers and technicians that addresses a known issue, often including diagnostic steps or recommended repairs, but it is not the same as a safety recall. Both matter because a TSB can signal a recurring field issue, and a recall may mean the manufacturer has identified a safety defect.

Request that the seller show proof of any claimed recall repairs or TSB-related service work, with dates and VIN-matched service records. If the seller cannot provide documentation, confirm the claimed work via the VIN-level NHTSA pages and the vehicle history report before proceeding to a test drive.

How to run a focused test drive and inspection for transmission issues

Warm the vehicle to normal operating temperature before doing serious shifting tests. Start with city driving, then move to a highway segment, and finally test acceleration under load. Pay attention to how the gearbox shifts between gears, whether shifts are quick and smooth, and whether there is any hesitation or slipping when accelerating hard. If you notice rough shifts or delays, stop and document the behavior for your mechanic RepairPal notes on repair and symptom patterns.

During inspection, ask the seller if transmission fluid was checked recently and look for the smell and color of the fluid if the dipstick is available. Old or burnt-smelling fluid can indicate past overheating or neglect. Also look for signs of recent transmission work such as new transmission mounts, aftermarket parts, or fresh pan gasket sealant, and compare any invoices to the VIN and service dates.

If the test drive produces any concerning behavior, postpone the purchase until a transmission-aware mechanic inspects the vehicle. Do not accept a verbal assurance from the seller that the problem is fixed without written, VIN-matched service documentation.
