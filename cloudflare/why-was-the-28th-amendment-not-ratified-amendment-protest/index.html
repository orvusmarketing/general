Quick answer: why a proposed 28th Amendment was not ratified and how an amendment protest can matter

Bottom-line summary

A proposed amendment often fails because Article V sets a high procedural bar that is hard to clear. A proposal must first pass two-thirds of both congressional chambers and then be ratified by three-fourths of the states, a sequence that routinely prevents amendments from being adopted National Archives.

In modern amendment efforts, Congress frequently attaches time limits to the ratification period and disputes arise over whether states may rescind earlier ratifications; those issues have stopped campaigns even when public attention grew Constitution Annotated.

Court precedent also matters. The Supreme Court in Coleman v. Miller treated contested ratifications primarily as political questions, which leaves many disputes unresolved by the judiciary and can end amendment drives when political actors decline to act Coleman v. Miller opinion.

How the constitutional amendment process works under Article V

Two paths: congressional proposal and state conventions

Article V establishes the basic mechanics: an amendment must be proposed either by two-thirds of both the House and the Senate, or by a convention called by two-thirds of state legislatures, and then ratified by three-fourths of the states in one of two ways. The requirement for supermajorities at both stages makes the process deliberately difficult, and that difficulty is the frequent practical barrier to adoption National Archives.

Most modern proposals use the congressional proposal route, which begins in Congress and then goes to state legislatures or to state conventions for ratification. The Constitution Annotated provides descriptions of how states have recorded and transmitted ratifications historically, and why the choice of ratification route matters for strategy Constitution Annotated.

Counting ratifications and the three-fourths rule

Once Congress selects the ratification route, the count of ratified states proceeds against the total number required, currently three-fourths of 50 states. That count is not merely arithmetic in practice; questions can arise over whether a state’s action is a proper ratification, whether it followed the required state procedure, and whether rescissions affect the tally Constitution Annotated.

Because the required threshold is high, small procedural disputes in a few states can change the outcome of an entire campaign. That sensitivity makes clear procedural clarity and coordinated state-level strategy vital to any amendment drive National Archives.

Procedural thresholds, ratification deadlines, rescissions and the anatomy of an amendment protest

What ratification deadlines do and why Congress uses them

Congress often attaches a time limit to the period allowed for state ratification, usually measured in years from submission. This compresses the timeline for state action and is intended to ensure a contemporaneous political consensus, but it also creates a hard stop that can invalidate later state ratifications if Congress enforces the deadline Constitution Annotated.

When a deadline is in place, sponsors must accelerate state campaigns or prioritize certain states, which raises the cost and complexity of coordination across jurisdictions and makes the national effort more vulnerable to short-term political shifts Congressional Research Service report on deadlines and rescissions.

Disputed rescissions and how protests arise

Another procedural flashpoint is the question of rescission, where a state attempts to withdraw a prior ratification. The legal status of rescissions is disputed, and disagreements over whether a rescission should count can produce formal protests, competing tallies, and litigation that stall national progress Congressional Research Service report on deadlines and rescissions.

An amendment protest can take different forms. State legislators, advocacy groups, or officials may file formal objections or demand congressional review of whether a state’s action met procedural requirements. Those protests can add political friction and legal uncertainty, lowering the chance that the three-fourths threshold is ever certified Constitution Annotated.

Coleman v. Miller (1939) and judicial uncertainty about amendment disputes

What the Supreme Court held in Coleman v. Miller

The Supreme Court in Coleman v. Miller concluded that disputes over state ratification and related procedural questions present political questions for the legislative and executive branches, rather than issues the judiciary must resolve in most cases. That posture leaves many legal questions unresolved and defers to political actors to settle disputes Coleman v. Miller opinion. See a concise summary at EBSCO Coleman v. Miller | EBSCO.

In practice, Coleman means courts may decline to adjudicate contested ratification counts, leaving Congress to decide whether to recognize late ratifications or rescissions. That uncertainty can end amendment drives when political leaders choose not to act or when the procedural path is politically fraught Coleman v. Miller opinion.

Why the decision makes courts defer to political branches

The practical effect of Coleman is to concentrate resolution power in Congress and, indirectly, in state governments. Because courts often see these disputes as nonjusticiable, legal remedies are limited and sponsors must rely on political negotiation or congressional rulings to settle contested tallies Constitution Annotated.

That judicial posture increases the importance of political strategy and increases the role of protests and objections, since a court is less likely to provide a definitive legal clearing of disputed ratifications Coleman v. Miller opinion.

Partisan polarization and shifting majorities: political reasons amendments fail

Why bipartisan coalitions have mattered historically

Historically, successful amendments have required broad coalitions across parties and regions. When an issue attracted support from lawmakers of both parties, it had a better chance of clearing the two-thirds and three-fourths hurdles, because coalition building reduced the risk that swings in a few jurisdictions would derail the effort Brookings Institution analysis.

A mix of factors explains the failure: Article V supermajority requirements, congressional deadlines and rescission disputes, judicial deference under Coleman v. Miller, partisan polarization, and inconsistent state procedures combined to prevent reaching the three-fourths threshold.

How modern polarization raises the bar

Today, partisan polarization and more frequent shifts in control of state legislatures make those broad coalitions harder to assemble and maintain. Research shows that divided partisan environments reduce the likelihood that an amendment will secure the long, cross-jurisdictional momentum needed for ratification Brookings Institution analysis.

Shifting majorities also mean that a state that ratified under one partisan coalition may change its position after elections, creating further uncertainty about whether previously recorded ratifications will stand without legal clarity on rescissions Pew Research Center analysis.

State-level practical obstacles: differing procedures, language ambiguity and weak mobilization

Variations in state ratification rules

State legislatures and secretaries of state use different procedures and formats for recording ratification. Some states require specific journal entries, others use simple legislative resolutions, and still others have procedural steps that can delay or complicate formal acceptance. These variations can create gaps for national campaigns trying to verify official counts Constitution Annotated.

For sponsors, that means a national strategy must adapt to many different procedural calendars and rules. Failure to do so can leave some states’ actions vulnerable to challenge or to administrative delay that affects the overall tally Congressional Research Service report on deadlines and rescissions.

How wording and ballot language can cause stalls

Unclear amendment text or inconsistent presentation to state legislators or conventions can produce confusion about what is being ratified. Ambiguity may invite legal challenges or procedural objections, slowing or stopping state-level adoption even when public interest exists Constitution Annotated.

Local advocacy and coordinated grassroots work matter because shortfalls in mobilization at the state level often explain why a national campaign cannot convert attention into the formal votes needed for three-fourths support Congressional Research Service report on deadlines and rescissions.

How time limits and rescissions change campaign strategy and timelines

Tactical responses to deadlines

When Congress attaches a deadline, sponsors often compress their state-by-state efforts, focusing on a sequence that prioritizes likely yes votes and swing jurisdictions. That tactical compression raises coordination costs and concentrates risk on a narrower timeframe Constitution Annotated.

Sponsors may also pursue parallel strategies, such as seeking a new congressional resolution to remove or extend a deadline or asking supportive state officials to expedite procedural steps. Those options are politically costly and uncertain, and they do not eliminate legal disputes about rescission or late ratifications Congressional Research Service report on deadlines and rescissions.

Legal avenues and their limits

Legal strategies include litigation to compel recognition of state ratifications or to dispute rescissions, but Coleman v. Miller limits the courts role by designating many of these matters as political questions. That judicial posture reduces the likelihood that litigation will provide clear remedies in time to affect an ongoing campaign Coleman v. Miller opinion.

Because courts may defer, many resolution paths remain political: congressional rulings, state legislative clarifications, or negotiated settlements among stakeholders. Those paths require sustained political consensus, which is often the scarce resource in contentious amendment drives Brookings Institution analysis.

Measuring public support: polls, opinion and their limits for ratification campaigns

Public opinion vs legislative action

Opinion polls can show broad public interest, but that interest does not substitute for the constitutional thresholds set in Article V. Legislators weigh many factors beyond polls, including institutional rules, party strategy, and legal risk when deciding whether to support ratification Pew Research Center analysis.

Polling can guide advocacy by identifying sympathetic states or demographic groups, but converting favorable poll numbers into three-fourths of state legislatures requires sustained organization and repeated legislative efforts that polls alone do not achieve Brookings Institution analysis.

How polls inform but do not determine outcomes

Polls may help set expectations and prioritize resources, yet analysts caution against equating high poll support with likely ratification. Institutional rules and procedural thresholds remain the decisive factors for successful constitutional change Constitution Annotated.

For readers, the practical takeaway is that public sentiment is necessary but not sufficient; the legal and procedural framework governs the final outcome National Archives.

How scholars and lawmakers have proposed reducing uncertainty

Possible congressional actions

Scholars and some lawmakers have proposed clarifying whether Congress can set and then waive or extend deadlines, or whether it may accept late ratifications in cases of clear state assent. These proposals aim to reduce uncertainty about time limits, but they face legal questions and political resistance Constitution Annotated.

Another idea is for Congress to set clearer administrative procedures for counting and certifying ratifications to reduce disputes over form and timing. However, implementing such standards would require political consensus that is not assured in a polarized environment Congressional Research Service report on deadlines and rescissions.

Scholarly proposals and their limits

Legal scholars also suggest rules to address rescissions, including legislative acknowledgments of historic ratifications or statutory frameworks for resolving state disputes. These ideas clarify options but do not eliminate Coleman v. Miller’s effect, which can keep courts from settling contested claims Coleman v. Miller opinion.

Overall, proposed reforms can reduce some uncertainty, yet political actors must still be willing to apply them for any change to take effect Brookings Institution analysis.

Conclusion: why proposed amendments like a 28th face an uphill path and what remains uncertain

In short, several interlocking barriers explain why a proposed amendment may not be ratified: Article V supermajority requirements, congressional deadlines and rescission disputes, Coleman v. Miller’s judicial deference, partisan polarization, and state-level procedural problems. These factors commonly interact to halt campaigns National Archives.

Open questions remain about how Congress will treat deadlines and whether courts will intervene in future rescission disputes. For now, uncertainty is often decisive, because political and procedural obstacles together make ratification difficult even for proposals with significant public attention Congressional Research Service report on deadlines and rescissions.

An amendment protest is an objection or formal dispute about whether a state’s action or procedural step in ratifying a constitutional amendment met legal or procedural requirements.

Congress can attempt to waive or extend a deadline by passing a new resolution, but such actions raise legal and political questions and may be contested.

Courts have sometimes declined to resolve ratification disputes, treating them as political questions and leaving resolution to Congress or state authorities.

Understanding why a proposed amendment fails requires looking beyond headlines to the procedural rules and political context that determine outcomes. For readers following a current amendment drive, primary records and neutral CRS analysis are the most reliable way to track whether a proposal is likely to be recognized. The legal and political questions around deadlines and rescissions remain open, and future congressional or judicial choices will shape how amendment protests are resolved.
