What the Florida election calendar is and where to find the official schedule

Statewide calendar versus county calendars, Florida election calendar

The Florida election calendar is the official schedule that lists statewide dates for primaries, the general election, vote-by-mail windows and certification milestones; it is published and maintained by the Florida Division of Elections, Office of the Secretary of State Election Dates and Activities Calendar.

The statewide calendar sets the baseline timeline used across counties, but county supervisors translate those statewide dates into local filing windows, ballot schedules and polling place details. Check your county supervisor page for the localized calendar that applies where you vote.

The calendar matters because Florida statutes define qualifying periods, voter-registration cutoffs and canvassing procedures; those statutory deadlines are the legal baseline when interpreting the published schedule Chapter 101, Florida Statutes.

Different users rely on the calendar for different reasons: voters check registration and ballot deadlines, candidates use qualifying and filing dates, and election officials use the schedule for operational planning and staffing.

Missing a statutory or calendar deadline can have practical consequences, including disqualification of filings, ineligibility for a ballot placement or missed opportunities to vote by mail; when in doubt, consult the statute text and the state calendar for the controlling rule.

Key fields on the Florida election calendar explained

Qualifying period and candidate filing dates

"Qualifying period" on the calendar indicates the statutory window a prospective candidate can file paperwork and fees to appear on a ballot; the Candidate and Campaign Treasurer Handbook explains how calendar labels map to filing actions and sample timelines for candidates Candidate and Campaign Treasurer Handbook.

Use the handbook and the state calendar together to confirm which documents are due, where to deliver filings and what signatures or fees are required. If a county page lists specific office hours for filings, treat that local information as the operational detail to follow.

Use a three-source check: start with the Division of Elections statewide calendar, confirm the county supervisor calendar for local details, and consult Chapter 101 or the Candidate and Campaign Treasurer Handbook for statutory definitions or ambiguous fields.

"Vote-by-mail windows" typically appear as a calendar field that shows when ballots will be sent and the range of dates when counties begin processing outgoing ballots; the statewide calendar lists these windows, and county pages provide mailing practices and local pickup or return options Election Dates and Activities Calendar.

When you read a calendar field, convert the phrase shown into an action: note the start date to request a ballot or file, and the final date that a county marks as the last acceptable receipt or certification step.

How Chapter 101 and official handbooks affect calendar interpretation

Statute text in Chapter 101 provides definitions and legal deadlines that can change how you read a calendar entry, for example by clarifying whether a date is "received by" versus "postmarked by" or by specifying when canvassing begins Chapter 101, Florida Statutes.

Calendar entries for vote-by-mail show when ballots are first sent and the window during which counties transmit and accept ballots; the statewide calendar lists those windows and the handbook gives examples of how to interpret them for timing and proof of transmission Election Dates and Activities Calendar.

UOCAVA transmission windows are the calendar entries that matter most for military and overseas voters; these entries indicate when counties will transmit ballots and what deadlines apply for return or electronic transmission in some cases.

If you are an overseas or military voter, confirm your county's UOCAVA processing dates and procedures early so you can request and receive a ballot with enough time to return it by the deadline.

Certification, canvassing and when results become official

Canvassing and certification entries show when counties and the state will review and finalize vote totals; those milestones turn provisional and absentee counts into the official tally and are listed on the state calendar with statutory anchors for timing Election Dates and Activities Calendar.

"Canvassing" refers to the process by which election officials examine ballots, resolve provisional ballots and count valid votes. "Certification" is the formal act that makes results final under state procedures and timelines set by statute.

When you see a certification date on the calendar, understand that it is the administrative date for finalizing results; if a legal challenge or recount is triggered, related steps follow statutory processes outlined in Chapter 101 and other applicable law.

Common mistakes people make when using the calendar

A frequent error is assuming county and state calendars are identical; in practice the statewide calendar sets the framework while county calendars supply operational details, so always confirm the county translation of a statewide entry Miami-Dade County Elections calendar.

Another common confusion is mistaking a mailing date for a received-by deadline. Read calendar labels carefully: "mailed by" does not equal "received by," and statutes or the handbook can clarify which standard applies for a specific deadline.

Finally, do not rely on secondary websites for final dates. Official state and county pages publish last-minute changes, including emergency postponements and UOCAVA adjustments that may not appear on third-party aggregators.

Emergency changes, postponements and where to check for last-minute updates

Emergency postponements and last-minute changes are published by the Division of Elections and by county supervisors; these updates are the authoritative source if a date shifts due to weather, a public-safety incident or an administrative necessity Election Dates and Activities Calendar.

If you suspect a calendar entry has changed shortly before a deadline, verify immediately on the state calendar and on your county supervisor page, and use saved official URLs and county alert subscriptions for the fastest confirmation.

Practical scenarios: three timelines readers can follow

Scenario A: a local voter planning to vote by mail

Step 1, check the statewide vote-by-mail window to see when ballots will first be mailed. Then open your county calendar to find the page that shows how the county mails and accepts ballots, and note any local return options or deadlines Election Dates and Activities Calendar. You can also review general state election timing at the US Vote Foundation.

Step 2, request a vote-by-mail ballot early using the county's official request form or office contact. Step 3, follow county instructions for returning the ballot and confirm whether the calendar entry requires a received-by date, postmark date or electronic transmission for UOCAVA cases.

Scenario B: a candidate preparing qualifying paperwork

Step 1, consult the statewide calendar for the qualifying period and the Candidate and Campaign Treasurer Handbook for required forms and examples. Step 2, check the county calendar where the filing office is located for local office hours and submission instructions Candidate and Campaign Treasurer Handbook.

Step 3, deliver filings in person or by the method allowed and save confirmation receipts. Step 4, track campaign finance reporting deadlines using the state handbook and FEC resources for federal reporting interactions.

Scenario C: an overseas voter relying on UOCAVA

Step 1, identify the UOCAVA transmission window on the state calendar and then confirm your county's exact transmission process. Step 2, request your ballot immediately and allow extra time for international transit or electronic options where available Broward County election calendar.

Step 3, follow county guidance for returning the ballot and obtain any tracking confirmations. When needed, contact the county supervisor well before the last acceptable date to confirm receipt procedures and alternate transmission options.
